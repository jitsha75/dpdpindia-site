Supreme Court of India

Landmark Supreme Court Decisions

Foundational judgements that shaped India's data protection framework — many forming the constitutional basis for the DPDP Act 2023 itself.

Supreme Court · 9-Judge Bench · 2017
Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India
Whether the right to privacy is a fundamental right under the Indian Constitution
Outcome
Unanimous 9-0: Privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21 (Right to Life). All earlier contrary judgements overruled. Privacy includes informational privacy — the right to control one's personal data.
The constitutional bedrock of the DPDP Act 2023. Every data protection right in the Act flows from this judgement.
Supreme Court · 5-Judge Bench · 26 September 2018 · (2019) 1 SCC 1
Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India — Aadhaar Case
Constitutionality of the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act, 2016 and biometric data collection by the state
Outcome
4:1 majority upheld core Aadhaar Act as constitutional. Struck down Section 57 (private entities mandating Aadhaar for services) and Section 33(2) (disclosure to police without court order) as unconstitutional. Biometric data classified as sensitive personal information.
Private sector cannot mandate Aadhaar for services — this holding directly shapes DPDP Act's prohibition on coercive consent. Sets constitutional limits on biometric data use by state and private actors.
Supreme Court · 24 March 2015 · AIR 2015 SC 1523
Shreya Singhal v. Union of India
Constitutionality of Section 66A of the IT Act 2000 — restrictions on online speech
Outcome
Section 66A struck down in entirety as unconstitutional — vague, overbroad, violates Article 19(1)(a) free speech guarantee. Section 79 (intermediary safe harbour) read down — intermediaries must remove content only on court or government orders, not private complaints.
Foundational case defining limits of state power over online speech. The Section 79 reading directly governs intermediary liability under the IT Act's safe harbour framework.

High Court Decisions

Delhi High Court · W.P.(C) 7663/2016 · August 2016
Karmanya Singh Sareen & Anr. v. Union of India & Ors. — WhatsApp Privacy Policy
Whether WhatsApp's September 2016 privacy policy update — permitting data sharing with Facebook — violated Indian users' right to privacy and informational self-determination
Outcome
Delhi HC directed WhatsApp to delete all data of Indian users who had not accepted the revised 2016 policy by 25 September 2016. The Division Bench held that users who did not opt in cannot have their data shared with Facebook entities. MeitY was directed to frame a data protection policy for India — an early impetus for what became the DPDP Act 2023.
Pre-existing data collected under an earlier consent cannot automatically be repurposed under a revised policy — consent must be specific, informed, and cover each purpose of use.

Data Protection Board · DPDPB

Data Protection Board — Status Update

The DPDP Act 2023 establishes the Data Protection Board of India as the primary adjudicatory and enforcement body. As of April 2026, the Board has not yet been formally constituted.

Current Status
DPDPB Not Yet Constituted

The Data Protection Board of India has not been formally constituted as of April 2026. Section 18 of the DPDP Act requires the Central Government to notify the establishment of the Board by gazette notification — this has not occurred yet.

Consequence: No DPDPB adjudicatory orders, consent notices, or penalty proceedings exist. Any source claiming otherwise should be treated with caution.

What the Act Provides
DPDPB Powers Under the DPDP Act

Once constituted, the DPDPB will have power to:

  • Inquire into personal data breaches on complaint or suo motu (§28)
  • Issue directions to Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors (§27)
  • Impose financial penalties up to ₹250 Crore per violation (§33)
  • Hear appeals (via Appellate Tribunal under IT Act) (§29)
  • Block non-compliant entities from processing data of Indian residents
Pending Steps
What Must Happen Before the Board Is Operational
  • Gazette notification formally constituting the DPDPB under §18
  • Appointment of Chairperson and Board Members by the Central Government
  • Notification of DPDP Rules (the Rules were published in draft form in January 2025 for public comment)
  • Operationalisation of the Consent Manager framework
  • Establishment of the digital office / complaint filing portal
