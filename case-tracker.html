Supreme Court of India

Landmark Supreme Court Decisions

Foundational Supreme Court judgements that shaped India's data protection, surveillance law, and digital evidence framework — many forming the direct constitutional and statutory basis for the DPDP Act 2023 and IT Act enforcement.

Supreme Court · 9-Judge Bench · 2017
Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India
Whether the right to privacy is a fundamental right under the Indian Constitution
Outcome
Unanimous 9-0: Privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21 (Right to Life). All earlier contrary judgements overruled. Privacy includes informational privacy — the right to control one's personal data.
The constitutional bedrock of the DPDP Act 2023. Every data protection right in the Act flows from this judgement.
Supreme Court · 5-Judge Bench · 26 September 2018 · (2019) 1 SCC 1
Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India — Aadhaar Case
Constitutionality of the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act, 2016 and biometric data collection by the state
Outcome
4:1 majority upheld core Aadhaar Act as constitutional. Struck down Section 57 (private entities mandating Aadhaar for services) and Section 33(2) (disclosure to police without court order) as unconstitutional. Biometric data classified as sensitive personal information.
Private sector cannot mandate Aadhaar for services — this holding directly shapes DPDP Act's prohibition on coercive consent. Sets constitutional limits on biometric data use by state and private actors.
Supreme Court · 24 March 2015 · (2015) 5 SCC 1
Shreya Singhal v. Union of India
Constitutionality of Section 66A of the IT Act 2000 — criminal liability for online speech; also the scope of intermediary safe harbour under Section 79
Outcome
Section 66A struck down in entirety as unconstitutional — vague, overbroad, violates Article 19(1)(a) free speech guarantee. Bench: J. Chelameswar and R.F. Nariman JJ. Section 79 (intermediary safe harbour) read down — intermediaries must remove content only on court or government orders, not on private complaints alone.
Foundational case defining limits of state power over online speech. The Section 79 reading directly governs intermediary liability under the IT Act's safe harbour framework and shapes DPDP Act compliance for platforms.
Supreme Court · 18 December 1996 · (1997) 1 SCC 301
People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India — Telephone Tapping Case
Whether state interception of telephone communications without procedural safeguards violates the right to privacy under Article 21
Outcome
Bench: Kuldip Singh J and S. Sagir Ahmad J. The Court held that phone tapping constitutes a serious invasion of privacy. Until Parliament legislated on the subject, the Government was directed to follow strict procedural guidelines — including ministerial-level authorisation, limited duration, and periodic review — before intercepting communications under Section 5(2) of the Indian Telegraph Act, 1885.
Predates the IT Act but established that surveillance by the state must be procedurally governed. The PUCL guidelines remained the only safeguard for communications surveillance until the IT Act's Section 69 rules were framed. Relevant to any analysis of AI-driven surveillance, CDR analysis, and lawful interception under Indian law.
Supreme Court · 3-Judge Bench · 18 September 2014 · (2014) 10 SCC 473
Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer — Electronic Evidence Admissibility
Whether electronic records can be admitted as secondary evidence without a certificate under Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872
Outcome
Bench: Kurian Joseph, R.F. Nariman and Arun Misra JJ. Held that a Section 65B certificate is mandatory for electronic records produced as secondary evidence — without it, such evidence is inadmissible. Overruled the earlier position in State (NCT of Delhi) v. Navjot Sandhu. Primary evidence (original hard drive / device in court) does not require the certificate.
Critical for cybercrime prosecutions: every digital printout, screenshot, CCTV recording, or call log submitted in court must be accompanied by a 65B certificate from a responsible official. Failure to obtain this certificate at the investigation stage can collapse a prosecution.
Supreme Court · 3-Judge Bench · 14 July 2020 · (2020) 7 SCC 1
Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Gorantyal — Electronic Evidence Reaffirmed
Whether the mandatory Section 65B certificate requirement from Anvar P.V. applies uniformly, and what remedies exist when a party is wrongly denied access to such a certificate
Outcome
Bench: R.F. Nariman, S. Ravindra Bhat and V. Ramasubramanian JJ. Reaffirmed Anvar P.V. — 65B certificate is mandatory without exception for secondary electronic evidence. Added that if a party is unable to obtain the certificate (e.g., the government refuses), the court can use its Section 165 Evidence Act powers to call for it. Clarified that the certificate can only be given by the person responsible for the computer producing the record.
Settled law on electronic evidence in India. Practitioners must secure a 65B certificate at the earliest stage — ideally at seizure. The judgment also clarifies who the "certifier" must be, which matters for cloud-stored data and SaaS platforms subject to DPDP Act obligations.
Supreme Court · 2-Judge Bench · 16 October 2019 · (2020) 4 SCC 162
Google India Pvt. Ltd. v. Visaka Industries — Intermediary Liability After Notice
Whether Google India can claim absolute immunity as an intermediary under Section 79 of the IT Act when it has actual knowledge of defamatory content posted on its platform and fails to remove it
Outcome
Bench: Ashok Bhushan and Navin Sinha JJ. The Court declined to quash defamation proceedings against Google India. Once an intermediary has actual knowledge of unlawful or defamatory content (via a takedown notice or otherwise), Section 79 immunity is conditional — the platform must act expeditiously. Failure to remove content after actual knowledge defeats the safe harbour defence.
Platforms cannot claim Section 79 immunity after they are notified of illegal content and fail to act. The "actual knowledge" test governs — relevant for platforms processing personal data under the DPDP Act where a data principal raises a deletion or erasure request that the platform ignores.
High Courts

High Court Decisions

Verified High Court precedents on data protection, cybercrime, and digital rights. Cases are added only after cross-referencing official court records or established legal databases (SCC Online / Manupatra).

Delhi High Court · W.P.(C) 7663/2016 · August 2016
Karmanya Singh Sareen & Anr. v. Union of India & Ors. — WhatsApp Privacy Policy
Whether WhatsApp's September 2016 privacy policy update — permitting data sharing with Facebook — violated Indian users' right to privacy and informational self-determination
Outcome
Delhi HC directed WhatsApp to delete all data of Indian users who had not accepted the revised 2016 policy by 25 September 2016. The Division Bench held that users who did not opt in cannot have their data shared with Facebook entities. MeitY was directed to frame a data protection policy for India — an early impetus for what became the DPDP Act 2023.
Pre-existing data collected under an earlier consent cannot automatically be repurposed under a revised policy — consent must be specific, informed, and cover each purpose of use.
Delhi High Court · CS(OS) 1279/2001 · 2001
SMC Pneumatics (India) Pvt. Ltd. v. Jogesh Kwatra — Email Defamation
Whether defamatory statements sent via email to a person's employer and colleagues constitute actionable defamation; and whether Indian courts can grant injunctive relief against online defamation
Outcome
Delhi HC granted an ex-parte ad-interim injunction restraining the defendant — a former employee — from sending defamatory emails to the plaintiff company's MD and business associates. This is India's first reported case of court-granted injunctive relief against cyber defamation. The court held that emails constitute a valid medium for defamation and that Indian courts have jurisdiction even for content transmitted digitally.
Emails, messages, and posts carry the same legal weight as printed defamatory matter. The case established that corporate victims of cyber harassment — including defamatory mass emails — can obtain emergency injunctions from Indian courts.
Delhi High Court · 23 March 2005 · 119 (2005) DLT 596
National Association of Software & Service Companies (NASSCOM) v. Ajay Sood & Ors. — Phishing Defined
Whether phishing — creating a spoof website and sending fraudulent emails impersonating a legitimate organisation to extract personal data — constitutes an actionable wrong under Indian law
Outcome
Delhi HC declared phishing illegal for the first time in Indian law. The defendants had created a website (nasscom.co.in) deceptively similar to NASSCOM's official site and sent fraudulent emails in NASSCOM officials' names to extract personal and professional data. The court defined phishing as "a misrepresentation made in the course of trade leading to confusion as to the source and origin of the e-mail." Injunction granted; defendants directed to pay ₹16 Lakh in damages.
India's first judicial recognition of phishing as a legal wrong — predating specific phishing legislation. This precedent underpins all identity theft and impersonation prosecutions under Section 66D of the IT Act (inserted in 2008 amendments).
Delhi High Court · 29 May 2008 · 150 (2008) DLT 769
Avnish Bajaj v. State (NCT of Delhi) — The Baazee.com / MMS Case
Criminal liability of a platform CEO (intermediary) for obscene content listed by a user on the company's e-commerce website; limits of corporate director liability under the IT Act
Outcome
A student had listed an obscene MMS clip for sale on Baazee.com (now eBay India); the CEO Avnish Bajaj was arrested. Delhi HC held that under IPC Sections 292/294 there is no automatic personal criminal liability of a director where the company has not been arraigned. However, under IT Act Section 67 read with Section 85 (corporate offences), a prima facie case was made out against the director since the Act recognises deemed criminal liability of directors. The prosecution was allowed to proceed on the IT Act charge alone.
Directly catalysed the 2008 IT Act amendments introducing the formal safe harbour regime under Section 79. Any CXO of a platform operating in India must ensure the company has an operational content takedown and grievance redressal system — or face personal criminal exposure under Section 85.
Punjab & Haryana High Court · CRR 66/2013 · 10 January 2013
Sanjay Kumar v. State of Haryana — Bank IT Fraud via System Manipulation
Whether a software maintenance vendor who exploits privileged access to a bank's computerised accounting system to fraudulently credit amounts to his own account is guilty under both the IPC and Sections 65–66 of the IT Act
Outcome
FIR No. 165/2003, Vijaya Bank Faridabad. The accused, a software vendor with administrative access to the bank's system, manipulated interest entries, inflated balances, and transferred ₹17,67,409 to his personal account over multiple transactions. Convicted at trial and on appeal. P&H HC dismissed revision — held that evidence of forged electronic records was overwhelming; convicted under IPC Sections 420, 467, 468, 471 and IT Act Sections 65 (tampering with computer source documents) and 66 (hacking). Acquitted under Section 72 (breach of confidentiality) as no disclosure to third parties was proven.
Privileged system access does not mean unfettered freedom. Insider manipulation of computerised bank records attracts both IPC fraud provisions and IT Act offences. Relevant to access-control and audit-log requirements under DPDP Act's data security obligations for Data Fiduciaries.
Trial Court — Landmark First

First IT Act Conviction

Notable lower court decisions that established key precedents in India's cyber law history — even before the High Courts had occasion to rule.

Addl. Chief Metropolitan Magistrate · Egmore, Chennai · November 2004
State of Tamil Nadu v. Suhas Katti — First Conviction Under IT Act
Whether posting obscene and harassing messages about a woman on the internet under a fake identity, and transmitting her contact details to cause unsolicited calls and messages, constitutes an offence under the IT Act 2000
Outcome
India's first successful prosecution under the IT Act 2000. The accused — an acquaintance of the victim — created a fake profile for her and posted obscene messages attributing statements to her, along with her contact number. She received hundreds of obscene calls. The case was disposed of in just 7 days from registration — a record for cybercrime prosecution at the time. Convicted under Section 67 IT Act (publishing obscene material in electronic form) and Section 509 IPC (word, gesture, or act intended to insult the modesty of a woman). Sentenced to rigorous imprisonment and fine.
Proved that Indian courts could move swiftly on cyber harassment cases and that the IT Act's Section 67 was workable for online abuse. Predated social media but the principle applies directly to deepfakes, morphed images, and impersonation attacks on platforms — all now covered by IT Act and DPDP Act together.
Data Protection Board · DPDPB

Data Protection Board — Status Update

The DPDP Act 2023 establishes the Data Protection Board of India as the primary adjudicatory and enforcement body. As of April 2026, the Board has not yet been formally constituted.

Current Status
DPDPB Not Yet Constituted

The Data Protection Board of India has not been formally constituted as of April 2026. Section 18 of the DPDP Act requires the Central Government to notify the establishment of the Board by gazette notification — this has not occurred yet.

Consequence: No DPDPB adjudicatory orders, consent notices, or penalty proceedings exist. Any source claiming otherwise should be treated with caution.

What the Act Provides
DPDPB Powers Under the DPDP Act

Once constituted, the DPDPB will have power to:

  • Inquire into personal data breaches on complaint or suo motu (§28)
  • Issue directions to Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors (§27)
  • Impose financial penalties up to ₹250 Crore per violation (§33)
  • Hear appeals (via Appellate Tribunal under IT Act) (§29)
  • Block non-compliant entities from processing data of Indian residents
Pending Steps
What Must Happen Before the Board Is Operational
  • Gazette notification formally constituting the DPDPB under §18
  • Appointment of Chairperson and Board Members by the Central Government
  • Notification of DPDP Rules (the Rules were published in draft form in January 2025 for public comment)
  • Operationalisation of the Consent Manager framework
  • Establishment of the digital office / complaint filing portal
