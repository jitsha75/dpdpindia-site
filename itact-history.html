Article 01 of 05 · Historical Evolution

IT Act 2000: Origins, Global Genesis
& the 2008 Security Pivot

From the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Commerce (1996) to India's enactment, and the landmark 2008 amendment that shifted focus from paperless trade to cybersecurity and corporate data liability.

Part I — Global Genesis (1996–2000)

The UNCITRAL Foundation

India's Information Technology Act, 2000 did not emerge in a vacuum. Its intellectual and structural genesis lies in the United Nations Commission on International Trade Law (UNCITRAL) Model Law on Electronic Commerce, 1996. The UN General Assembly, recognising the explosive growth of cross-border electronic commerce, recommended that member states modernise their domestic laws to give legal recognition to electronic records, transactions, and signatures — enabling what the UN called "Paperless Trade."

India formally adopted the UNCITRAL Model Law's architecture, making it one of the first Asian democracies at the time to enact a comprehensive framework for electronic commerce and digital transactions. This alignment with international standards was deliberate: it facilitated cross-border commerce, reduced legal uncertainty for foreign investors, and positioned India within the global digital trade framework.

"If a law requires information to be in writing, an electronic record satisfies that requirement if the information contained therein is accessible so as to be usable for subsequent reference."
— Principle of Functional Equivalence, UNCITRAL Model Law 1996

The Principle of Functional Equivalence

The cornerstone of the UNCITRAL framework — and therefore of the IT Act — is the doctrine of Functional Equivalence. This principle holds that electronic records and signatures perform the same legal functions as their paper-based equivalents, provided certain conditions of reliability and accessibility are met. In practical terms:

  • If a law requires a document to be "in writing," an electronic record satisfies this if it is accessible for future reference.
  • If a law requires a "signature," a digital signature satisfies this if it identifies the signatory and indicates their intent.
  • If a law requires a document to be "retained" or "preserved," electronic storage satisfies this if the record is reproducible.

These principles are embodied in Sections 4, 5, and 7 of the IT Act, 2000 respectively — giving legal standing to electronic records, digital signatures, and electronic document retention.

India's Enactment: May 2000

The Information Technology Act, 2000 was passed by the Indian Parliament in May 2000 and notified on 17 October 2000. At that time, India became one of the few countries in the world with a dedicated, comprehensive statute on electronic commerce and cybercrime. The Act simultaneously:

  • Amended the Indian Penal Code, 1860 and the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 to recognise electronic records and digital evidence.
  • Amended the Bankers' Books Evidence Act, 1891 for electronic banking records.
  • Established the Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA) to regulate digital signature infrastructure.
  • Created the Cyber Appellate Tribunal (CAT) for dispute resolution.
  • Established offences and penalties for cybercrimes — hacking (s. 66), publishing obscene material (s. 67), and breach of confidentiality (s. 72).

Part II — The IT (Amendment) Act, 2008: The Pivot to Security

By 2005, it was evident that the IT Act, 2000 was primarily an e-commerce statute that was poorly equipped to address the emerging landscape of cybercrime, corporate data breaches, and state surveillance needs. A series of high-profile incidents — including the Baazee.com case and the 2008 Mumbai terror attacks, which exposed gaps in India's electronic surveillance architecture — accelerated the amendment process.

The IT (Amendment) Act, 2008, notified on 27 October 2009, fundamentally recalibrated the Act's centre of gravity from commercial facilitation to cybersecurity and data governance.

Technology Neutrality — Electronic Signatures

One of the most significant conceptual shifts in the 2008 amendment was the replacement of the narrowly defined "Digital Signatures" (which required PKI-based infrastructure) with the broader concept of "Electronic Signatures" under Section 3A. This made the Act technology-neutral — any authentication technique specified in the Second Schedule (and subsequently notified) would qualify as a valid electronic signature. This was critical for accommodating emerging authentication methods like OTP-based e-signing and biometric authentication used in Aadhaar eKYC.

Section 43A · IT Act 2000 (as amended 2008)

Corporate Liability for Data Protection

Where a body corporate, possessing, dealing with or handling any sensitive personal data or information in a computer resource which it owns, controls or operates, is negligent in implementing and maintaining reasonable security practices and procedures and thereby causes wrongful loss or wrongful gain to any person, such body corporate shall be liable to pay damages by way of compensation, not exceeding five crore rupees, to the person so affected.

Note: Section 43A is largely superseded by the DPDP Act 2023 for personal data matters. The DPDP Act's penalty regime (up to ₹250 crore) now governs data protection obligations.

Section 43A — Reasonable Security Practices

Section 43A introduced the concept of "Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures" into Indian law — the first time a statutory obligation of this nature was imposed on private entities. A body corporate that handles "Sensitive Personal Data or Information" (SPDI) was obligated to implement these practices and could face compensation claims up to ₹5 crore for negligent data handling causing wrongful loss or gain.

The MeitY subsequently issued the IT (Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures and Sensitive Personal Data or Information) Rules, 2011, defining what constitutes SPDI (passwords, financial information, health data, biometrics, etc.) and specifying the IS/ISO/IEC 27001 standard as a reference framework for "reasonable security practices." Section 43A is now substantially superseded by the DPDP Act 2023 for matters involving personal data of individuals.

Section 66A — The Controversial Addition (Now Struck Down)

Section 66A, inserted by the 2008 amendment, criminalised the sending of information through a computer resource or communication device that was:

  • (a) Grossly offensive or of a menacing character
  • (b) False and sent to cause annoyance, inconvenience, danger, obstruction, insult, injury, enmity, hatred, or ill will
  • (c) Intended to deceive or mislead the recipient about the origin

The provision attracted widespread criticism for its unconstitutionally vague language. Terms like "annoyance," "inconvenience," and "grossly offensive" gave police unchecked discretion, resulting in well-documented misuse against political dissidents, journalists, and social media users. The Supreme Court of India finally struck down Section 66A in its entirety in 2015 in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India — see Article 02 of this KB.

Section 69 — Expanded State Surveillance Powers

The 2008 amendment significantly expanded Section 69, conferring upon the Central Government and State Governments the power to direct any agency to intercept, monitor, or decrypt any information transmitted through a computer resource if it is in the interest of:

  • Sovereignty and integrity of India
  • Defence of India
  • Security of the State
  • Friendly relations with foreign states
  • Public order
  • Prevention of incitement to commission of any cognisable offence
  • Investigation of any offence

The procedural safeguards for Section 69 were specified in the IT (Procedure and Safeguards for Interception, Monitoring and Decryption of Information) Rules, 2009. These Rules created a multi-tier authorisation process, but critics have raised concerns about the breadth of the "public order" and "incitement" grounds.

Part III — Section 69A: Blocking of Public Access

Section 69A, also inserted by the 2008 amendment, authorises the Central Government to direct any agency to block access to online content for public viewing where it is deemed necessary in the interests of sovereignty and integrity of India, defence, state security, friendly relations with foreign states, or public order. The IT (Procedure and Safeguards for Blocking for Access of Information by Public) Rules, 2009 govern this process.

Critically, the Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal upheld Section 69A as constitutional — distinguishing it from the struck-down Section 66A on the grounds that 69A is "a narrowly drawn provision with several safeguards" including committee review, written directions, and judicial review. As of 2025–26, Section 69A is at the centre of a significant litigation involving X Corp (formerly Twitter) challenging the government's use of Section 79(3)(b) as an alternative blocking mechanism that bypasses 69A's procedural safeguards.
