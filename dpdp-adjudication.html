01

The Data Protection Board of India (DPB)

Structure, powers, and independence considerations

The Data Protection Board of India (DPB) is the enforcement and adjudicatory authority created by the DPDP Act. The DPB is established as a digital-by-default body — it receives complaints electronically, conducts proceedings through video conferencing, and issues orders through an online portal.

The Central Government appoints the DPB's Chairperson and members — a design feature that civil society organisations have criticised as potentially compromising the Board's independence from the government, particularly given Section 17's broad government exemptions.

DPB Functions
Adjudication & Enforcement
The DPB receives and adjudicates complaints from data principals against Data Fiduciaries. It can conduct inquiries, summon evidence, and issue directions and penalties. Its orders are appealable to the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT).
DPB Powers
Investigation & Interim Relief
The DPB can call for information from any Data Fiduciary, conduct inquiries, and issue interim directions where there is an imminent threat of harm. It can also initiate proceedings suo motu based on information from any source.
Digital-First
Technology-Driven Operations
The DPB operates primarily through digital channels — online complaint portal, video-conferenced hearings, and electronic orders. This design aims to reduce procedural delays but requires data principals to have digital access to use the system effectively.
Independence Concern
Government-Appointed Members
The Act provides for Central Government appointment of the DPB Chairperson and members. Academic and civil society commentary has noted this may limit institutional independence, particularly in complaints involving government entities that are exempt under Section 17.
02

Dispute Resolution Flow

From breach notification to TDSAT appeal — the complete enforcement pathway

1
Breach / Violation Occurs
A personal data breach occurs, or a Data Fiduciary fails to comply with the Act or Rules. The Data Fiduciary has an obligation to self-report breaches to the DPB within prescribed timelines.
2
Complaint Filed or Inquiry Initiated
A data principal files a complaint with the DPB through the online portal (fee-free), OR the DPB initiates an inquiry suo motu based on information from any source.
3
DPB Inquiry
The DPB reviews the complaint, may call for information from the Data Fiduciary, and may summon both parties. Proceedings can be conducted via video conference. The Data Fiduciary has an opportunity to be heard.
4
Adjudicatory Order — Penalties
If a violation is established, the DPB issues a reasoned order and may impose a monetary penalty. The order specifies the violation found, the penalty amount, and remediation required.
5
TDSAT Appeal
Either party may appeal the DPB's order to the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT) within 60 days of the order. TDSAT's order can be further challenged in the High Court.
Accessibility feature: Any data principal can file a complaint with the DPB without engaging a lawyer and without paying a fee. This low-barrier access is a deliberate design choice to prevent enforcement being limited to well-resourced complainants.
03

Penalty Schedule — Fixed Caps, Not Turnover-Based

The complete monetary penalty framework under Schedule I of the Act

The DPDP Act takes a fixed-cap approach to monetary penalties — a deliberate departure from GDPR's percentage-of-global-turnover model. The maximum penalties are specified in Schedule I of the Act. The DPB must consider the severity of the breach, repetition, and mitigating factors when determining the actual penalty within the cap.

Schedule I — Maximum Monetary Penalties
Failure to implement reasonable security safeguards (Section 8)₹250 Cr
Failure to notify the DPB of a personal data breach (Section 8)₹200 Cr
Violation of children's data obligations (Section 9)₹200 Cr
Violation of Significant Data Fiduciary obligations (Section 10)₹150 Cr
Breach of duties of Data Processor₹10 Cr
General violations — non-compliance with any other provision₹50 Cr
False / frivolous complaints by data principals₹10,000
Fixed cap vs GDPR: Under GDPR, the maximum fine is 4% of global annual turnover or €20 million — whichever is higher. For a global tech company with €200B+ annual turnover, this produces a potential €8B maximum fine. The DPDP Act's ₹250 Crore cap (approximately €28 million) is significantly lower in absolute terms for large multinationals, leading critics to argue it lacks deterrent effect for the largest data processors operating in India.
