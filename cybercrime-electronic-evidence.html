BSA 2023 · §§ 61–63

Electronic Evidence
in Indian Courts

The complete guide to BSA 2023 Sections 61–63 (formerly IEA §65A/65B), the mandatory §63 certificate, admissibility conditions, and the landmark Supreme Court judgments that define how digital evidence enters an Indian trial.

BSA 2023 IEA §65A/65B Anvar PV v Basheer Arjun Panditrao §63 Certificate Cybercrime Evidence
Statutory Framework

BSA 2023 vs IEA 1872 — The Mapping

The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023 (BSA) replaced the Indian Evidence Act 1872 on 1 July 2024 but retained the architecture of §65A and §65B IEA almost verbatim, renumbering them as §§ 61–63 BSA. Any case-law on IEA §65A/65B continues to apply directly to BSA §§ 61–63.

BSA 2023IEA 1872Subject
§ 61§ 65ASpecial provisions — admissibility of electronic records
§ 63§ 65BElectronic records as evidence — the certificate-driven gateway
§ 62—Secondary evidence of electronic records (new clarificatory provision)
§ 57§ 59Oral evidence — still primary for facts
Foundational Distinction

Primary vs Secondary Electronic Evidence

Indian evidence law draws a sharp distinction between the original device and a copy of its contents. The admissibility route differs entirely based on which form is produced in court.

FormDescription§ 63 Certificate Required?
PrimaryOriginal device / storage medium produced before the courtNo — the original speaks for itself
SecondaryPrintout, clone, copy, screenshot, forensic imageYes — mandatory; inadmissible without it

This distinction, confirmed in Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (2020), resolves decades of confusion: if the original device is in court, no certificate is needed. If anything else is tendered, §63 applies strictly.

The Gateway Provision

Section 63 BSA 2023 — Four Conditions + The Certificate

§ 63(1) BSA 2023

Any information contained in an electronic record which is printed on paper, stored, recorded or copied in optical or magnetic media or semiconductor memory which is produced by a computer or any communication device… shall be deemed to be also a document… admissible in any proceedings, without further proof or production of the original, as evidence of any contents of the original or of any fact stated therein, of which direct evidence would be admissible.

The Four Conditions — §63(2)

Before any computer output can be admitted as evidence, the party tendering it must satisfy all four conditions:

01

Regular use during the relevant period

The computer output was produced by a computer that was regularly used to store or process information for the activities carried on during the relevant period.

02

Information regularly fed in the ordinary course

Information of the kind contained in the electronic record was regularly fed into the computer in the ordinary course of those activities.

03

Computer operating properly

The computer was operating properly throughout the material part of the period; or any malfunction was such that it did not affect the electronic record or the accuracy of its contents.

04

Derived from information fed in

The information in the document reproduces or is derived from information fed into the computer in the ordinary course of the relevant activities.

The §63(4) Certificate — Who Signs It and What It Must State

The certificate must be signed by a person "occupying a responsible official position in relation to the operation of the relevant device or the management of the relevant activities." It must:

  • Identify the electronic record and state how it was produced
  • Provide particulars of any device involved in its production
  • Deal with any of the four conditions under §63(2) as may be appropriate
  • Purport to be signed "to the best of the knowledge and belief" of the signatory
Critical Practice Point

A §63 certificate cannot be dispensed with on grounds of hardship or inconvenience — Shafhi Mohammad v State of HP (2018 2-judge bench) was expressly overruled on this point by the 3-judge bench in Arjun Panditrao. However, courts have discretion to permit the certificate to be filed at the trial stage even after documents are already in evidence.

Landmark Judgments

The Cases That Define Admissibility

Supreme Court of India · (2014) 10 SCC 473 · 3-Judge Bench

Anvar P.V. v P.K. Basheer & Ors

Facts: An election petition involved audio-visual CDs tendered as evidence of corrupt practices. The trial court admitted them without a §65B IEA certificate, relying on oral evidence under §65 IEA.

Held: §65B IEA is the exclusive mode for admissibility of secondary electronic evidence. Electronic records cannot be admitted merely through oral testimony. §65B is a complete code in itself and overrides the general secondary evidence provisions of §§63, 65 IEA. Without a §65B certificate — the evidence is inadmissible.

Watershed Ruling — Still Good Law
Supreme Court of India · (2015) 7 SCC 178

Tomaso Bruno v State of UP

Held: Electronic records could be admitted through §63 IEA (oral evidence), allowing a §65B certificate to be dispensed with where the original was produced.

Overruled by Arjun Panditrao
Supreme Court of India · (2020) 7 SCC 1 · 3-Judge Bench

Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal

Facts: Election dispute involving electronic evidence where the §65B certificate was not filed with the document but at a later stage.

Held: (1) Clarified Anvar PV: certificate is a mandatory pre-condition for secondary electronic evidence. (2) Where the original device itself is produced as primary evidence, no certificate is needed. (3) Courts have discretion to permit late filing of the certificate at the trial stage. (4) Shafhi Mohammad overruled — certificate cannot be dispensed with on convenience grounds. (5) Tomaso Bruno expressly overruled.

Current Controlling Authority
Supreme Court of India · (2018) 2 SCC 801 · 2-Judge Bench

Shafhi Mohammad v State of Himachal Pradesh

Held: §65B certificate may be relaxed when the party seeking to produce evidence cannot reasonably obtain it.

Overruled by Arjun Panditrao
Practical Compliance

§63 BSA Certificate — Essential Elements

Drawing from Anvar PV and Arjun Panditrao, a compliant §63 BSA certificate (formerly §65B IEA) must contain the following elements to withstand challenge:

ElementWhat to Include
Identification of recordDescription of the electronic record: filename, hash value (SHA-256), file size, creation/modification date
Device particularsMake, model, serial number of the computer/device that produced the record
Period of regular useDates during which the computer was regularly used for the relevant activities
Data-entry confirmationStatement that information of this kind was regularly fed in the ordinary course of business
Proper operation statementStatement that the computer operated properly; or if malfunction occurred, that it did not affect this record
Signatory particularsName, designation, and official position of the person signing; must hold responsible official position re: device operation
Knowledge qualificationStatement "to the best of knowledge and belief"

Common Defects That Get Evidence Excluded

  • Certificate signed by IT officer who did not operate the relevant system
  • No hash value — defence challenges that printout may differ from original file
  • Certificate does not cover the specific dates of the transactions in dispute
  • General certificate for "all CCTV footage" rather than the specific clip tendered
  • WhatsApp screenshots tendered without certificate from Meta/service provider
  • Certificate filed after charge is framed — timing challenge even if discretion may cure it
Applied Analysis

Evidence Admissibility in Specific Cybercrime Contexts

WhatsApp / Signal Messages

Screenshots of chat messages are secondary electronic evidence. Certificate must be obtained from the device owner (or law enforcement if device is seized) in the capacity of the "responsible official" who manages the device. Courts have faced difficulty when the other party's device is unavailable — defence in Arjun Panditrao terms argues the certificate cannot be waived.

CCTV Footage

Footage from private establishments requires a certificate from the person responsible for the CCTV system (typically the security manager or building manager). Police must obtain a signed certificate at the time of seizure — not months later — to avoid late-filing challenges.

Email Evidence

Printouts of emails require certificates from the email service provider or the IT administrator who manages the corporate email server. For Gmail/Outlook etc., Indian courts have accepted certificates from the corporate IT head confirming the print was generated from the system during the relevant period.

Financial Transaction Records / Bank Logs

Banks are well-practiced in issuing §65B/§63 certificates for digital transaction records. The bank's Nodal Officer for IT typically signs. These certificates are rarely challenged — the risk area is ATM CCTV footage which falls under a separate CCTV certificate requirement.

Deleted or Recovered Files

Files recovered by forensic tools (FTK, EnCase) present a harder problem. The certificate must come from the certified forensic examiner under §79A IT Act designation. The examiner must also explain the recovery methodology in the certificate to satisfy Condition 3 (computer operating properly).
