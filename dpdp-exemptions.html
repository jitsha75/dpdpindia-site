01

Section 17 — The Government Exemption Provisions

The most contested part of the DPDP Act

Section 17 of the DPDP Act empowers the Central Government to exempt any instrumentality of the state from all or some of the Act's provisions — including the consent requirements, data principal rights, and data fiduciary obligations. This is done through a notification in the Official Gazette, without Parliamentary approval.

The grounds for exemption include: interests of sovereignty, integrity, and security of India; prevention of incitement to offences; maintenance of public order; and maintaining friendly relations with foreign states.

Section 17(2) — Grounds for Government Exemption
(a)Interests of sovereignty and integrity of India
(b)Security of the State
(c)Friendly relations with foreign States
(d)Maintenance of public order
(e)Prevention of incitement to any cognizable offence
Constitutional concern: The Puttaswamy (2017) 9-judge bench established that any state interference with privacy must satisfy a three-part test: Legality, Legitimate Aim, and Proportionality. Section 17 contains no proportionality constraint on the Central Government's exemption power — civil society has argued this may not withstand constitutional scrutiny.
02

Who Can Be Exempted and How

The notification mechanism — executive power without Parliamentary scrutiny

Broad Scope
Entire Act Can Be Excluded
A government body could be exempted from all provisions — including the consent requirement, the data principal rights under Sections 11–14, the security safeguards obligation, and the DPB's jurisdiction. Consent rights and erasure rights would not apply to that body.
No Parliamentary Scrutiny
Executive Notification — Not Legislation
The exemption is granted by Central Government notification — secondary legislation that does not require Parliamentary approval. This concentrates significant power in the executive to determine which government bodies are accountable under data protection law.
Conditional Safeguards
Government May Attach Conditions
The notification may attach conditions — e.g., requiring the exempt body to maintain security safeguards. Whether such conditions are consistently attached in practice remains to be seen once exemptions are actually notified.
No Sunset Clause
Indefinite Duration by Default
There is no requirement for exemptions to be time-limited or periodically renewed. An exemption granted once would remain in force indefinitely unless revoked by a subsequent notification.
03

The Puttaswamy Tension and Judicial Review Prospects

Where the constitutional challenge may arise

The most sustained academic criticism of the DPDP Act focuses on the gap between Section 17's exemption framework and the proportionality standard from Puttaswamy. Critics note three specific tensions:

  • Legality is satisfied — a Gazette notification is 'law' in the formal sense.
  • Legitimate aim is broadly met — sovereignty, security, and public order are well-recognised state interests.
  • Proportionality is absent — the Act imposes no requirement that exemptions be limited to what is strictly necessary, that alternatives be considered, or that the scope of the exemption match the stated justification.

Judicial review of a Section 17 exemption notification would be available through a High Court or Supreme Court writ petition. The Puttaswamy standard would apply in any such challenge — and the government would bear the burden of demonstrating that the exemption is proportionate to the legitimate aim invoked.

Comparison with GDPR Article 23: GDPR's equivalent provision allows member states to restrict data protection obligations for national security and other reasons, but requires the restriction to be done through legislation (not executive notification), to be necessary and proportionate, and to contain specific safeguards including provisions on purpose limitation, categories of data, and retention periods. The DPDP Act's Section 17 provides none of these procedural constraints.
