SARAL — The Overarching Framework

Simple, Accessible, Responsible, and Accountable Legislation

The DPDP Act 2023 is built around the SARAL framework — an acronym capturing its design philosophy: Simple (short, principles-based statute), Accessible (notices must be clear and in regional languages), Responsible (data fiduciaries bear accountability), and Accountable (enforcement through the Data Protection Board).

The Act applies to processing of digital personal data — (a) within India, and (b) outside India if it involves offering goods or services to data principals in India. It does not apply to processing by individuals for personal or domestic purposes, or to personal data made publicly available by the data principal themselves.

Scope note: Unlike GDPR, the DPDP Act currently covers only digital personal data (including data that was originally offline but has been digitised). Pure offline/physical personal data processing is not within scope — though this boundary may shift in future rules.

Sections 4–6: Notice, Consent, and Lawful Processing

The consent architecture that every Data Fiduciary must implement

Section 4 — Lawful Processing
Grounds for Processing
Processing is lawful only if: (a) the data principal has given consent for one or more specified purposes, or (b) it falls within one of the 'legitimate uses' — legal obligations, state functions, medical emergencies, employment, or public interest. There is no broad 'legitimate interests' ground.
Section 5 — Notice
Notice Requirements
Before seeking consent, Data Fiduciaries must provide clear notice in English or any scheduled language of India. The notice must describe what data is collected, the purpose, how to exercise rights, and how to reach the grievance officer. Vague or bundled notices are prohibited.
Section 6 — Consent
Consent Standards
Consent must be free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous. It must be given through a clear affirmative action. Consent can be revoked at any time — and the Data Fiduciary must cease processing within a reasonable time after revocation.
Legitimate Uses
Processing Without Consent
Section 7 lists limited grounds where processing is lawful without consent: the State performing its functions, legal obligations, medical treatment, national security, employment purposes, and protecting the vital interests of the data principal.
Revocation implication: Once a data principal revokes consent, the Data Fiduciary must stop processing and — subject to retention obligations under other laws — delete the data. There is no GDPR-style 'legitimate interests' that can override a withdrawn consent.

Section 9: Children's Data — Special Protection

No tracking, no behavioural monitoring, verifiable parental consent mandatory

Section 9 provides the Act's strongest protections. A Data Fiduciary must not process personal data of a child (person under 18 years of age) without verifiable consent of the parent or lawful guardian. The rules will specify the mechanism for age verification and parental consent verification.

Three absolute prohibitions apply to all entities processing children's data — these cannot be overridden by consent:

Prohibition 1
No Behavioural Monitoring
No tracking or monitoring of children's online behaviour for profiling, targeted advertising, or any other purpose. This covers recommendation systems, click tracking, engagement optimisation, and any similar mechanism.
Prohibition 2
No Targeted Advertising to Children
Serving advertisements targeted at children based on profiling is prohibited — regardless of whether the parent has consented to data processing for other purposes.
Prohibition 3
Processing Detrimental to Children
Any processing that may be detrimental to a child's wellbeing is prohibited. This is a broadly worded provision that gives the DPB significant scope in enforcement against platforms and services.
Age gate obligation: Data Fiduciaries must implement age-verification systems. The DPDP Rules 2025 specify technical and procedural standards for verifying age. Operating services accessible to children without adequate age gating exposes a Data Fiduciary to up to ₹200 Crore in penalties under Section 33.
04

Section 10: Significant Data Fiduciaries

Elevated obligations for entities designated by the Central Government

The Central Government may designate any Data Fiduciary or class of Data Fiduciaries as a Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF) based on volume and sensitivity of data processed, risk to data principals' rights, national security considerations, and public order implications. SDFs face three additional obligations not required of ordinary Data Fiduciaries:

Three Additional SDF Obligations under Section 10
1
Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO)
The DPO must be based in India and represent the SDF before the Data Protection Board. Unlike GDPR, the Act does not require the DPO to have specific professional qualifications — though the rules may specify criteria.
2
Conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA)
DPIAs must be conducted for significant processing activities. The rules will specify when DPIAs are triggered, what they must cover, and whether outcomes must be shared with the DPB.
3
Periodic Independent Audits
SDFs must conduct periodic audits of their data processing activities by independent auditors. Audit reports may be required to be submitted to the DPB. The interval and scope of audits will be specified in rules.
