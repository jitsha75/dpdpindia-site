01

Who is a Data Fiduciary

Definition and relational nature of the role

A "Data Fiduciary" (Section 2(i)) is any person who, alone or in conjunction with others, determines the purpose and means of processing personal data. This is analogous to a "data controller" in GDPR terminology.

The concept is relational: an entity is a Data Fiduciary in relation to a specific dataset. The same organisation may be a Data Fiduciary for some processing and a Data Processor for others.

Entity Role Why
Hospital processing patient records Data Fiduciary Determines purpose (treatment) and means (EHR system)
Cloud provider hosting hospital's database Data Processor Acts on hospital's instructions; does not determine purpose
Payroll software managing employer's HR data Data Processor Processes on behalf of the employer (Data Fiduciary)
Ed-tech platform analysing student data for its own product Data Fiduciary Determines purpose of its own analytics
Analytics firm processing data for a brand's campaign Data Processor Acts on the brand's (Data Fiduciary's) instructions

The key distinction is who determines "why" and "how" the data is processed. Obligations flow primarily to the Data Fiduciary; Data Processors are bound contractually by the Fiduciary's instructions and must process only as directed.

02

Seven Core Obligations (Section 8)

Mandatory requirements for all Data Fiduciaries

Section 8 imposes seven core obligations on ALL Data Fiduciaries processing digital personal data of Indians. These are non-negotiable baseline requirements:

1. Purpose Limitation (S.8(1))
Process personal data only for the specified, clear, and lawful purpose for which consent was given (or a deemed consent ground exists). Secondary use of data requires fresh consent.
2. Data Minimisation (S.8(2))
Collect only such personal data as is necessary for the specified purpose. The standard is "necessary" — not "useful" or "might be useful later."
3. Data Accuracy (S.8(3) & 8(4))
Take reasonable steps to ensure data is accurate, complete, and kept up-to-date where the inaccuracy could adversely affect the data principal or the purpose of processing.
4. Storage Limitation (S.8(7))
Retain personal data only as long as necessary for the specified purpose. Once the purpose is fulfilled or consent is withdrawn, delete data unless retention is required by law.
5. Security Safeguards (S.8(5))
Implement appropriate technical and organisational security measures to protect personal data from breach, loss, alteration, or unauthorised access. Standard: "reasonable security safeguards" — context-dependent.
6. Breach Notification (S.8(6))
Notify the Data Protection Board AND affected Data Principals in the event of a personal data breach. Form and timeline to be prescribed in Rules.
7. Grievance Officer (S.8(10))
Appoint and publish contact details of a Grievance Officer. The officer must acknowledge complaints and resolve them within prescribed timelines.
Breach notification standard: Unlike GDPR's 72-hour breach notification, the DPDP Act requires simultaneous notification to BOTH the DPB and affected Data Principals. The timeline will be prescribed in Rules — but combined with CERT-In's mandatory 6-hour reporting, the practical expectation is rapid, coordinated action.
03

Data Breach Notification (S.8(6)) in Detail

What happens when personal data is compromised

A "personal data breach" is any unauthorised processing, accidental disclosure, acquisition, sharing, use, alteration, destruction, or loss of access to personal data that compromises its confidentiality, integrity, or availability.

Obligations triggered by a breach:

Notify the DPB: In prescribed form, within prescribed timeline (Rules pending)
Notify affected Data Principals: In plain language — what happened, what data was affected, what the Fiduciary is doing, and how to contact the Grievance Officer
Maintain internal records: Date, scope, nature of breach, remediation steps taken
DPDP Act S.8(6) CERT-In Directions 2022
Trigger Personal data breach Any of 50 cybersecurity incident types
Notify DPB + Data Principals CERT-In
Timeline As per Rules (pending) Within 6 hours of detection
Penalty for failure Up to ₹200 crore IT Act provisions
Dual compliance strategy: Design a unified breach response playbook that satisfies both frameworks. The CERT-In report (operational, within 6 hours) provides the factual base. The DPDP notification to Data Principals (plain language, layperson-friendly) is a separate communication. Draft both templates in advance and test them in breach simulation exercises.
04

Children's Data Obligations (S.9) — Summary

Extra protections for processing data of individuals under 18

Section 9 imposes additional obligations for processing personal data of children (under 18):

Verifiable parental consent before any processing begins
Absolute prohibition on behavioural tracking of children
Absolute prohibition on targeted advertising to children
Prohibition on processing detrimental to the child's well-being
Potential reduced-threshold exemptions for certain platforms under S.9(4) (Rules pending)
For full analysis: See the dedicated article on Children's Data — Section 9 Full Analysis for age verification challenges, global comparison (COPPA, GDPR, UK Children's Code), and the S.9(4) exemption controversy. Penalty for breach: up to ₹200 crore.
05

Significant Data Fiduciaries (Section 10)

Enhanced obligations for large-scale or high-risk processors

The Central Government may notify certain Data Fiduciaries as "Significant Data Fiduciaries" (SDFs) based on: volume/sensitivity of data processed; risk to rights of data principals; national security risk; risk to sovereignty or electoral democracy; public order risk.

SDFs face four additional mandatory obligations:

Obligation 1
Data Protection Officer (DPO)
Must appoint a DPO who is a natural person (individual, not corporate entity) based in India. The DPO represents the SDF before the DPB and is the primary contact for data principals.
Obligation 2
Independent Data Audit
Must commission periodic audits by an independent data auditor. Audit standards and framework for auditors to be prescribed in Rules.
Obligation 3
Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)
Must conduct periodic DPIAs to assess the impact of processing activities on data principals' rights. DPIA format to be prescribed.
Obligation 4
Government-Prescribed Safeguards
Central Government may impose additional restrictions on SDFs — including on data transfers, retention periods, or product features — by Rules or notification.
SDF designation status: As of April 2026, no SDF list has been officially published. Major tech platforms (social media, search engines, e-commerce marketplaces), large fintech/BFSI players, health aggregators, and telecom operators are expected to be notified as SDFs. Organisations should conduct self-assessment against the SDF criteria and begin SDF compliance infrastructure in anticipation.
06

Data Processors & Contractual Obligations

How Data Fiduciaries manage relationships with Data Processors

Data Processors process personal data only on the Data Fiduciary's instructions and do not have direct DPDP Act obligations to Data Principals. However:

The Data Fiduciary remains accountable to the DPB for the Processor's actions
Contracts with Data Processors must be carefully structured to ensure DPDP compliance flows down the supply chain
Data Processors outside India are subject to cross-border transfer Rules
Key clauses in a Data Fiduciary–Processor contract:
  • Processing instructions: Purpose, nature, types of data
  • Security standards: Equivalent to the Fiduciary's
  • Breach notification: To Fiduciary within defined timeframe (to enable Fiduciary's own DPB/Data Principal notification)
  • Audit rights: For the Fiduciary to verify compliance
  • Data handling on termination: Deletion/return of data
  • Sub-processor restrictions: Written approval required
  • Jurisdiction: Indian law preferred for data processed in India
