§1 · Constitutional & Statutory Mandate

The CAG's authority to audit public-sector IT systems

In one paragraph. The Comptroller and Auditor General of India audits every PSU and government department under Articles 148 to 151 of the Constitution and the CAG (Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service) Act, 1971. As public-sector activity has digitised, that audit has migrated from paper vouchers to Information Systems (IS) Audit — examining whether public IT implementations safeguard assets, maintain data integrity and align with business objectives. The audit lens is COBIT 2019 and ISO/IEC 27001:2022, applied through the CAG's three-volume IT Audit Manual and the IS Audit Manual 2024.

The CAG's IS audit is not optional. It is the constitutional accountability mechanism for every rupee processed through a public-sector system. The June 2023 transition into a fully digital audit environment means that a PSU board that has not aligned its IT estate to COBIT 2019 will face audit observations whose remediation is no longer a matter of office procedure but of executive accountability and, under the DPDP Act, statutory penalty.

Authority chain: Article 149 (CAG's duties) → CAG Act 1971 → CAG Auditing Standards 2017 → CAG IT Audit Manual (Vols I–III, 2017) → Manual on Information Systems (IS) Audit 2024 — A Practitioner's Guide. The 2024 Practitioner's Guide formally embeds COBIT 2019 and ISO/IEC 27001:2022 as the assessment frameworks across all IS audit engagements.

The three CAG IS Audit control domains

Every CAG IS audit examines a PSU's IT estate through three domains. Boards should reorganise their existing IT documentation against this structure — every artefact your auditor asks for sits inside one of these three.

Domain 1
Governance Mechanisms
Board IT strategy, risk-management framework, policy library, monitoring of progress against targets, adoption of performance metrics (KPIs, KGIs, CSFs).
Domain 2
General Controls (Environment)
Logical access, information security, change management, disaster recovery and business continuity, third-party sourcing management.
Domain 3
Application Controls
Input validation, processing logic, output verification, master data integrity, end-to-end system flows.

The seven components COBIT looks for in every process

A COBIT-aligned audit does not just ask whether an IT process exists. It asks whether the process is supported by all seven components. Missing any one is an observable gap.

  • Processes — documented activities and practices, not just oral convention
  • Organisational Structures — committees, roles, decision rights
  • Policies and Procedures — written, board-approved, version-controlled
  • Culture, Ethics and Behaviour — the human factor
  • Information — the data, reports and dashboards needed to govern
  • Services, Infrastructure and Applications — the technology stack
  • People, Skills and Competencies — capability to operate the above

COBIT capability maturity — 0 to 5

Every COBIT-aligned IT process is rated on a 0-to-5 scale. A board target of Level 3 (Defined) is the minimum credible posture for a SDF PSU; mission-critical processes should reach Level 4.

§5 · Systems Under Development

SUD & SDLC audit — what the CAG checks during new builds

In one paragraph. The CAG's SUD audit examines new IT projects during design, development and testing. The auditor maintains an objective, observational role — never an internal consultant — so the system remains auditable later. Three thrusts: (i) project-management efficiency, effectiveness and economy; (ii) adequacy of audit trails and internal controls in the system design; (iii) operational controls preventing unauthorised access or processing errors. PSUs should pass these checks before signing off any new go-live.

The CAG checklist for new system builds — board summary

Standing rule: The CAG auditor will not advise the PSU on how to fix a SDLC gap. The auditor observes. Boards that wait for the audit to surface SDLC gaps have already lost the argument with the next CAG report.
§6 · The Unified Control Set

Integrating COBIT 2019 with DPDPA — three anchor objectives

Most PSUs run privacy and IT-audit programmes in parallel. They should run as one. Three COBIT objectives carry the integration — APO14, MEA03 and APO13/DSS05.

APO14 — Managed Data (the data lifecycle)

APO14 manages the enterprise's data assets across the entire lifecycle — creation, delivery, maintenance and archiving. This aligns directly with the DPDPA principles of data minimisation, purpose limitation and storage limitation. PSUs operationalise APO14 by:

  • Database-level metadata management with automatic classification of personal data
  • Dynamic data masking for sensitive identifiers (Aadhaar, PAN, biometrics)
  • Data quality and validation checks built into the database architecture — not the application layer alone
  • Accuracy obligations under Section 8 of the DPDPA satisfied by completeness, correctness and consistency rules
  • Retention and erasure schedules enforced by APO14 controls, not by tickets to the DBA

MEA03 — Managed Compliance with External Requirements

MEA03 addresses how an organisation evaluates its conformance with external laws, regulations and contractual obligations. For a PSU under the DPDPA, MEA03 is the audit-of-the-audit. The MEA03 register should contain:

  • Statutory items — DPDPA notice (Rule 3), Consent Manager interoperability (Rule 4), child consent (Rule 10), grievance redressal (Rule 14)
  • Regular compliance assertions from business and IT process owners — documented, dated and version-controlled
  • Third-party attestations from downstream processors (SOC 2 reports, external data-audit certificates)
  • Contractual clauses requiring processors to maintain the same level of compliance

APO13 + DSS05 — Manage Security and Security Services

APO13 establishes the security framework; DSS05 runs it day-to-day. Both align with the Section 8 obligation to implement reasonable technical and organisational security safeguards. Concrete controls every PSU should be able to demonstrate:

Logical Access
  • Role-Based Access Control with documented least-privilege segregation
  • Database administrative roles separated from business roles
  • Multi-Factor Authentication across all endpoints — including admin consoles
Data Protection
  • AES-256 encryption at rest across databases, file systems and backups
  • TLS 1.3 (or higher) on every network path carrying personal data
  • Full-disk encryption on staff laptops; USB write-block on workstations handling sensitive datasets
Security Event Monitoring
  • System, database and application access logs retained tamper-proof for ≥ 12 months
  • SIEM correlation rules tuned to DPDPA breach criteria
  • Documented playbook to support the 72-hour notification window to the DPBI
§7 · CAG Audit Exhibit

BCPL — the case every PSU board should read

CAG Report No. 2 of 2024 · Ministry of Petroleum & Natural Gas
IS Audit of selected CPSEs — Brahmaputra Cracker and Polymer Limited
The CAG's information-systems audit of the SAP ERP implementation at BCPL produced three findings that read as a checklist of avoidable failure: (i) the organisation had not designed or measured COBIT-aligned Critical Success Factors, Key Performance Indicators or Key Goal Indicators to monitor system performance and security; (ii) default usernames with default passwords persisted in active database instances long after go-live; (iii) multiple critical and conflicting authorisation roles were assigned to single users — a classic failure of segregation of duties.

Under the DPDPA each of these is now penalty-bearing. A board that has not closed them carries both a CAG observation and a potential ₹250 crore exposure.

§10 · Responding to a CAG IT Audit

The audit-response framework — turning observations into action

A CAG observation is not a final verdict. It is the start of a documented dialogue. PSUs that respond with framework-anchored remediation close paras faster and avoid Public Accounts Committee escalation.

The four-step response posture

  • Step 1 — Classification. Every observation is classified by domain (Governance / General / Application), severity (Critical / High / Medium / Low) and traceability to a COBIT objective. Boards that respond domain-by-domain rather than para-by-para signal maturity.
  • Step 2 — CAPA construction. Each observation must carry a Corrective Action and Preventive Action — owner, due date, verification method, evidence catalogue. CAPA is the language the CAG closes paras in.
  • Step 3 — Independent attestation. Where the observation touches a SDF DPDPA obligation, the external independent data auditor under Rule 13 attests the closure. One artefact, two audit responses.
  • Step 4 — Forward instrumentation. Every closed para should be reflected in a KPI on the board-level IT dashboard, so the next CAG visit confirms continued compliance rather than re-opens the observation.
