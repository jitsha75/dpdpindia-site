The PM Gati Shakti Data Governance Gap:
Where GIS Meets DPDP Compliance

India's multi-modal infrastructure master plan aggregates geospatial data from 16+ ministries on a centralised NIC cloud. But under the DPDP Act 2023, the platform faces a critical and unresolved governance gap: who is accountable as the data fiduciary?

Published 27 March 2026
Category DPDP Act · E-Governance
Read time 8 min
Gati Shakti GIS Policy DPDP Act 2023 Data Fiduciary E-Governance NIC Infrastructure

When the PM Gati Shakti National Master Plan was launched in October 2021, it represented a landmark moment in Indian infrastructure governance: for the first time, 16 core infrastructure ministries — Railways, Highways, Power, Telecom, Petroleum, Shipping, Aviation, Water Resources, and others — agreed to feed their geospatial and operational data into a unified, purpose-built platform hosted on the NIC cloud. The promise was clear: coordinated, data-driven infrastructure planning at national scale. Today, as the DPDP Act enters force, that promise is being tested by a structural governance flaw that the architects of the platform may not have fully anticipated.

The Gati Shakti Architecture: Data Without a Fiduciary

The PM Gati Shakti platform operates as an aggregator. Data flows in from multiple sources:

  • ▸ Drone survey data from the SVAMITVA Scheme (Ministry of Panchayati Raj) showing rural abadi boundaries, property demarcation, and land use patterns
  • ▸ Spatial layers from railways, highways, power transmission networks, and telecom infrastructure maintained by sector-specific operators
  • ▸ Geospatial datasets created under the National Geospatial Data Framework, governed by the National Geospatial Policy 2022 (Department of Science & Technology)
  • ▸ Real-time operational feeds that can include location intelligence from government services and departments

The platform was designed with a single, pragmatic objective: enable ministries to visualise, analyse, and coordinate their respective infrastructure plans on a common geospatial canvas. But the DPDP Act — which came into force nearly three years after Gati Shakti's launch — now forces an uncomfortable question that was never formally addressed in the platform's governance framework: When data from multiple fiduciaries converges on a unified platform, who bears the legal responsibility for ensuring that the combined dataset complies with India's data protection obligations?

⚠ Legal Exposure

Under DPDP Act s. 2(i), a Data Fiduciary is defined as "any person, including the State, who alone or in conjunction with others determines the purpose and means of processing of personal data." The definition is broad. But it is also circular in multi-source aggregation contexts: if the NIC hosts the platform, is the NIC the fiduciary? If individual ministries are the sources, are they each fiduciaries? If the DPIIT coordinates the scheme, does accountability attach there? The Act does not yet provide clarity on joint fiduciary responsibility in government platform contexts.

The Multi-Source Problem Under the National Geospatial Policy

The National Geospatial Policy 2022 (issued by the Department of Science & Technology) explicitly categorises geospatial datasets into three tiers: Open (freely available), Restricted (available under specified conditions), and Confidential (not publicly available). The policy established the National Geospatial Data Framework (NGDF) to manage this categorisation and to promote responsible data sharing.

But here is where Gati Shakti's architecture creates vulnerability. The platform ingests data across all three categories — some datasets are geographically precise down to individual properties, others reveal infrastructure vulnerabilities, still others contain sensitive operational intelligence. Once aggregated:

  • Q Can the classification tier change when data is integrated with other datasets? For example, if residential property boundaries (from SVAMITVA) are combined with railway infrastructure data, does the combined layer trigger a reclassification to "Restricted" or "Confidential"?
  • Q Who has authority to reclassify an open dataset once it has been federated into a platform that holds restricted or confidential data?
  • Q Under the DPDP Act s. 5 (purpose limitation), if drone survey data was collected for property tax assessment, is it compliant to use the same data for infrastructure planning on a national scale? Does a fresh consent or statutory basis need to be established?
  • Q If a data breach affects Gati Shakti, whose responsibility is it to notify data principals under DPDP Act s. 8(6)? The originating ministry? The platform operator (NIC)? The nodal ministry (DPIIT) that oversees the scheme?
📌 Design Flaw

The Gati Shakti platform was architected as a data aggregator, not a data governance platform. The technology enables integration and visualisation; the governance architecture does not clearly assign fiduciary responsibility when data from multiple sources converges. This gap is now material under the DPDP Act.

The Drone Rules and the SVAMITVA Data Chain

The Ministry of Civil Aviation's Drone Rules 2021 have liberalised aerial survey data collection. The SVAMITVA Scheme (Pradhan Mantri Scheme for Formalization of Unlisted Workers), administered by the Ministry of Panchayati Raj, uses drone-based surveys to map rural abadi (settlement) boundaries and create verifiable land ownership records for rural property holders.

SVAMITVA data is now flowing into Gati Shakti as a source layer. This creates a specific compliance chain that is worth examining:

  • § Data Collection. Drone surveys collect high-resolution aerial imagery that can identify individual properties, land use, and settlement patterns. This imagery is inherently location-linked and inferable to individuals under the DPDP Act definition of personal data.
  • § Processing and Storage. The Ministry of Panchayati Raj processes this imagery to create abadi boundary maps and property records, which are stored on designated systems. The Ministry is, in this context, a data fiduciary under DPDP Act s. 2(i).
  • § Sharing to Gati Shakti. Processed SVAMITVA layers are shared with Gati Shakti for national infrastructure visualisation. At this point, the NIC (or the platform operator) becomes a joint processor or secondary fiduciary — but the exact legal classification remains undefined.

The Drone Rules 2021 do not explicitly address the question of data sharing across government schemes or the continuation of data fiduciary obligations once a dataset leaves its originating system. The vacuum is now filled by the DPDP Act — but the Act itself does not yet have published government guidance on how to apply its fiduciary framework in inter-ministry data aggregation scenarios.

Critical Infrastructure, IT Act Obligations, and CERT-In Compliance

The IT Act 2000, s. 70, defines Protected Systems as "computer resources or communication devices connected to a computer resource of the critical information infrastructure." The scope of this definition may extend to Gati Shakti, especially if the platform hosts infrastructure-critical data (power grid coordinates, pipeline routes, telecommunications backbone).

If Gati Shakti is designated (or could be argued to be) a protected system, then IT Act compliance obligations become binding:

  • ▸ CERT-In Directions 2022. Mandatory incident reporting within 6 hours of discovering a security breach; maintenance of logs for 180 days; deployment of intrusion detection systems (IDS) and regular vulnerability assessments.
  • ▸ IT Act s. 43A. Liability of data fiduciaries for failure to maintain reasonable security measures. This now overlaps with DPDP Act s. 8 (Security Safeguards).
  • ▸ Cross-ministry breach cascade risk. If a breach in Gati Shakti exposes shared data, all fiduciaries whose data is on the platform may face concurrent liability under both the IT Act and the DPDP Act.

The current platform governance does not appear to have formally addressed whether it qualifies as a Protected System under s. 70, or whether it has implemented the specific technical and procedural safeguards required by the CERT-In Directions.

"When 16 ministries converge their data on a centralised platform without a clear fiduciary framework, a data breach becomes not one ministry's problem — it becomes a national incident."

Justice Puttaswamy and the Precedent for Aggregated State Data

In Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017) 10 SCC 1, the Supreme Court recognised that privacy is a fundamental right — and it is a right that attaches to individuals' interactions with the State. While the Court was focused on Aadhaar, the reasoning extends directly to large-scale government data aggregation:

The collection, collation, retention, analysis and publication of personal information by the State constitutes an invasion of privacy unless authorised by valid legislation which prescribes safeguards, including principles of purpose limitation, data minimisation, and security.

The implications for Gati Shakti are stark. The platform aggregates personal or inferable data (location-linked, property-linked, infrastructure-sensitive information) collected under diverse statutory authorities (SVAMITVA, the Drone Rules, sector-specific legislation). Unless the platform has been formally constituted under legislative or executive authority that explicitly establishes data protection safeguards, the Court's holding in Puttaswamy suggests the platform may be vulnerable to legal challenge on constitutional privacy grounds — independently of whether it complies with the DPDP Act.

The DPDP-Compliant Governance Framework: What Needs to Happen

Bringing Gati Shakti into full DPDP compliance requires a structural reassessment of its data governance architecture. The platform does not need to be dismantled — but it does need to be rebuilt with explicit fiduciary accountability. Here is what a compliant framework would require:

  • ✓ Formal Fiduciary Designation. A single entity — whether the NIC, a dedicated authority, or a consortium of source ministries — must be formally designated as the data fiduciary for Gati Shakti. This designation must be published in the gazette and must define the fiduciary's obligations under DPDP Act ss. 6–8.
  • ✓ Data Stewardship Agreements. Each source ministry must enter into a formal Data Stewardship Agreement with the fiduciary, clearly defining purpose, scope, retention, and access controls for each dataset. These agreements must be DPDP-compliant and must be reviewed by legal teams.
  • ✓ Purpose-Bound Access Controls. The platform must implement granular, role-based access controls so that a ministry accessing the platform for its sector (e.g., railways) cannot by default view sensitive data from another sector. This is essential under DPDP Act s. 5 (purpose limitation).
  • ✓ Audit Logging and Breach Readiness. The platform must maintain comprehensive audit logs of all data access, queries, and exports. In the event of a breach, the designated fiduciary must have the capability to notify all affected data principals within 72 hours (as required by DPDP Act s. 8(6) and CERT-In Directions 2022).
  • ✓ Statutory Transparency Report. The fiduciary must publish an annual transparency report detailing data flows, access requests, breaches, and compliance with security safeguards. This is not required by the DPDP Act, but it is essential for constitutional accountability under Puttaswamy.
💡 Implementation Priority

The most urgent gap to close is fiduciary designation and responsibility assignment. Without clarity on who is accountable for what, the entire platform's compliance posture is legally uncertain. This should be addressed through amendment to the Gati Shakti Governance Framework and formal publication of the fiduciary responsibilities under section 8 of the DPDP Act.

The Central Problem

PM Gati Shakti was built to solve a coordination problem: how to break down silos and enable infrastructure ministries to see, plan, and coordinate at national scale. It is a powerful concept. But the DPDP Act has revealed that coordination without clear fiduciary responsibility is now a legal liability, not a policy achievement.

"The question is no longer how to aggregate data faster. It is how to aggregate it responsibly."
