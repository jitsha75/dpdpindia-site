Data Fiduciaries — Obligations & Compliance

Every organisation that determines the purpose and means of processing personal data is a Data Fiduciary under the DPDP Act 2023. This spoke covers the full spectrum of Fiduciary obligations — from notice and consent to security, breach notification, children's data, and the enhanced requirements for Significant Data Fiduciaries.

01

Definition & Scope — Section 2(i)

Who qualifies as a Data Fiduciary and the trust-law rationale

Under s. 2(i) of the DPDP Act 2023, a Data Fiduciary means "any person who alone or in conjunction with other persons determines the purpose and means of processing of personal data." The term encompasses any individual, company, firm, State instrumentality, association of persons, or body of individuals.

The concept was pioneered in the Srikrishna Committee Report (2018) and deliberately borrows from trust law: a fiduciary is someone who holds power over another's interests and is bound to exercise that power in the interest of the beneficiary. By calling data controllers "fiduciaries," the Act signals a heightened duty of care — not merely compliance with rules, but a genuine obligation to act in the Data Principal's interest.

Controller vs Fiduciary: The GDPR term is "data controller" — implying authority to control. The DPDP Act's term "Data Fiduciary" implies a duty of trust toward the Data Principal. Legally, this distinction matters: a fiduciary duty requires acting in the interest of the beneficiary, not merely complying with rules. Whether Indian courts will enforce this fiduciary duty independently of the Act's specific obligations remains to be seen.

The definition turns on purpose and means determination. An entity that merely processes data on instructions from another — without autonomy to decide why or how — is a Data Processor, not a Fiduciary. Many outsourcing arrangements and cloud service providers therefore qualify only as Data Processors.

General Obligations — Section 8

The core compliance framework for every Data Fiduciary

  • s. 8(1)Every Data Fiduciary must process personal data only in accordance with the Act and for lawful purposes — whether based on consent or legitimate use.
  • s. 8(3)Reasonable efforts must be made to ensure that personal data is accurate and complete — particularly where inaccuracy may harm the Data Principal.
  • s. 8(4)Data minimisation: Only such personal data as is necessary for the specified purpose may be collected. This implements the proportionality principle from Puttaswamy (2017).
  • s. 8(5)Security safeguards: Appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure the security of personal data — including measures to prevent unauthorised access, use, disclosure, alteration or destruction.
  • s. 8(6)Breach notification: In the event of a personal data breach, notify the Data Protection Board of India and affected Data Principals in the manner prescribed.
  • s. 8(7)Storage limitation / erasure duty: Upon the purpose for which data was collected being satisfied, or upon withdrawal of consent, the Fiduciary must erase the personal data (and cause Data Processors to erase it) — unless retention is required by law.
  • s. 8(8)The Fiduciary must publish the contact details of a Data Protection Officer (if SDF) or an appropriate person for the exercise of Data Principal rights and for grievance redressal.
  • s. 8(9)The Fiduciary must establish an effective grievance redressal mechanism, enabling Data Principals to register complaints and receive responses within prescribed timelines.
Accountability for Processors: A Data Fiduciary is accountable for the acts of its Data Processors — an agreement between Fiduciary and Processor does not transfer liability to the Processor as far as the Data Principal or DPBI is concerned. The Fiduciary must ensure Processors comply with all applicable obligations.
03

Notice & Consent Duties

Sections 5–6 — the pre-processing obligations

Before processing personal data on the basis of consent, a Data Fiduciary must provide a notice (s. 5) to the Data Principal containing:

  • The personal data to be processed and the specified purpose of processing.
  • The manner in which the Data Principal may exercise their rights under the Act.
  • The manner in which the Data Principal may make a complaint to the DPBI.

Notices must be in English or any language specified in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution. The DPDP Rules 2025 prescribe the format and linguistic requirements — notices must be in clear, plain language accessible to the average person, not legalese.

Consent (s. 6) must be:

  • Free — not a precondition for an otherwise available service (where the data is not necessary for that service).
  • Specific — tied to a particular purpose, not blanket.
  • Informed — preceded by a compliant notice.
  • Unconditional — not bundled with unrelated consents.
  • Unambiguous — signified by a clear affirmative action. Pre-ticked boxes, silence, or inaction do not constitute consent.
Existing data — Transitional consent: For personal data already collected before the DPDP Act came into force, Data Fiduciaries must provide a notice to Data Principals describing the processing activities and the right to withdraw consent. Processing may continue until the Principal withdraws consent or the retention period expires.
04

Personal Data Breach Notification — Section 8(6)

Mandatory notification to the DPBI and affected Data Principals

A personal data breach under the Act means any unauthorised processing of personal data, or accidental disclosure, acquisition, sharing, use, alteration, destruction, or loss of access to personal data that compromises its confidentiality, integrity, or availability.

Notification obligations:

  • Upon becoming aware of a personal data breach, the Data Fiduciary must notify the Data Protection Board of India in the manner and within the time prescribed by the DPDP Rules 2025.
  • Notification must also be provided to each affected Data Principal whose data has been breached.
  • The notification must include: nature of the breach, the personal data affected, the number of Data Principals affected, likely consequences, and remediation steps taken or proposed.
Penalty for non-notification: Failure to notify the DPBI or affected Data Principals of a breach attracts a financial penalty of up to ₹200 crore under s. 33(2). This is among the highest penalties in the Act — reflecting the legislative view that concealing breaches is particularly egregious.

The DPDP Rules 2025 prescribe a 72-hour initial notification window to the DPBI (matching the GDPR standard), followed by a more detailed notification within a further prescribed period. Data Principals must be notified without delay, in the manner prescribed — which may be through the Consent Manager where applicable.

Significant Data Fiduciaries — Section 10

Enhanced obligations for high-risk, high-volume processors

The Central Government may notify any Data Fiduciary or class of Fiduciaries as Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs) based on: volume and sensitivity of personal data processed; risk to Data Principals; potential impact on sovereignty and integrity of India; risk to electoral democracy; security of the State; and public order.

Data Protection Officer

SDFs must appoint a DPO resident in India, who reports to the board of directors (not legal or compliance department). The DPO is the primary point of contact for Data Principals and the DPBI. s. 10(a)

Independent Data Auditor

SDFs must have their processing activities audited by an independent data auditor to evaluate compliance with the Act. Audit reports must be maintained and may be sought by the DPBI. s. 10(b)

Data Protection Impact Assessment

Before undertaking processing activities that carry a high risk to Data Principals' rights, SDFs must conduct a DPIA — evaluating risks and mitigating measures — in the format prescribed by the DPDP Rules 2025. s. 10(c)

Any Other Measures Prescribed

The DPDP Rules 2025 and future Rules may prescribe additional obligations for SDFs — including algorithmic transparency, interoperability requirements, or enhanced data minimisation standards. s. 10(d)

Who will be notified as SDF? MeitY has indicated that large social media platforms, major e-commerce entities, health data processors, and financial services companies are likely to be notified as SDFs. The threshold is expected to be based on a combination of user count (likely 5 crore+ registered Indian users) and data sensitivity.

Government Exemptions — Section 17 (Tiered Structure)

Not a blanket carve-out — a three-tier notification-based framework

A common misconception is that the government — as a whole — is exempt from the DPDP Act. This is incorrect. Section 17 establishes a tiered, notification-dependent exemption framework. No government body is automatically exempt; exemptions must be specifically granted by Central Government notification.

Three Tiers of Government Treatment

Tier 1 · Full Exemption
Notified security & intelligence instrumentalities — s. 17(1)
The Central Government may, by notification, exempt specific instrumentalities of the State from all or any provisions of the Act, in the interest of: sovereignty and integrity of India; security of the State; friendly relations with foreign States; maintenance of public order; or prevention of incitement to cognisable offences. This tier covers national security agencies, foreign intelligence bodies, and counter-terrorism units. Exemptions are not automatic — each entity must be individually notified. No such notifications had been issued as of early 2026.
Tier 2 · Partial Relaxation
State bodies processing data for welfare & public services — s. 17(4) read with s. 7
State instrumentalities processing personal data for the delivery of subsidies, benefits, licences, certificates, or other services under law may use the "Legitimate Uses" ground under s. 7 — processing without individual consent where it is necessary for lawful State functions. Additionally, the Central Government may grant limited relief from the storage limitation (erasure) obligation and certain correction rights for such bodies, provided the data is not used for decision-making that adversely affects the Data Principal.
Tier 3 · Full Obligations
Commercial & service-oriented public bodies — full Data Fiduciary
State-owned entities that process personal data for commercial, insurance, healthcare, energy supply, or employment purposes are Data Fiduciaries in full. They determine the purpose and means of processing — and the DPDP Act imposes all Fiduciary obligations on them. The fact that the State owns or controls the entity is irrelevant; what matters is the nature of the processing activity.
Key principle: Classification depends on what data is processed and for what purpose — not on who owns the organisation. A single large public sector body may simultaneously be a Tier 1 candidate (if it has national security functions) and a Tier 3 Fiduciary (for its commercial or HR operations) — with different obligations applying to each category of processing.

Which Public Bodies Are Subject to the Full Act?

The following categories of public-sector entities are Data Fiduciaries subject to the full DPDP Act framework — unless specifically notified otherwise:

  • Social insurance & welfare corporations — bodies administering employee health insurance, provident funds, pension schemes, and social security. They process vast volumes of health, financial, and biometric data for millions of beneficiaries.
  • Public sector insurance entities — life, general, and health insurance bodies owned by the State. Processing of policyholder data (financial details, medical history, nominee information) falls squarely within Fiduciary obligations.
  • State-owned energy & utility companies — refiners, distributors, and retailers of fuel, gas, and electricity who process customer billing, location, and consumption data at scale.
  • Government hospitals & apex medical institutions — process highly sensitive health data. Must implement security safeguards, respond to Data Principal rights requests within timelines prescribed in the DPDP Rules 2025, and may process emergency health data without consent under s. 7(b) — but this does not exempt them from the Act's overall framework.
  • Public sector banks & financial institutions — RBI-regulated entities processing payment, credit, and KYC data. Already subject to RBI data localisation and security norms, which must now be read alongside DPDP obligations.
Compliance signal from public sector: Several public sector bodies have already begun internal DPDP alignment — issuing internal advisories requiring officers to access personal data on a "need-to-know" basis and mandating breach reporting to their IT security divisions. This confirms these bodies understand they are subject to — not exempt from — the Act.
Constitutional constraint on all exemptions: Any exemption under s. 17 must satisfy the proportionality test from Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) — legality, legitimate aim, and proportionality. A blanket exemption for an agency whose activities go beyond national security into routine data processing would be constitutionally vulnerable. Challenges under Articles 32 and 226 are anticipated once enforcement begins.

Exemption Comparison — Security vs Service Bodies

Obligation matrix across the two principal categories of government Data Fiduciaries

Obligation Tier 1 — Notified Security & Intelligence Bodies Tier 3 — Commercial / Service Public Bodies
Exemption basis s. 17(1) — National security / public order grounds. Must be specifically notified by Central Government. None — full Data Fiduciary obligations apply. s. 7 Legitimate Uses may substitute for consent.
Notice & Consent Generally exempt once notified. May process without notice or consent for classified operations. Mandatory. Must provide notice before processing and obtain consent — or rely on an enumerated s. 7 legitimate use ground.
Security safeguards Must still maintain appropriate security measures to prevent unauthorised access and breaches — exemption from the Act does not remove security obligations under other laws. Fully mandatory under s. 8(5). Failure attracts penalties up to ₹250 crore.
Data Principal rights (access, correction, erasure) Generally restricted or excluded for security-sensitive processing once notified. Full rights apply. Data Principals may request information, correction, and erasure within prescribed timelines (DPDP Rules 2025).
Breach notification Classified breach procedures under other security legislation apply. DPBI notification may be excluded by notification. Mandatory — notify DPBI and affected Data Principals. Failure attracts up to ₹200 crore penalty.
DPBI oversight Limited or excluded for notified entities. Parliamentary oversight mechanisms (committees, RTI) may apply instead. Full DPBI oversight. Can receive complaints from Data Principals, conduct inquiries, and impose penalties.
Storage limitation / erasure Exempt from s. 8(7) erasure obligations under national security grounds. Generally applies. Partial relaxation available under s. 17(4) for welfare processing not used in individual decision-making.
Significant Data Fiduciary designation Unlikely — exempted bodies fall outside the SDF framework. Possible, based on volume, sensitivity, and national importance of data processed. Enhanced obligations (DPO, DPIA, audit) would then apply.
