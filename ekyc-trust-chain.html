01

The Aadhaar Ecosystem as a Trust Chain

Each tier has a distinct legal boundary — no single point of failure can compromise the national database

To understand why the eKYC entities are structured the way they are, it helps to look at the Aadhaar ecosystem as a Trust Chain. Each entity occupies a specific legal and technical tier, with carefully defined boundaries between them. The design goal is simple but critical: no single point of failure should be capable of compromising the entire national biometric database — a database holding the identity records of 1.4 billion people.

The Trust Chain is not merely an architectural convenience. It is a deliberate regulatory instrument. Each boundary is backed by a specific regulation, a contractual obligation, and a technical control — so that even if one tier is breached, the attacker gains only what that tier was permitted to hold, and no more.

Tier 0 — Root
UIDAI / CIDR
Central Authority

Holds the master biometric database. Issues all licences. The only entity that can decrypt biometric data or perform identity matching. Cannot be bypassed at any step.

ASA / KSA

Design principle: Each downward boundary in the Trust Chain is a one-way information valve — data flows from UIDAI outward in the minimum amount needed by each tier, encrypted specifically for that tier. No tier can escalate its own privileges to access the data held by the tier above it.
02

Differentiation of Functions: ASA vs AUA vs KUA

Three entities, three distinct roles, three fundamentally different data boundaries

Entity Primary Function Data Handled Real-World Analogy
ASA Infrastructure & Connectivity. Acts as a secure "pipe" between Requesting Entities and UIDAI. Maintains dedicated leased-line access to CIDR, distributes cryptographic keys to its KUAs, and forwards encrypted packets without any ability to inspect their contents. Encrypted packets only. The ASA cannot see, decrypt, or store any biometric or KYC data. To the ASA, every transaction is an opaque ciphertext envelope addressed to UIDAI.
📡Like an ISP or a secure VPN tunnel — it carries the message at high speed with guaranteed delivery, but cannot read what is inside.
 The Internet Service Provider or the secure tunnel. A bonded courier van between the requestor and the vault — the driver has no key to the package.
AUA Identity Verification (Yes / No). Asks UIDAI: "Is this person who they claim to be?" Uses the Aadhaar Authentication API. Receives only a binary pass/fail response plus a reference ID — no demographic data is returned. Auth response only. A Y (verified) or N (failed) and an error code. No name, no address, no photo, no date of birth.
🛡️The Security Guard at a gate who checks an ID — knows "this person is on the list" but does not record the visitor's home address.
 An attendance terminal, a building access system, or a utility verifying account ownership — it needs only a "yes, this person is genuine," nothing more.
KUA Data Retrieval (e-KYC). Asks UIDAI: "Please provide the verified demographic details of this person." Uses the eKYC API. Receives a digitally signed KYC record encrypted specifically for the KUA's public key. Demographic data. Name, Date of Birth, Gender, Address, Photograph (JPEG), and optionally hashed Mobile and Email — as a UIDAI-signed XML with legal equivalence to an Aadhaar letter.
📋The Registration Desk filling out a form for you — the bank's KYC desk that completes an account-opening form using verified government records.
 A bank opening an account, a telecom registering a SIM, or an insurer onboarding a policyholder — entities with a regulatory obligation to collect and verify full identity details.
Key distinction: A KUA is always also an AUA — it can perform both plain authentication and eKYC. But an AUA is not automatically a KUA. A business must apply specifically for KUA status and demonstrate a legitimate need to receive demographic data. UIDAI's approval is required for each use case category.
03

Why Are They Segregated?

Three regulatory rationales behind UIDAI's layered entity architecture

The segregation of ASA, AUA, and KUA is not an accident of implementation — it is deliberate policy design. UIDAI enforces this structure for three core reasons, each backed by both a legal and a technical rationale.

01 / Security
Reducing the Attack Surface

If every bank, insurer, and local agency connected directly to UIDAI, there would be thousands of independent entry points into the national identity infrastructure — each a potential target.

By requiring all traffic to route through a small number of high-security, dedicated ASAs on registered leased lines, UIDAI reduces the "gateways" it must defend to a manageable few. The ASA model converts a thousand potential attack surfaces into a handful of hardened choke points — what information-security professionals call a Security Perimeter design.

02 / Compliance
Specialisation of Regulatory Duty

Each tier is responsible for a fundamentally different compliance domain:

  • ASAs focus on 24×7 network uptime, hardware security (HSM modules), leased-line SLAs, and physical infrastructure protection.
  • AUAs and KUAs focus on user consent management, purpose limitation, the legal right to request identity verification, and the handling of data they receive.

Combining both responsibilities in one entity would create conflicts of interest and dilute accountability when something goes wrong.

03 / Privacy
Data Minimisation by Design

By separating AUA (Yes/No) from KUA (full demographic data), UIDAI structurally enforces data minimisation — the principle that organisations should only access the minimum personal data necessary for their specific purpose.

A simple attendance system only needs "is this the right person?" — it has no legitimate need for the employee's home address. The AUA model makes it technically impossible for such a system to receive that data, even if the operator wanted it. The law says no, and the architecture enforces it.

04

Appointing a Sub-KUA — The Regulatory Process

Permitted with strict prior UIDAI approval and multiple mandatory compliance gates

A KUA (for example, a bank) may permit a third-party entity — an insurer, a fintech partner, or a specific department — to use its Aadhaar integration infrastructure. That third party is designated as a Sub-KUA. Yes, a KUA can do this — but the regulatory bar is high and the process is detailed.

Legal basis: Regulation 14(1)(ga) of the Aadhaar (Authentication and Offline Verification) Regulations, 2021, reinforced by Circular 2 of 2025 and Circular 1 of 2026, requires a KUA to obtain prior approval from UIDAI before appointing any third-party entity as a Sub-KUA. There is no self-declaration or post-facto registration — approval must precede any live Sub-KUA transaction.
1
Joint Undertaking — Shared Liability

Both the KUA (e.g., the bank) and the proposed Sub-KUA must sign a Joint Undertaking before submitting an application to UIDAI. This document holds both parties jointly and severally liable for any data breach, misuse, or regulatory violation arising from the Sub-KUA's use of the eKYC infrastructure.

The Joint Undertaking is not a formality — UIDAI may proceed against either party independently in the event of a breach. The KUA cannot escape liability by pointing to the Sub-KUA as the responsible party.

Implication: A bank must conduct thorough technical and legal due diligence on any proposed Sub-KUA before signing, because it will bear legal responsibility for the Sub-KUA's data-handling practices going forward.
2
Formal Application & Licence Fee

The KUA submits a formal application to UIDAI for Sub-KUA approval, accompanied by the signed Joint Undertaking and documentation describing the Sub-KUA's business case, intended use of eKYC data, and data-handling architecture.

As of 2026, a Licence Fee — recently revised upward under UIDAI circulars — is payable for each Sub-KUA appointment. The fee structure distinguishes between Sub-KUA categories based on transaction volume and sector.

Note: UIDAI's fee schedule is updated periodically. KUAs must verify the current fee on the UIDAI ecosystem portal before submitting, as incorrect fees may result in the application being returned.
3
IS Audit by CERT-IN Empanelled Auditor

Before the Sub-KUA can go live, it must undergo an Information Security (IS) Audit conducted by a CERT-IN empanelled auditor. This gate is mandatory — no waiver is available.

The audit must verify, at minimum:

  • The Sub-KUA's systems do not store biometric data at any point in the transaction flow.
  • Application and system logs do not record Aadhaar numbers, biometric templates, OTP values, or demographic KYC fields.
  • The Sub-KUA maintains an Aadhaar Data Vault (ADV) meeting UIDAI's AES-256 and HSM requirements.
  • Network and access controls are adequate to prevent unauthorised access to eKYC data in transit and at rest.
  • Incident response procedures are documented and tested.
Practical note: Audit preparation typically takes 4–8 weeks for an organisation new to the Aadhaar compliance framework. Findings must be remediated and re-verified before UIDAI will issue a Sub-KUA code. Plan accordingly.
4
UIDAI Issues Unique Sub-KUA Code

Once UIDAI is satisfied — application approved, fee paid, IS audit passed — it issues a unique Sub-KUA code to the newly registered entity.

Under Circular 1 of 2026, every eKYC transaction initiated by a Sub-KUA must carry this unique code in the sa attribute of the Auth XML. This gives UIDAI the ability to track, at the individual transaction level, exactly which sub-entity initiated each request — creating a complete, auditable chain of accountability from resident through to CIDR.

Audit trail: UIDAI's public ASA transaction dashboard allows authorised parties to view Sub-KUA-level transaction volumes, providing ecosystem-wide visibility into how each Sub-KUA licence is being used.
5
Ongoing Obligations Post-Appointment

Sub-KUA appointment is not a one-time event. Ongoing obligations apply to both the Sub-KUA and its parent KUA:

  • Periodic IS re-audits at intervals specified by UIDAI.
  • Reporting of any security incidents involving eKYC data to UIDAI within prescribed timelines.
  • Compliance with any revised circulars or regulations — which supersede earlier guidance automatically.
  • The KUA must notify UIDAI of any material change in the Sub-KUA's business, ownership, or technical architecture.
  • If the Sub-KUA relationship is terminated, the KUA must inform UIDAI and revoke the Sub-KUA's infrastructure access immediately.

Development Companies — When Does Sub-KUA Registration Apply?

The crucial distinction between writing the code and processing the transactions

This is one of the most frequently misunderstood points in the eKYC ecosystem. Whether a development or technology company requires Sub-KUA registration depends entirely on where the application runs and who controls the transaction pipeline — not on who wrote the code.

⚠ Important — The Hosting and Processing Distinction

A development company that is solely writing code for a bank does not need Sub-KUA status. If the bank's own servers receive and process the eKYC transactions — and the development company merely delivered the software — no Sub-KUA registration is required for the developer.

However, if that same company hosts the application on its own servers and those servers receive, process, or relay eKYC transactions for the bank's customers — even as a managed service or SaaS platform — then the development company is functionally acting as a Sub-KUA and must be registered as one before processing any live transaction.

✓ Sub-KUA Registration NOT Required
  • Dev company builds the eKYC integration module and delivers it to the bank.
  • The module runs on the bank's own infrastructure (on-premises or bank-controlled cloud).
  • The bank's servers receive all eKYC traffic from UIDAI directly.
  • The dev company has no access to live transaction data.
  • Updates and maintenance are delivered as code releases, not through direct system access.
✗ Sub-KUA Registration Required
  • Dev company hosts the eKYC application on its own servers (cloud or on-premises).
  • The company's servers receive, process, or relay eKYC transactions for the bank's customers.
  • Even if it is a "pass-through" or "relay" arrangement — traffic flows through the company's infrastructure.
  • The company has operational visibility into transaction data in transit.
  • The company could technically inspect, log, or intercept eKYC data from its position in the pipeline.
The test to apply: At any point in the transaction lifecycle, does the company's infrastructure sit in the data path between the end user's device and UIDAI's CIDR — or between CIDR and the KUA? If yes, even briefly, that company is processing eKYC data and requires Sub-KUA registration before going live.
Non-compliance consequence: Operating as an unregistered Sub-KUA — even unintentionally — is a violation of the Aadhaar Act 2016 and the Authentication Regulations 2021. UIDAI may suspend the parent KUA's licence, impose financial penalties, and refer the matter for prosecution under Section 40 of the Act (imprisonment up to 3 years and/or fine up to ₹10 lakh).
