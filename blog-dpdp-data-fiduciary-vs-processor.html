Data Fiduciary vs Data Processor:
The Accountability Gap India Must Close

When a cloud vendor breaches, who is liable? The DPDP Act is unambiguous: the data fiduciary. But the contractual architecture between fiduciary and processor remains a minefield for Indian organizations.

Published 27 March 2026
Category DPDP Act · Data Governance
Read time 7 min
DPDP Act 2023 Data Fiduciary Data Processor Accountability IT Act DPA

The definitions matter more than the labels. A Data Fiduciary is accountable; a Data Processor executes on behalf of the fiduciary. But this division of labor does not divide liability. Under DPDP Act §8(2), the fiduciary remains liable even when the processor fails. This creates an accountability gap that most Indian organizations are not yet addressing in their cloud contracts.

Statutory Definitions: What the Act Actually Says

Section 2(i) defines the Data Fiduciary:

📌 DPDP Act §2(i)

"The person who alone, or jointly with others, determines the purposes and means of processing of personal data."

Section 2(k) defines the Data Processor:

📌 DPDP Act §2(k)

"The person who processes personal data on behalf of the data fiduciary."

The distinction is clean: A fiduciary decides what data to collect and why. A processor does the technical work of storing, accessing, and managing that data. The fiduciary owns the decision; the processor owns the execution.

But here is the critical part that most contracts miss: the processor's actions bind the fiduciary. When the processor fails to implement security controls, or retains data beyond the specified period, or discloses data to unauthorized third parties, the fiduciary is not relieved of liability. The Act imposes the breach on the fiduciary's shoulders, not the processor's.

Fiduciary Liability Under §8: The Accountability Gap

Section 8(1) establishes the fiduciary's obligations when processing personal data:

  • ✓ Process data only for the specified purpose (purpose limitation)
  • ✓ Retain data only as long as necessary (storage limitation)
  • ✓ Implement security measures commensurate with the sensitivity of data
  • ✓ Provide transparency to data principals about processing
  • ✓ Enable data principals to exercise their rights (access, correction, deletion)

Now read Section 8(2):

⚠ DPDP Act §8(2)

"Where a data fiduciary engages a data processor, the fiduciary shall ensure that the processor complies with the obligations specified in this section."

This is the accountability gap. The fiduciary does not outsource its legal responsibility. The fiduciary is responsible for ensuring the processor complies. If the processor fails, the fiduciary is liable — unless it can demonstrate it took all reasonable steps to ensure processor compliance.

This is stricter than many organizations realize. It is not enough to hire a certified processor or sign a contract. The fiduciary must maintain ongoing oversight, audit processor systems, and respond when processor performance degrades.

Special Case: Children's Data Under §9

Section 9 imposes heightened obligations when processing children's personal data. The fiduciary's responsibility is even more stringent:

  • ✓ Reasonable steps must be taken to verify the consent of a parent or guardian. A checkbox from someone claiming to be a parent is not reasonable.
  • ✓ Children's data must not be shared with third parties except under specific exemptions (e.g., legal requirement, safety).
  • ✓ If processing is delegated to a processor, the fiduciary must verify the processor has measures in place appropriate for children's data protection.

Under §9, delegating children's data processing to a processor does not reduce the fiduciary's obligation to verify that the processor has appropriate safeguards in place. A generic Data Processing Agreement (DPA) is not enough.

"The fiduciary does not outsource liability. It only outsources execution. The law keeps the fiduciary accountable for what the processor does."

IT Act Legacy: §43A and Pre-DPDP Liability

Before the DPDP Act took effect, data security obligations were governed by the Information Technology Act 2000, Section 43A, and the IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules 2011. These rules remain operative until superseded by DPDP Rules.

Section 43A imposes liability on "body corporates" (organizations) for failure to implement reasonable security practices. Critically, a body corporate is liable even if the security failure occurred at a vendor or processor level, provided the body corporate failed to exercise reasonable care in selecting and monitoring the vendor.

The DPDP Act does not eliminate IT Act §43A liability — it layers on top of it. A data breach can trigger liability under both Acts simultaneously:

  • ₹ DPDP Act penalty: Up to ₹100 crore for breach of fiduciary security obligations
  • ₹ IT Act §43A penalty: Compensation for loss/damage suffered by affected individuals (no statutory cap, but typically assessed at ₹100,000 to ₹1,000,000 per person)
  • ₹ Cumulative exposure: Depending on breach size, easily exceeds ₹200-300 crore

The IT Rules also require breach notification. Unless your contract with your processor explicitly includes breach notification obligations and timelines, you will violate the notification requirement when your processor discovers a breach.

Building Contractual Architecture: Data Processing Agreements

This is where most Indian organizations are weak. A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) is the legal mechanism by which a fiduciary delegates processing to a processor while maintaining accountability.

A compliant DPDP DPA must specify:

  • ✓ Processing scope: Exactly what data categories, purposes, retention periods, and processing activities the processor may perform.
  • ✓ Security obligations: Specific technical and organizational measures the processor must implement (encryption, access control, audit logs, incident response).
  • ✓ Breach notification: Processor must notify fiduciary immediately (within 24-48 hours) of any suspected or actual breach.
  • ✓ Sub-processor clause: Processor cannot delegate to further sub-processors without explicit written approval from the fiduciary.
  • ✓ Audit rights: Fiduciary has the right to audit processor systems, request security assessments, and access processor logs.
  • ✓ Data deletion: On termination of the contract, processor must delete or return all data within 30-90 days.
  • ✓ Liability and indemnity: Processor indemnifies fiduciary for losses arising from processor's breach of DPA.
⚠ Vendor Pushback

Cloud vendors often resist fiduciary audit rights, claiming "security through obscurity." This is unacceptable under DPDP. If a processor will not allow audit, the processor is not DPDP-compliant. The fiduciary cannot delegate to a processor it cannot verify.

Real-World Scenarios: When the Gap Opens

Here are scenarios where the accountability gap becomes concrete liability:

Scenario 1: Cloud Vendor Breach

Your SaaS processor (e.g., a payroll or CRM vendor) experiences a breach affecting 10,000 employee records. The processor's audit logs show the breach occurred six months ago but was not disclosed to you. You are not liable for the vendor's failure to detect the breach — but you are liable for not having contractual audit rights that would have enabled discovery. Fiduciary liability: DPDP §8(2) breach, IT Act §43A liability.

Scenario 2: Unauthorized Sub-processor

Your cloud processor (e.g., AWS) uses a third-party analytics vendor to process data within your environment without your explicit approval. The sub-processor is located in a jurisdiction with lax data protection laws. You are not liable for the sub-processor's processing — but you are liable for not having a sub-processor control clause in your DPA. Fiduciary liability: Breach of fiduciary obligation under §8(1).

Scenario 3: Data Retention Violation

Your processor retains backup copies of deleted customer data beyond the retention period specified in your contract. When a data principal requests deletion, the principal's data still exists in backup systems. The processor failed, but you failed to implement a deletion verification mechanism. Fiduciary liability: DPDP §5 storage limitation breach.

Scenario 4: Children's Data Sharing

Your educational app collects children's data and stores it with a third-party processor. The processor shares aggregated analytics with a marketing firm, claiming "anonymized data is not personal data." You did not authorize this sharing and had no audit mechanism to prevent it. Fiduciary liability: DPDP §9 children's data breach, consent violation.

In all four cases, the processor is not fully blameless — but the fiduciary remains liable unless it can demonstrate it took all reasonable steps to ensure processor compliance. That defense is weak without a DPA, audit rights, and evidence of monitoring.

GDPR Article 28 vs DPDP §8: Key Differences

The GDPR (Article 28) requires a "contract or other legal act" that binds the processor. The DPDP Act similarly requires a Data Processing Agreement. But there are subtle differences:

  • GDPR Processor cannot process data outside the instructions of the controller. Any processing beyond instructions is a breach.
  • DPDP Processor must ensure compliance with fiduciary obligations. The scope is broader — processor must actively implement security, handle deletion requests, and support data principal rights.
  • GDPR Sub-processor can be engaged with prior notice to controller. GDPR allows opt-out, not opt-in.
  • DPDP Sub-processor requires explicit written approval from fiduciary. This is stricter than GDPR.
  • GDPR Controller can recover damages from processor only if processor breaches the contract. Processor is not jointly liable for controller's violations.
  • DPDP Fiduciary is jointly liable with processor if processor's breach contributes to a data principal's harm. This creates shared liability exposure.

The DPDP standard is stricter on processor engagement and tighter on sub-processor control. Organizations migrating from GDPR-compliant practices may find their DPDP exposure is higher, not lower.

Final Word

The accountability gap is real. Fiduciaries who believe they can outsource data protection by hiring a "certified" processor are taking on unmeasured risk. The DPDP Act does not permit this escape. What it requires is a contractual architecture that keeps the fiduciary accountable through audit rights, breach notification, sub-processor controls, and ongoing monitoring.

"You may delegate execution, but not accountability. Know what your processor is doing — because you are answerable for it."
