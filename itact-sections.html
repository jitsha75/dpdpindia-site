Article 04 of 05 · Statutory Reference

Key Sections of the IT Act
Annotated Reference Guide

A practitioner's annotated guide to the most important sections of the Information Technology Act, 2000 — with current status, enforcement context, and crosslinks to the DPDP Act 2023.

Intermediary Notice: DPDPIndia.in is an intermediary under s. 2(1)(w) IT Act. This reference is for educational purposes only — not legal advice. Independently verify before relying. See Safe Harbour Policy.

§43
● Active
Section 43 — Penalty for Damage to Computer Systems
Civil liability for unauthorised access, virus introduction, denial of service

Section 43 imposes civil liability on any person who, without the permission of the owner or in charge of a computer, computer system, or computer network: (a) accesses or secures access; (b) downloads, copies, or extracts any data; (c) introduces or causes the introduction of any computer contaminant or computer virus; (d) damages or causes disruption; (e) denies or causes denial of access; (f) charges services availed of by a person to another person; or (g) destroys, deletes, or alters any information residing in the computer resource.

The liable person must pay the affected party damages by way of compensation — not exceeding one crore rupees (₹1 crore). This is a civil remedy distinct from the criminal offences under Section 66.

§43A
○ Largely Superseded
Section 43A — Corporate Liability for SPDI
Reasonable security practices for sensitive personal data — now overlapped by DPDP Act 2023

Inserted by the 2008 amendment, Section 43A imposed civil liability on body corporates that are negligent in implementing "reasonable security practices and procedures" for sensitive personal data or information (SPDI), causing wrongful loss or gain. The SPDI Rules, 2011 defined SPDI to include passwords, financial information, health data, biometrics, and sexual orientation.

The maximum compensation was ₹5 crore per affected person. The DPDP Act 2023 now substantially governs personal data protection — Section 43A survives only for non-personal data or where the DPDP Act explicitly does not apply. The DPB penalty regime (up to ₹250 crore) replaces the 43A compensation mechanism for personal data breaches.

§66
● Active
Section 66 — Computer Related Offences
Criminal offences mirroring Section 43 — imprisonment up to 3 years

Where any person dishonestly or fraudulently does any act referred to in Section 43, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to three years, or fine up to five lakh rupees, or both. The key distinction from Section 43 is the element of dishonesty or fraud — transforming civil wrongs into cognisable criminal offences.

Section 66 also encompasses specific offences: 66C (identity theft — fraudulently using another's electronic signature, password, or unique identification feature, punishable up to 3 years and ₹1 lakh fine); 66D (cheating by personation using computer resource, punishable up to 3 years and ₹1 lakh fine); 66E (punishment for violation of privacy by capturing, publishing, or transmitting image of private area without consent — up to 3 years and ₹2 lakh fine); 66F (cyber terrorism — knowledge that it may cause injury to persons or damage to critical infrastructure, punishable with imprisonment up to life).

§66A
✕ Struck Down
Section 66A — Offensive Online Messages [VOID]
Struck down as unconstitutional — Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) 5 SCC 1

Section 66A criminalised sending "grossly offensive," "annoying," or "menacing" messages via computer or communication devices. The Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) 5 SCC 1 struck down Section 66A in its entirety as unconstitutional — violating Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech) and not falling within any of the reasonable restrictions permitted under Article 19(2).

Section 66A is void and unenforceable. Despite this, documented cases of police invoking Section 66A after the 2015 verdict have been reported — the Supreme Court subsequently issued clarificatory orders directing all state DGPs to ensure police compliance. This section has no legal effect and cannot be enforced.

§69
● Active
Section 69 — Power to Intercept, Monitor, or Decrypt
State surveillance powers — Central and State Governments

Section 69 authorises the Central Government or State Government to issue directions to any agency for interception, monitoring, or decryption of any information transmitted through any computer resource — in the interest of sovereignty and integrity of India, defence, state security, friendly relations with foreign states, public order, or prevention or investigation of cognisable offences.

The IT (Procedure and Safeguards for Interception, Monitoring and Decryption of Information) Rules, 2009 govern the authorisation process. Section 69B separately authorises the Central Government to monitor traffic data through the Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In) for cybersecurity purposes.

§69A
● Active (Litigated 2025)
Section 69A — Blocking of Online Content
Central Government power to direct blocking — upheld in Shreya Singhal; contested via Sahyog Portal 2025

Section 69A empowers the Central Government to direct any agency or intermediary to block public access to any information in any computer resource in the interest of sovereignty, integrity, defence, security, public order, friendly relations, or to prevent incitement to cognisable offences. The blocking process is governed by the IT (Procedure and Safeguards for Blocking for Access of Information) Rules, 2009.

The Supreme Court upheld Section 69A in Shreya Singhal as a narrowly drawn provision with adequate safeguards — including Secretary-level authorisation, Review Committee review, and written direction requirements. As of 2025, Section 69A is at the centre of ongoing litigation (X Corp v. Union of India, Karnataka HC) concerning whether the government can issue equivalent blocking orders under Section 79(3)(b) without following Section 69A's procedural safeguards via the Sahyog portal.

§70B
● Active
Section 70B — Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In)
National cybersecurity incident response body; 2022 Directions impose 6-hour reporting

Section 70B establishes the Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In) as the national agency for performing functions relating to cybersecurity incidents. CERT-In's functions include emergency measures, coordination with ministries and organisations in response to cybersecurity incidents, and issuance of guidelines, advisories, and directions.

The CERT-In Directions of April 28, 2022 are the most consequential exercise of Section 70B powers: they mandate a 6-hour mandatory incident reporting window for data centres, VPS providers, cloud providers, ISPs, and other regulated entities. VPN providers must retain customer logs for 5 years. System clocks must be synchronised with NTP servers. Non-compliance attracts penalties under the IT Act including imprisonment up to one year.

§79
◐ Active (Read Down)
Section 79 — Exemption from Liability of Intermediary
Safe Harbour provision — read down by Shreya Singhal to require court/government order for takedown

Section 79 grants intermediaries conditional immunity from liability for third-party content they host or transmit, provided they: (a) do not initiate the transmission or select recipients; (b) observe due diligence as prescribed; and (c) do not conspire, abet, or aid the unlawful act.

The safe harbour is lost under Section 79(3)(b) if the intermediary, upon receiving "actual knowledge" of illegal content, fails to expeditiously remove it. The Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal read "actual knowledge" narrowly — intermediaries must act only on court orders or government authority directions. Private complaints do not trigger mandatory removal. The IT Rules 2021 set the due diligence conditions for maintaining Section 79 protection.
