Pillar 1 · Privacy Compliance

DPDP Act 2023 for PSUs & Government Departments

§1 · Applicability

Is your PSU a Data Fiduciary? Yes — and that has consequences.

In one paragraph. Any public sector undertaking, municipal corporation or government department that determines the purposes and means of processing digital personal data is a Data Fiduciary under the DPDP Act 2023. This includes data collected digitally and data collected offline but later digitised. The constitutional baseline is Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) — privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21. The statutory consequence is a strict set of operational obligations with penalties up to ₹250 crore. The state is not insulated; Section 17 carve-outs are functional and narrow, not institutional.

Examples of clearly covered PSU operations

  • Public utility billing portals (electricity, water, gas distribution PSUs)
  • Transport and ticketing platforms (IRCTC, state transport, metro, port booking)
  • Banking and NBFC platforms run by PSU banks, NBFCs and state cooperative entities
  • e-Governance citizen-service portals (welfare, licensing, certificate issuance)
  • HR and payroll systems holding employee personal data
  • Vendor onboarding and procurement portals (KYC, PAN, GSTIN data)
§2 · Section 17 — The Narrow Carve-out

Functional exemption, not institutional immunity

Section 17 of the DPDPA empowers the Central Government to exempt the state from specific provisions — primarily for sovereignty, public order and crime prevention. The exemption is operational, applies workflow by workflow, and must be documented to be defended.

Standing rule for PSU Legal Counsel: A PSU cannot claim a blanket institutional exemption. Each citizen-facing application must be mapped to determine whether it falls within Section 7 "legitimate uses" or Section 17 exemptions — and which provisions it is exempted from. Default position is full compliance.

Section 17 Function-Mapping Exercise — what your Legal Counsel must produce

  • Workflow inventory — every citizen-facing process, system or scheme listed and classified
  • Section 7 mapping — which workflows qualify as "legitimate uses" (subsidy delivery, statutory licensing, public health, law enforcement)
  • Section 17 mapping — which workflows fall within a notified exemption category
  • Provision-level analysis — exactly which provisions the workflow is exempted from (notice, consent, retention, etc.)
  • Section 17 Exemption Register — formal document maintained by the DPO, ratified by the Board
§3 · SDF Classification

Why most PSUs will be Significant Data Fiduciaries

Section 10 of the DPDPA empowers the Central Government to notify a Data Fiduciary or class of Data Fiduciaries as a Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF). Five statutory factors decide it. PSUs running citizen-facing databases at scale will, in most cases, qualify.

The five Section 10 factors

  • Volume and sensitivity of personal data processed
  • Risk of harm to data principals
  • Potential impact on the sovereignty and integrity of India
  • Risk to electoral democracy
  • Use of emerging technologies (AI/ML, biometrics, IoT)

Appointing a DPO in a PSU — what the Board resolution should cover

  • India-resident individual with documented privacy-law expertise (often paired with CISA/DISA or DPDPA-specific certification)
  • Direct reporting line to the Audit Committee; dotted line to CEO/MD
  • Sufficient seniority and authority — not a junior compliance role
  • Independence from IT operations and procurement
  • Published business contact details on every citizen-facing channel — website, app, transactional portal, signage
  • Annual confidential report to the Board on the state of DPDPA compliance
§5 · Rule 12 — Algorithmic Due Diligence

A new obligation that catches every PSU running automated decisions

In one paragraph. Rule 12 of the DPDP Rules 2025 requires Significant Data Fiduciaries to verify that algorithmic software used to host, display, upload, modify, transmit or share personal data does not pose risks to the rights of data principals. For PSUs, this catches welfare-eligibility scoring, traffic-challan engines, KYC scoring, profiling systems, automated case-disposal engines, and any AI-driven citizen-service workflow. Most existing DPDPA content underplays this; we lead with it because the implementation lift is non-trivial.

Five operational requirements under Rule 12

  • Explainability mechanism — every automated adverse decision must be capable of human-readable explanation, in a language the principal understands
  • Bias detection — periodic testing on representative datasets, documented in the DPIA
  • Human-in-the-loop — meaningful review for decisions affecting entitlements, licensing or enforcement; not rubber-stamping
  • Tamper-proof decision logs — sufficient to support an independent audit of any individual decision
  • Annual DPIA coverage — Rule 12 obligations integrated into the high-risk processing DPIA

§7 · The RTI × DPDPA Reconfiguration

Section 8(1)(j) — the amendment most PSUs have not absorbed

In one paragraph. The DPDP Act 2023 amends Section 8(1)(j) of the RTI Act 2005, removing the "public interest override" that previously allowed disclosure of personal information of public officials. The amended provision is a near-blanket statutory bar on disclosing personal information — asset declarations, disciplinary records, credentials, performance appraisals. Every PSU Public Information Officer must reconfigure response protocols; failure to do so creates DPDPA exposure on one side and RTI litigation on the other.
RTI Section 8(1)(j) — Before vs After DPDPA
Before DPDPA (pre-2023)
Personal information could be withheld unless the larger public interest justified disclosure. The PIO carried a discretionary balancing test — the "public interest override" was workable.
After DPDPA (post-2023)
Personal information is statutorily barred from disclosure. The public-interest override is removed. The PIO's residual discretion is narrow and must be exercised in writing, documented and defensible.

What every PSU PIO must do this quarter

  • Update standard RTI response templates to reflect the amended Section 8(1)(j)
  • Train PIOs in the narrower discretion — and the documentation requirements
  • Coordinate with the DPO on borderline disclosure decisions
  • Maintain a decision register of every withholding under Section 8(1)(j) for audit
§8 · Breach Response

72 hours to the DPBI — and the affected individuals

In the event of a confirmed personal data breach, a PSU must notify both the Data Protection Board of India and the affected individuals within 72 hours, regardless of volume or severity. The CERT-In 6-hour notification obligation runs in parallel for cyber incidents. The two clocks start on awareness, not on confirmation.

Boards must approve an Incident Response Playbook BEFORE go-live of any new SDF-class system. Without a tested playbook, the 72-hour clock becomes the moment a PSU starts losing penalty arguments. Tabletop exercises every six months, with the DPO and CISO co-leading.

The breach response chain

  • T+0 — Security monitoring team detects anomaly; Incident Response Playbook activated
  • T+1h — Affected systems isolated; preliminary root-cause analysis
  • T+6h — CERT-In notification (if cyber incident criteria met)
  • T+24h — Internal breach report compiled; DPO assesses notification obligation
  • T+48h — Board / Audit Committee briefed; communications plan approved
  • T+72h — DPBI notification submitted; affected individuals notified via published channels
  • Post-incident — Forensic investigation, CAPA, lessons-learned report to the Board
