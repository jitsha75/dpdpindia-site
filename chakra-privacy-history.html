01

Colonial-Era Foundations

Telegraph Act 1885 to Indian Post Office Act 1898

The conceptual ancestors of privacy protection in India predate independence by several decades. The Indian Telegraph Act, 1885 recognised the sanctity of communications in transit, prohibiting interception of telegraph messages except on the order of a competent authority. This represented an early legislative acknowledgment that private communications deserve protection from state intrusion.

The Indian Post Office Act, 1898 similarly protected correspondence in the postal system. Section 26 permitted the Central Government to authorise interception of postal articles "in the interest of the sovereignty or integrity of India, the security of the State, friendly relations with foreign States, public order, decency or morality" — language strikingly similar to the reasonable restrictions in Article 19(2) of the Constitution adopted five decades later.

The Indian Evidence Act, 1872 protected certain professional communications from compelled disclosure — attorney-client privilege (s. 126), medical privilege, and marital communications — reflecting the common-law tradition that certain private relationships carry an implicit expectation of confidentiality.

Colonial Legacy: India inherited English common law's recognition of privacy in specific, bounded contexts (home, correspondence, professional relationships) — but not as a general right. This contextual approach shaped debates in the Constituent Assembly and later Supreme Court jurisprudence.
02

Constituent Assembly Debates (1946–49)

Why privacy was not expressly enumerated — and what the framers intended

When the Constituent Assembly drafted the fundamental rights provisions in 1946–49, privacy was actively discussed but ultimately not expressly enumerated as a standalone right. K.M. Munshi moved an amendment to Article 17 (later Article 21) to include "the right of every citizen to his personal liberty and to the security of his person, house, papers and effects against unreasonable searches and seizures." The amendment was not adopted.

B.R. Ambedkar, as Chairman of the Drafting Committee, explained that the omission was deliberate — not because privacy was unimportant, but because the framers trusted that the broad language of Article 21 ("No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law") was capacious enough to encompass privacy through judicial interpretation.

Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech and expression) and Article 19(1)(d) (freedom of movement) were also recognised as carrying implicit privacy dimensions. The framers anticipated that courts would read these rights expansively.

Key Drafting Choice: India's framers chose a living, judicially-interpreted constitution over an exhaustive list of rights. This allowed Article 21 to grow with society — ultimately absorbing the right to privacy in 2017. The contrast with the U.S. Fourth Amendment's express enumeration of search-and-seizure protections is instructive.

The Directive Principles of State Policy also recognised the right to privacy implicitly. Article 39(e) speaks of protecting workers from conditions injurious to health — read alongside Article 21, this later supported the view that bodily privacy is constitutionally mandated.

03

Supreme Court Evolution (1954–2015)

From M.P. Sharma to Gobind — six decades of privacy jurisprudence

M.P. Sharma & Others v. Satish Chandra
AIR 1954 SC 300 · Eight-Judge Bench

The Supreme Court held that the Constitution did not guarantee a fundamental right to privacy as understood in U.S. Fourth Amendment jurisprudence. The Court's reasoning — that the framers consciously omitted the equivalent of the Fourth Amendment — would anchor privacy scepticism for decades. However, the bench was careful to limit its holding to the specific context of search warrants.

Kharak Singh v. State of Uttar Pradesh
AIR 1963 SC 1295 · Six-Judge Bench

The Court struck down police surveillance regulations under U.P. Police Regulations permitting surveillance, night-time domiciliary visits, and secret picketing. A six-judge majority held that domiciliary visits violated Article 21. Crucially, Justice Subba Rao (dissenting on other points) held that the right to privacy was an essential ingredient of personal liberty under Article 21 — a dissent that would become the majority view over time.

Gobind v. State of Madhya Pradesh
1975 (2) SCC 148 · Three-Judge Bench

Justice Mathew's landmark judgment accepted that the right to privacy is implicit in the guarantee of life and personal liberty in Article 21. The Court held that this right must yield to compelling state interests, applying a strict scrutiny-like test. Gobind established privacy as a qualified fundamental right — subject to regulation, but constitutionally protected. This was the first clear Supreme Court recognition of a general right to privacy.

R. Rajagopal v. State of Tamil Nadu
1994 (6) SCC 632

The "Auto Shankar" case recognised a constitutional right to privacy protecting individuals from unauthorised publication of private facts. The Court balanced privacy against freedom of the press, holding that public figures have reduced but not zero privacy expectations. This case imported elements of the U.S. "public figure" doctrine into Indian constitutional law.

PUCL v. Union of India
1997 (1) SCC 301

The Court held that telephone tapping constitutes a serious invasion of privacy and that s. 5(2) of the Indian Telegraph Act must be read subject to constitutional protections. The Court laid down procedural safeguards for interception — including requirements of necessity, proportionality, and independent review — that presaged the framework now embodied in the surveillance provisions of the IT Act.

Naz Foundation v. Government of NCT of Delhi
2009 (160) DLT 277 · Delhi High Court

The Delhi High Court read down Section 377 of the IPC, holding that it violated the right to dignity and privacy under Articles 21, 14 and 15. While the Supreme Court reversed this in Suresh Kumar Koushal (2013), the Naz judgment's privacy reasoning was later vindicated in Puttaswamy (2017) and Navtej Singh Johar (2018).

04

Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)

The unanimous nine-judge bench that settled the constitutional status of privacy

On 24 August 2017, a nine-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court unanimously held that the right to privacy is a fundamental right protected under Part III of the Constitution, primarily under Article 21, and permeating through Articles 14 and 19. The judgment — running to over 500 pages across six separate concurrences — is the most significant constitutional pronouncement since the Basic Structure doctrine in Kesavananda Bharati (1973).

Operative holdings:

  • M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1963) — insofar as they held that the Constitution does not protect a right to privacy — were expressly overruled.
  • Privacy is not an absolute right. It is subject to reasonable restrictions — but those restrictions must satisfy the triple test of legality (law authorising the restriction), legitimate aim, and proportionality.
  • Privacy encompasses multiple dimensions: bodily integrity, spatial privacy (home and personal spaces), decisional autonomy (choices about personal matters), informational privacy, and communicational privacy.
  • The right extends to data about individuals — the Court expressly noted that informational self-determination is a facet of the constitutional right to privacy.
The Proportionality Test: Justice D.Y. Chandrachud's lead concurrence laid down a four-part test for restrictions on privacy: (1) existence of a law; (2) legitimate state aim; (3) the restriction must be proportionate — the least restrictive means must be used; and (4) procedural guarantees against abuse. This test now governs all privacy-adjacent legislation, including the DPDP Act.

Justice Chandrachud's judgment also specifically addressed informational privacy in the context of Aadhaar: "An individual may have a legitimate expectation that personal information provided to the state for one purpose will not be used for another purpose without consent." This passage became the doctrinal foundation for the data protection obligations in the DPDP Act 2023.

Justice S.A. Bobde (concurring) emphasised the "reasonable expectation of privacy" standard drawn from U.S. Fourth Amendment jurisprudence — applicable even in public spaces when a person has a subjective expectation that society recognises as reasonable.

Justice S.K. Kaul (concurring) addressed the need for a data protection law explicitly: "The right to informational self-determination mandates that the individual must have control over data relating to her... India needs a robust data protection law." This judicial mandate accelerated the legislative process leading to the DPDP Act 2023.

05

Privacy as the Foundation of DPDP Act 2023

From constitutional mandate to legislative architecture

The Puttaswamy judgment did not merely declare privacy a fundamental right — it effectively mandated that Parliament legislate a data protection framework. The Court held that the state has a positive obligation to protect citizens' informational privacy, not just a negative duty to refrain from violating it.

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 is the legislative response to this constitutional mandate. Its Preamble expressly states the twin objects of protecting "the right of individuals to protect their personal data" while recognising "the need to process personal data for lawful purposes." This mirrors the proportionality framework of Puttaswamy: privacy is protected, but subject to legitimate, proportionate data processing.

Key DPDP Act provisions that flow directly from Puttaswamy:

  • Consent-based processing (s. 6): Operationalises the informational self-determination principle — individuals must consent before their data is processed.
  • Purpose limitation (s. 8(3)): Reflects the Court's holding that data given for one purpose cannot be used for another.
  • Right to erasure (s. 12(3)): Gives practical force to the constitutional right to be forgotten, recognised in Justice Kaul's concurrence.
  • Data minimisation (s. 8(4)): Reflects the proportionality requirement — only data strictly necessary for the stated purpose may be collected.
Constitutional Supremacy: Any provision of the DPDP Act that falls below the constitutional standard set by Puttaswamy would be liable to challenge under Article 32. Conversely, the State's exemptions in s. 17 of the Act must themselves satisfy the Puttaswamy proportionality test to be constitutionally valid.
06

International Comparison

How India's privacy jurisprudence fits the global landscape

India's constitutional journey toward recognising privacy as a fundamental right mirrors developments in other major democracies, though the route was distinctive:

  • United States: Privacy implied from First, Third, Fourth, Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments — recognised in Griswold v. Connecticut (1965) and Roe v. Wade (1973, overruled in part in 2022). The right is more limited in scope than India's post-Puttaswamy framework.
  • European Union: Article 8 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights expressly protects personal data as a distinct right, separate from privacy (Article 7). GDPR implements this at legislative level.
  • Germany: The Federal Constitutional Court recognised a right of "informational self-determination" (Volkszählungsurteil, 1983) — the closest parallel to India's Puttaswamy, predating India by 34 years.
  • South Africa: Section 14 of the Constitution expressly protects privacy, including the right to have personal information protected. POPIA (2013) implements data protection.
  • Brazil: LGPD (2018) enacted after the Supreme Court confirmed privacy as a fundamental right — mirroring India's legislative path post-Puttaswamy.
Research Note: India's DPDP Act 2023 is sometimes compared to GDPR. However, the constitutional foundations differ: GDPR rests on the EU Charter's express Article 8 (personal data protection as a distinct right), while India's DPDP Act rests on the implied Article 21 right identified in Puttaswamy. This distinction may matter in future constitutional litigation over the Act's exemptions.
