01

Overview — Civil vs Criminal Remedies

Dual-track redressal system under the IT Act

The IT Act creates a dual-track redressal system. The same act — for example, unauthorized access to a computer system — may give rise to both a civil claim for compensation under Section 43 and a criminal prosecution under Section 66. The choice of track depends on the complainant's objective: compensation (civil) or punishment (criminal). This dual-track structure is a defining feature of the Act and must be understood by both practitioners and victims.

Civil Track
Section 43 Compensation
Adjudicating Officer (AO) → CATC → High Court. Remedy: Compensation to aggrieved party. No cap post-2008 (up to ₹5 crore before AO). Key: No criminal intent required.
Criminal Track
Sections 65–70 Prosecution
Police/CERT-In → Sessions Court (or JMFC for offences <3 yrs). Remedy: Imprisonment and/or fine. Key: Dishonest or fraudulent intent required for S.66.
Timeline distinction: Civil claims typically take longer to process through adjudication and tribunal appeal; criminal cases follow CrPC (now BNSS 2023) and may move faster if police investigation is efficient.
02

Adjudicating Officers (Section 46)

Specialized officials with quasi-judicial powers

Under Section 46, the Central Government appoints Adjudicating Officers (AOs) who are officials of the rank of Secretary to the Government. They have jurisdiction to adjudicate civil claims under Section 43 and 43A (though 43A has been largely superseded by the DPDP Act 2023). AOs can award compensation up to ₹5 crore — claims above this threshold go directly to civil courts.

Powers of AOs: They may summon persons, require document production, issue certificates. They follow a quasi-judicial process — natural justice principles apply. The AO's order can be appealed to the Cyber Appellate Tribunal (CATC).

Accessibility note: The adjudication process was originally meant to be quick and accessible — a specialist alternative to civil courts. In practice, the number of AO orders has been limited, with many complainants preferring criminal routes or civil suits.
03

Cyber Appellate Tribunal (Section 48)

Appeal forum for AO orders and specialized cyber disputes

The Cyber Appellate Tribunal (CATC) is established under Section 48 of the IT Act. Appeals from AO orders lie to the CATC. The Presiding Officer must be a person qualified to be a judge of a High Court. Beyond the CATC, further appeals lie to the High Court on questions of law.

CATC Structure: Presiding Officer (High Court judge rank) + two members. Sits in New Delhi. Can grant interim relief, confirm/modify/reverse AO orders.

Current operational status: The CATC has historically been non-functional for extended periods due to vacancies in the Presiding Officer position. This is a significant gap in the enforcement architecture. Parties have frequently been forced to approach High Courts directly.
04

Criminal Courts & Jurisdiction

Court hierarchy and investigative authority for IT Act offences

Criminal offences under the IT Act are cognizable and non-bailable in most cases. Jurisdiction is tiered by severity:

Offences punishable >3 years: Sessions Court (higher jurisdiction)
Offences punishable ≤3 years: Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC)
Investigation: Police (DSP rank or above per S.80), CERT-In, state cyber cells
Complaint portal: cybercrime.gov.in (operated by I4C, Home Ministry)
Extraterritorial reach: Section 1(2) and Section 75 give the IT Act extraterritorial reach — offences committed outside India against computers located in India fall within Indian jurisdiction.

Trial procedure: Follows CrPC (now BNSS 2023). Digital evidence governed by Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act (now Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023).

05

Digital Evidence (Section 65B)

Admissibility and certification of electronic records

Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act (BSA 2023 equivalent) is critical for any cybercrime prosecution or civil claim. It governs the admissibility of electronic records.

Key requirements:

A Section 65B certificate must accompany any electronic record sought to be admitted as evidence
The certificate must be issued by a responsible official of the device/system used to produce the record
The certificate must confirm: (a) the electronic record was produced by the device/computer; (b) the device was in regular use; (c) the information was stored in the ordinary course of activities; (d) the record is a true representation
Chain of custody: Hash values (MD5, SHA-256) are used to prove integrity of digital evidence. CFSL (Central Forensic Science Laboratory) and state FSLs are the designated agencies for digital forensics
Critical failure point: Failure to obtain a S.65B certificate at the time of seizure is one of the most common causes of cybercrime case failure in India. Police and legal teams must ensure certification at the point of evidence collection, not after.

Landmark case: Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (2020) SC — the Supreme Court clarified that the S.65B certificate is mandatory (not directory) for electronic records, except where the original device itself is produced before court.

06

Compoundable Offences (Section 77A)

Settlement and out-of-court resolution options

Section 77A allows certain IT Act offences to be compounded (settled between parties) — either with court permission or (for less serious offences) by the parties themselves. Offences punishable with imprisonment of up to 3 years may generally be compounded. Offences punishable with more than 3 years cannot be compounded.

Non-compoundable serious offences:

Section 66F (Cyber terrorism): Life imprisonment — non-compoundable
Section 67B (CSAM): 5–7 years imprisonment — non-compoundable
Section 70 (Protected systems): 10 years imprisonment — non-compoundable

Compounding procedure: Application to the court having jurisdiction. Court may impose a compounding fee. Once compounded, the accused is acquitted of that charge.
