Section 66C and Identity Theft:
The IT Act's Most Under-Used Protection

Dishonestly using someone's electronic signature, password, or unique identifier is identity theft under Section 66C — punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment and ₹1 lakh fine. Yet prosecutions remain rare. Discover why the law exists, why enforcement appears limited (based on observed case trends, not official statistics), and how to build a complaint that sticks.

Published 27 March 2026
Category IT Act · Cybercrime
Read time 7 min
IT Act §66C Identity Theft Cybercrime Digital Fraud Aadhaar SIM Swap

Identity theft under Indian law is not a new crime. But Section 66C of the IT Act 2000 made it a cybercrime, carrying imprisonment and fine. Yet across Indian police forces, CERT-In complaints, and prosecutorial practice, Section 66C cases remain vanishingly rare. Understanding why — and how to prosecute them effectively — is essential for victims and digital-era legal practitioners.

The Statutory Framework: Section 66C and Section 66D

Section 66C of the IT Act 2000 defines identity theft in the digital age:

📌 Statutory Text

A person commits identity theft when they dishonestly use the electronic signature, password, or unique identification feature of another person in a manner that causes injury to that person. Punishment: imprisonment up to 3 years and/or fine up to ₹1 lakh.

Section 66C is a dishonesty-based offence — meaning the perpetrator must act with knowledge that they are impersonating another person. Accidental unauthorized access (hacking into a system by mistake, for instance) would not fall under section 66C, though it would trigger Section 43 (civil liability) or Section 66 (general unauthorized access).

Section 66D (cheating by personation) complements section 66C and covers a broader category: using a computer resource to cheat or defraud by impersonation. Section 66D carries the same penalty as section 66C but applies more broadly to any computer-mediated fraud, not just identity theft per se.

⚠ Overlapping Liability

A single incident — for example, using someone's Aadhaar number to create a fake bank account — can trigger Section 66C (identity theft), Section 66D (cheating by personation), IPC Section 420 (cheating), and IPC Section 463 (forgery). Police and prosecutors may charge multiple sections, creating cumulative sentencing exposure.

Why Section 66C Prosecutions Are Rare

Despite the statute's breadth, section 66C prosecutions remain exceedingly rare. Three systemic barriers explain this:

1. Evidentiary Burden: Proving "Dishonest" Intent

Section 66C requires proof of dishonest intent — the perpetrator knew they were using another person's identifier. In court, this is harder than proving mere unauthorized access. A defendant can claim ignorance or accident. Police must prove:

  • Q The accused knew they were impersonating the victim
  • Q The accused intended to cause injury or knew injury would follow
  • Q A causal link between the impersonation and the injury suffered

This is forensically and legally complex. Many breaches are attributed to "database leaks" or "phishing attacks" where the perpetrator remains unidentified or unlocatable. Proving dishonest intent against an anonymous cybercriminal in a foreign jurisdiction is operationally infeasible for most Indian police units.

2. Digital Forensics Capacity Gaps

Proving identity theft digitally requires:

  • · Chain of custody documentation — showing how evidence was collected, stored, and preserved without tampering
  • · Hash values and digital signatures — cryptographic proof that digital evidence has not been altered
  • · IP address logs and metadata — linking a perpetrator's access to their location or ISP
  • · Evidence Act compliance — under Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act (now covered by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023), digital evidence must be certified by the keeper of the computer system that produced it

Most Indian police cyber units lack training and equipment to generate forensically sound evidence meeting these standards. The result: investigations stall, and cases are not filed.

3. Police FIR Registration Reluctance

Police are reluctant to register FIRs for identity theft because:

  • → Perception of "soft" crime: Identity theft is viewed as lower-priority than physical assault or theft of goods. Cyber units in many states are under-resourced and overloaded.
  • → Jurisdictional complexity: Identity theft often involves perpetrators in different states or countries. Determining which police jurisdiction has authority delays FIR registration.
  • → Victim's lack of authority: In many cases, the actual harmed party is a bank, telecom company, or service provider — not the individual whose identity was stolen. Police hesitate to register complaints on behalf of victims rather than on victim request.
📌 Practical Reality

Filing a section 66C complaint with police is straightforward; getting them to register an FIR and investigate is a multistep battle. Victims often need to escalate to the Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) or approach the National Cyber Crime Helpline (1930) to trigger action.

Specific Identity Theft Scenarios in India

Understanding how identity theft manifests in India helps build stronger complaints:

SIM Swap Fraud

An attacker uses your personal information (Aadhaar, name, address) to convince a telecom retailer to transfer your SIM card to a new phone. They now control your mobile number and OTP channel. This triggers Section 66C (your phone number and SIM are unique identifiers) and is among the easiest identity thefts to prove — telecom logs and retailer CCTV will show the swap.

Aadhaar-Based Identity Theft

Your Aadhaar number is used to open a bank account, apply for credit, or activate a phone line without authorization. The Aadhaar Act 2016, Section 29 criminalizes disclosure of Aadhaar information; combined with Section 66C, this creates dual liability. Additionally, Section 38 of the Aadhaar Act provides for criminal penalties (up to 3 years and ₹10,000 fine) specifically for identity-based Aadhaar misuse.

OTP and Password Theft

An attacker obtains your banking or email password through phishing, malware, or data breach, then accesses your accounts. Section 66C covers this directly: the password is your "electronic signature," and using it dishonestly is identity theft. However, the perpetrator must be identified for prosecution — tracing faceless cybercriminals remains the core challenge.

Email/Social Account Takeover

Your email or social media account is hijacked and used to impersonate you (sending scam messages, conducting financial fraud, etc.). This falls squarely under section 66C and section 66D, but proving the perpetrator's dishonest intent is difficult if the hijacking was facilitated by negligent password practices or data breaches.

"Section 66C exists on paper. But in practice, it requires victims to navigate evidentiary complexity, police reluctance, and forensic capacity gaps. Building a prosecutable case demands discipline and digital sophistication."

How to Build a Prosecutable Section 66C Complaint

If you are a victim of identity theft, follow these steps to maximize the likelihood of FIR registration and eventual prosecution:

Step 1: Document the Incident

  • ✓ Screenshot and preserve all evidence: Messages, transaction records, account access logs, emails — capture timestamps, sender details, and device information
  • ✓ Download account access logs: From your bank, email, social media, or telecom provider showing unauthorized logins and when they occurred
  • ✓ Note IP addresses and device identifiers: If possible, extract IP addresses from access logs; these can be traced to ISPs and geolocation

Step 2: File with Relevant Service Providers

  • ✓ Notify your bank/service provider: They are legally obligated (under banking regulations, the DPDP Act, and the IT Act) to preserve evidence and may file parallel complaints or provide forensic support
  • ✓ Obtain a written acknowledgment: A formal letter from your bank or telecom provider confirming the unauthorized access strengthens your complaint

Step 3: Use the National Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in)

  • ✓ File a complaint on cybercrime.gov.in: This portal is monitored by CERT-In and state cybercrime units. It is more responsive than visiting local police stations.
  • ✓ Clearly cite Section 66C and Section 66D: Explicitly state in your complaint that the accused dishonestly used your identifier, causing financial or reputational injury
  • ✓ Attach documentary evidence: Screenshots, account logs, bank statements, and service provider acknowledgments

Step 4: File an FIR Locally (If Cybercrime Portal Does Not Respond)

  • ✓ Visit your local cybercrime or police station: File a formal written complaint naming the specific sections (66C, 66D, 420 IPC/BNS, 463 IPC/BNS)
  • ✓ Demand FIR registration: If police refuse to register an FIR, cite Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure 1973 (now Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023), which mandates FIR registration for cognizable offences like cybercrime
  • ✓ File a complaint with the Police Commissioner: If local police refuse to register, escalate to the Police Commissioner or the State Cyber Crime Cell

Step 5: Escalation Paths — CBI/SFIO

  • ✓ If identity theft involves financial fraud exceeding ₹1 crore, the case may trigger CBI (Central Bureau of Investigation) jurisdiction under the Prevention of Corruption Act or IPC provisions
  • ✓ If the perpetrator is a corporate entity or employee, approach the Serious Fraud Investigation Office (SFIO) under the Ministry of Corporate Affairs

Related Offences and Overlapping Liability

Identity theft intersects with multiple statutory frameworks:

IPC 1860 (Now BNS 2023) Provisions

  • § Section 419 (cheating by personation): Cheating using personation; identical to Section 66D but predates the IT Act and applies to non-digital impersonation
  • § Section 420 (cheating): Dishonestly causing loss; overlaps with section 66D fraud scenarios
  • § Section 463 (forgery): Making false documents; relevant when identity theft involves forging signatures or documents

Aadhaar Act 2016

  • § Section 29 (prohibition on disclosure): Criminalizes unauthorized disclosure of Aadhaar data; complements section 66C when Aadhaar is misused
  • § Section 38 (offences): Up to 3 years imprisonment and ₹10,000 fine for Aadhaar-specific violations

DPDP Act 2023: Complementary Enforcement

Section 8 of the DPDP Act 2023 imposes a duty on data fiduciaries to prevent identity theft through reasonable security measures. While the DPDP Act is a regulatory (not criminal) statute, it creates enforcement leverage:

  • Q If a data fiduciary (bank, telecom provider, e-commerce platform) fails to prevent your identity from being stolen through weak security, you can lodge a complaint with the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)
  • Q The DPBI can impose penalties on the fiduciary (up to ₹150 crore) and order remediation — which may include compensation to affected individuals

This creates a dual-track remedy: criminal prosecution of the perpetrator under section 66C, and regulatory action against the negligent data fiduciary under the DPDP Act.

Evidentiary Standards: Section 65B, Indian Evidence Act

Digital evidence in criminal trials must meet rigorous standards. Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act (now covered by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023) requires that digital evidence be authenticated by a certificate from the person in charge of the computer system that produced it.

When filing a complaint, ensure that service providers (banks, telecom, email providers) provide:

  • ✓ A Section 65B certificate — confirming the authenticity and integrity of digital records produced by their systems
  • ✓ Hash values — cryptographic fingerprints proving the data has not been tampered with since extraction
  • ✓ Chain of custody documentation — showing how evidence was collected, preserved, and transmitted to police
⚠ Critical for Prosecution

Without proper Section 65B certification and chain of custody, digital evidence may be inadmissible in court — even if it clearly proves identity theft. Victims must proactively demand these certifications from service providers rather than expecting police to obtain them.

Food for Thought

Section 66C is powerful law on paper — identity theft, dishonest impersonation, up to 3 years and ₹1 lakh fine. But the gap between statutory promise and prosecutorial reality is vast. Weak digital forensics, police capacity constraints, and evidentiary complexity mean most perpetrators escape accountability.

"Enforcement is the victim's burden — not the state's."
