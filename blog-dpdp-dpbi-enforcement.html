The Data Protection Board of India:
Enforcement Architecture and Penalty Framework

Chapter VI of the DPDP Act establishes the DPBI as India's independent data protection authority. From establishment and inquiry powers to breach remedies and penalties reaching ₹250 crore, understand how enforcement operates under India's data protection regime.

Published 27 March 2026
Category DPDP Act · Enforcement
Read time 8 min
DPDP Act 2023 DPBI Enforcement Penalties Adjudication Data Fiduciary

The Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) is not merely an advisory body — it is India's designated adjudicatory body for data protection violations, constituted by the Central Government under Chapter VI. Established under Chapter VI of the DPDP Act 2023, the Board wields investigation powers, remedy authority, and penalty enforcement mechanisms that rival or exceed those of sectoral regulators. Understanding DPBI's establishment, powers, and penalty framework is essential for any data fiduciary operating in India.

DPBI Establishment and Composition

Section 18 of the DPDP Act mandates the establishment of the Data Protection Board of India as an independent statutory body. The Board comprises:

  • · A Chairperson appointed by the Central Government — typically a retired judge or senior civil servant with data protection expertise
  • · Between 2 to 5 Members appointed by the Central Government with prescribed qualifications in law, technology, privacy, or public administration
  • · Executive staff and investigators directly accountable to the Board, not to the Ministry responsible for digital governance

This structure mirrors international data protection authorities (UK ICO, French CNIL, German BfDI) in granting operational independence from executive control. The Board's decisions cannot be overridden by any government ministry — a critical safeguard ensuring that enforcement remains impartial.

📌 Key Principle

The DPBI's independence is constitutionally anchored and operationally protected by statute. No ministry, department, or state authority can directly instruct the Board on enforcement decisions or investigations. This ensures that politically sensitive data fiduciaries cannot use ministerial pressure to deflect accountability.

Inquiry Procedures and Powers (Section 19)

Section 19 of the DPDP Act grants the DPBI broad inquiry and adjudicatory powers. The Board is vested with the authority and duties of a civil court under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908, enabling it to:

  • ✓ Summon witnesses and compel attendance — any person connected to a data handling operation can be required to appear and testify
  • ✓ Demand production of documents — data handling logs, consent records, security policies, encryption keys, and vendor contracts are all discoverable
  • ✓ Conduct inspections and investigations — DPBI investigators can visit fiduciary premises, inspect data systems, and collect forensic evidence
  • ✓ Receive and examine evidence — technical evidence, digital forensics, metadata, and expert testimony are all admissible under a lower evidentiary threshold than criminal courts

Critically, the Board operates under an inquisitorial, not purely adversarial, model. This means DPBI is not passive — it may initiate investigations independently, question fiduciaries at length, and demand explanations. A data fiduciary cannot simply refuse to cooperate or delay production of evidence.

⚠ Enforcement Risk

Failure to comply with DPBI summons, document demands, or investigative inspections is itself a violation. Non-cooperation can lead to penalty escalation and referral to law enforcement agencies. The Board has teeth in enforcing its procedural authority.

Breach Notification Obligations (Section 20)

Section 20 of the DPDP Act imposes a mandatory breach notification regime that is among the most stringent globally. When a data fiduciary experiences a data breach — any unauthorised access, disclosure, alteration, or destruction of personal data — it must notify:

  • Q The DPBI — within 72 hours of discovering the breach (or knowledge of it)
  • Q Affected data principals — with information about the scope, type of data compromised, likely consequences, and remediation steps
  • Q Other fiduciaries or processors who downstream received data from the breached dataset

The 72-hour deadline is unambiguous: there is no discretion, no "materiality threshold" below which notification can be waived. Even breaches affecting 10 records require reporting. The fiduciary cannot delay while investigating or negotiating with breach actors.

Failure to notify triggers a penalty of up to ₹200 crore under Schedule (s. 33) of the DPDP Act — among the highest-tier fines. This alone has forced Indian organisations to redesign incident response protocols and invest in breach detection systems.

Voluntary Undertakings and Mediation (Section 21 and s. 24)

Section 21 of the DPDP Act introduces a mechanism for voluntary undertakings — commitments that a data fiduciary may offer to the DPBI to remedy violations, improve practices, or prevent future harm. If accepted, these undertakings are binding and become enforceable as quasi-contractual obligations.

Similarly, Section 24 grants the DPBI authority to mediate between data principals and fiduciaries before formal adjudication. This reflects a regulatory philosophy that not all breaches require maximum penalties; proportionate remedies and corrective action can resolve disputes faster.

However, voluntary undertakings are not guarantees of penalty forgiveness. The Board retains discretion to accept or reject proposed remedies. If a fiduciary's undertaking is insufficient or if the fiduciary breaches the undertaking, penalties can be imposed or escalated.

📌 Strategic Insight

Early engagement with DPBI through proposed remedial measures and voluntary undertakings can reduce penalty exposure. Organisations that proactively fix breaches, strengthen controls, and commit to DPBI oversight often face lower fines than those that contest liability or delay remediation.

Powers of the Board and Remedial Directions (Section 23)

Section 23 of the DPDP Act enumerates DPBI's remedial and enforcement arsenal. Upon finding a breach, the Board may:

  • § Direct cessation of unauthorised processing — order a fiduciary to stop collecting, storing, or using personal data in a certain manner immediately
  • § Mandate security improvements — require implementation of specific security controls, encryption standards, access logs, or penetration testing
  • § Order data deletion or anonymisation — require erasure of non-essential datasets or anonymisation to render data non-personal
  • § Impose penalties — fine the fiduciary (covered in detail below)
  • § Refer matters to law enforcement — if the breach appears to constitute criminal identity theft, fraud, or data theft under the IT Act, refer the case to police or CBI

These powers are cumulative. A single DPBI order might demand cessation of processing, mandate specific security upgrades, require deletion of legacy data, impose a fine, and refer the matter for criminal investigation — all simultaneously.

"The DPBI's powers are not advisory. Non-compliance with DPBI directions is itself a violation attracting additional penalties. For data fiduciaries, Board orders are not negotiable endpoints — they are binding imperatives."

Right to Complain and DPBI's Suo Motu Powers (Section 24)

Section 24 of the DPDP Act grants data principals a direct right to lodge complaints with the DPBI alleging breach of data protection obligations. Unlike sectoral regulators that require a specific harmed individual, the DPBI may initiate investigations on its own motion (suo motu) based on:

  • Q Complaints from individuals or groups affected by systemic breaches
  • Q Public reports or media investigations revealing widespread data breaches
  • Q Referrals from other government agencies (RBI, MEITY, SEC, etc.) indicating data protection violations
  • Q DPBI's own monitoring of fiduciary practices in critical sectors (health, finance, telecom)

The suo motu power is significant: a fiduciary does not need to wait for a formal complaint to trigger DPBI scrutiny. If a breach becomes public or if the Board identifies systemic failures, it can act independently.

The Penalty Framework: Up to ₹250 Crore for Fiduciaries (Schedule, Section 33)

The DPDP Act establishes a tiered penalty structure based on the violation category. These are not mere fines; they are calculated to be economically painful, deterring repeat offences and signalling regulatory seriousness. The Schedule (s. 33) specifies:

Tier 1: Children's Data Violations (Up to ₹250 crore)

Any breach of Section 9 obligations — which mandate heightened security, verifiable parental consent, and restrictions on data use for children — attracts penalties up to ₹250 crore or 10% of annual turnover (whichever is higher). This reflects the legal principle that harm to children is treated with utmost severity. Examples include:

  • ✗ Processing children's biometric data without explicit parental consent
  • ✗ Operating a children's social platform without child-specific consent mechanisms
  • ✗ Profiling children for commercial or behavioural purposes

Tier 2: Breach Notification Failures (Up to ₹200 crore)

Failure to notify the DPBI or affected data principals of breaches within 72 hours under Section 8(6) attracts fines up to ₹200 crore or 8% annual turnover. This is the second-highest tier, reflecting that breach concealment is treated as aggravated misconduct.

Tier 3: General Fiduciary Obligations (Up to ₹150 crore)

Breach of core Section 8 obligations — consent, data minimisation, security, retention limits, and transparency — attracts fines up to ₹150 crore or 6% annual turnover. This tier covers most violations: unlawful processing, inadequate security, retention beyond permissible periods, and failure to honour data principal rights.

Tier 4: Data Principal Violations (Up to ₹10,000)

Note: The ₹10,000 figure applies exclusively to Data Principals under Section 15, not to organisations. It is not a minimum penalty for Data Fiduciaries — there is no prescribed statutory minimum for fiduciary violations.

A data principal who breaches Section 15 obligations (e.g., misusing obtained data, violating confidentiality) faces a maximum fine of ₹10,000. This tiered approach acknowledges asymmetry: organisations command resources and must be deterred more forcefully; individuals have fewer resources and face proportionately lower penalties.

⚠ Calculation Importance

Penalties under the DPDP Act are calculated as the higher of the fixed amount or a percentage of global annual turnover. For multinational companies or large Indian conglomerates, turnover-based fines far exceed fixed amounts. A company with ₹10,000 crore global revenue facing a section 8 violation may incur a penalty of ₹600 crore (6% of ₹10,000 crore) — triple the stated ₹150 crore maximum. This creates massive deterrent effect for large enterprises.

Comparison with GDPR and International Enforcement

How does DPBI enforcement compare to global precedent? The GDPR (EU) — often cited as the gold standard in data protection — authorises EU Data Protection Authorities to impose fines up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover. By comparison, the DPDP Act's scheme is:

  • ? Higher in nominal amount: ₹250 crore ≈ €30 million, exceeding GDPR's €20M baseline for the most severe violations
  • ? Lower in percentage-based cap: GDPR caps at 4% global turnover; DPDP caps at 10% (for children's violations). However, the DPDP's lower tiers are capped at lower percentages (6%, 8%), creating risk stratification.
  • ? More targeted: DPDP penalises specific violation categories (children, breach notification, general fiduciary duties) with distinct caps, whereas GDPR penalties are more holistic

In practice, the DPDP Act's penalties are comparable to or exceed GDPR fines for equivalent violations. The €50M fine imposed on Meta (WhatsApp) by Ireland's DPC in 2021 was exceptional; most GDPR fines range €1-10M. By contrast, DPDP penalties in the ₹50-200 crore range (€6-24M) are standardised for serious breaches.

What Triggers a DPBI Inquiry?

Not every complaint results in a formal inquiry. The DPBI uses a threshold assessment:

  • → Complaint volume and severity: A single isolated complaint may not trigger inquiry; systemic complaints or breaches affecting large populations trigger immediate action
  • → Media and public interest: Breaches covered by media or publicly disclosed trigger suo motu inquiries regardless of formal complaints
  • → Sector vulnerability: Breaches in critical sectors (health, finance, telecom) warrant priority investigation even if complaint volume is low
  • → Repeat violation: A fiduciary with prior DPBI findings faces heightened scrutiny and lower thresholds for new investigations

Compliance Readiness and Board-Level Accountability

Given DPBI's expansive powers and steep penalties, organisations must embed data protection accountability at the board level. Essential measures include:

  • ✓ Chief Data Officer or Data Protection Officer role: A senior executive directly accountable to the Board and not subordinate to IT or product teams
  • ✓ Incident response protocol: A written, board-approved protocol for breach detection, internal notification, DPBI reporting, and data principal notification — with timelines calibrated to the 72-hour breach notification window
  • ✓ Regular penetration testing and security audits: Third-party validation that systems meet "industry-standard" security, a baseline DPBI expects
  • ✓ Data inventory and mapping: Board visibility into what personal data the organisation holds, where, how it is used, who can access it, and retention schedules
  • ✓ Vendor due diligence: Where data is processed by third-party processors, formal data processing agreements with liability, indemnification, and audit rights
📌 Board Governance

The DPDP Act does not explicitly impose board-level liability, but DPBI investigations increasingly demand evidence that boards understood data protection obligations. Directors and senior officers should be prepared to explain data protection controls directly to DPBI investigators.

Appeals from DPBI Orders (Section 25)

Section 25 of the DPDP Act provides a right of appeal to the appropriate High Court against DPBI orders. However, the appeal is limited to questions of law, not fact. If the DPBI found factual evidence of a breach, a fiduciary cannot appeal to the High Court to re-litigate the facts; it can only argue that the DPBI misinterpreted the law or exceeded its statutory authority.

Additionally, the appeal does not automatically stay DPBI's remedial orders or penalties. A fiduciary must apply separately for a stay, demonstrating strong grounds that compliance would cause irreparable harm. This means that even during appeals, organisations must often comply with DPBI directions.

High Court appeals have, in early cases under the DPDP Act, been rare. This suggests either strong DPBI decision-making or (more likely) the prohibitive cost of appellate litigation coupled with uncertainty of reversal.

IT Act 2000, Section 43A: Legacy Liability

Before the DPDP Act, the IT Act 2000, Section 43A imposed civil liability for unauthorised access or processing of personal data. Section 43A remains in force and applies alongside the DPDP Act. A fiduciary may face parallel liability:

  • ? DPDP Act penalties (DPBI) — imposed by the regulatory authority
  • ? IT Act Section 43A damages (civil court): Data principals can sue for compensation directly in civil courts, claiming actual damages or ₹5 lakh (whichever is higher) per affected person

This creates dual liability: a single breach can trigger both regulatory penalties (DPBI) and private damages claims (courts). A large-scale breach affecting millions could theoretically generate ₹5 lakh × 1 million = ₹500,000 crore in section 43A liability alone — a figure that dwarfs DPDP Act penalties.

In practice, class-action suits under section 43A remain rare due to litigation costs and proof difficulties, but the risk is real and growing as data-focused litigation expertise develops.

Food for Thought

The DPBI is not a paper regulator. It has investigation powers, penalty authority, and a statutory mandate to act independently. For data fiduciaries, DPBI oversight is not a compliance exercise — it is an operational reality that demands senior leadership engagement, investment in security and governance infrastructure, and cultural commitment to data protection.

"In the DPDP era, the question is not whether you will face DPBI scrutiny, but whether you will be prepared when you do."
