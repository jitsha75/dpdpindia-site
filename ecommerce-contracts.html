Foundational Law

Electronic Contracts — Validity under Indian Law

§ 10A IT Act 2000 (inserted by 2008 Amendment)

Where in a contract formation, the communication of proposals, the acceptance of proposals, the revocation of proposals and acceptances, as the case may be, are expressed in electronic form or by means of an electronic record, such contract shall not be deemed to be unenforceable solely on the ground that such electronic form or means was used for that purpose.

Section 10A resolves the foundational question: electronic form alone cannot void a contract. The contract must still satisfy all requirements of the Indian Contract Act 1872 — lawful proposal, acceptance, consideration, capacity, free consent, and lawful object — but electronic medium is not itself a disqualifier.

Key ICA Provisions Applied to Online Contracts

  • §4 ICA — Communication of proposal complete when it comes to knowledge of proposee; acceptance complete against proposer when put in course of transmission; complete against acceptor when acceptor has knowledge of it
  • §13 IT Act — Dispatch and receipt rules for electronic records: dispatch when the record enters a computer resource outside sender's control; receipt when it enters the designated information system of the addressee
  • §§5 and 10 IT Act — Electronic signatures satisfy writing and signature requirements for most contracts
Contract Types

Click-Wrap, Browse-Wrap & Shrink-Wrap

TypeMechanismEnforceability in India
Click-wrapUser clicks "I Agree" button after viewing T&CsGenerally enforceable — affirmative assent given
Browse-wrapT&Cs accessible via link; continued use = assentEnforceable only with conspicuous notice + opportunity to read; weaker without it (Specht v Netscape principle applied)
Shrink-wrapSoftware T&Cs inside box revealed on opening; use = acceptanceCautiously accepted; weaker than click-wrap; not authoritatively decided in India

The Conspicuous Notice Requirement for Browse-Wrap

Indian courts (following Specht v Netscape Communications, 2d Cir 2002) require that for browse-wrap to be enforceable, the link to T&Cs must be prominently placed in the user's line of sight — not buried below the fold. A "Download" button placed above the T&Cs link does not provide sufficient notice of the terms.

Landmark Judgments

Key Cases on Online Contract Formation

Supreme Court of India · (2010) 3 SCC 1

Trimex International FZE Ltd v Vedanta Aluminium Ltd

Facts: A contract for supply of bauxite was concluded through an exchange of emails between the parties. No formal signed agreement existed. When a dispute arose, the question was whether the email exchange constituted a binding contract.

Held: The email exchange constituted a valid binding contract. §10A IT Act + §§ 4 and 7 ICA together validate contracts concluded through electronic communication. A signed master agreement is not required where the essential terms are agreed by electronic correspondence.

Standard Indian Authority on Email Contracts
US Court of Appeals, 7th Circuit · 86 F.3d 1447 (1996)

ProCD Inc v Zeidenberg

Held: Shrink-wrap licence enforceable. User had opportunity to return the product after reading terms; choosing to use the software constitutes acceptance. Foundational US authority on machine-readable contracts, cited by Indian courts for its reasoning on assent.

Persuasive — US Authority
US Court of Appeals, 2nd Circuit · 306 F.3d 17 (2002)

Specht v Netscape Communications

Held: Browse-wrap unenforceable where the T&Cs link was placed below the "Download" button, outside the user's likely visual field. Lack of conspicuous notice = no assent. Indian courts regularly cite this for the conspicuous-notice requirement in digital T&Cs design.

Persuasive — Cited by Indian Courts
Supreme Court of India · (1966) 1 SCR 656

Bhagwandas Goverdhandas Kedia v Girdharilal Pashottamdas

Held: For instantaneous communication (phone), the postal rule does not apply; contract concluded at the place where acceptance is received and comes to the knowledge of the proposer. Pre-IT Act but foundational for online contract formation analysis.

Digital Payments

Payment & Settlement Systems Act 2007 — UPI & Digital Payments

§ 4 Payment & Settlement Systems Act 2007

No person other than the Reserve Bank shall commence or operate a payment system except under and in accordance with an authorisation issued by the Reserve Bank under the provisions of this Act.

All payment systems operating in India — UPI, IMPS, NEFT, RTGS, card networks, wallets — require PSSA authorisation. RBI is the apex regulator. NPCI operates the retail payment infrastructure as a §8 company under Companies Act, promoted by major banks.

Consumer Protection

Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules 2020

The CPC E-Commerce Rules 2020 (notified under Consumer Protection Act 2019) impose obligations on all e-commerce entities operating in India or serving Indian consumers:

Key Obligations on E-Commerce Entities

  • Disclosure: Legal name, address, contact details, CIN prominently on platform
  • Grievance redressal: Grievance officer must be appointed; complaint resolution within 48 hours of receipt, resolution in one month
  • No fake reviews: E-commerce entities cannot allow fake or manipulated customer reviews
  • Price transparency: Total price including all charges must be displayed before payment
  • Cancellation refunds: Refund within prescribed timelines for cancelled orders
  • Flash sales: Manipulative flash sales designed to discourage right of return are prohibited
  • No discrimination: Marketplace e-commerce entities cannot discriminate between sellers — same-tier sellers must receive same conditions
Data Protection Intersection

E-commerce entities handling customer data must comply with both the CPC E-Commerce Rules 2020 and the DPDP Act 2023. Consent obtained during checkout must satisfy the DPDP Act §§6–7 standards — bundled consent is specifically prohibited under DPDP Act Rule 3(1) 2025 draft rules.
