01

The IT (SPDI) Rules, 2011 — India's First Data Protection Norms

Section 43A of the IT Act and the Sensitive Personal Data or Information Rules

India's first modern data protection provisions came not through standalone legislation but through subordinate rules under the Information Technology Act, 2000. The IT (Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures and Sensitive Personal Data or Information) Rules, 2011 — commonly called the SPDI Rules — were notified on 11 April 2011 under s. 43A of the IT (Amendment) Act 2008.

The SPDI Rules introduced several concepts that would later mature into DPDP Act provisions:

  • Sensitive Personal Data or Information (SPDI): A defined category including passwords, financial information, health data, sexual orientation, medical records, and biometric data — analogous to the DPDP Act's forthcoming "special category" data.
  • Consent requirement: Collectors of SPDI must obtain prior written consent from the provider, stating the purpose of use.
  • Privacy policy: Bodies corporate must publish a privacy policy.
  • Transfer restrictions: SPDI may only be transferred to parties providing the same level of data protection.
  • Reasonable security practices: IS/ISO/IEC 27001 or equivalent standards mandated.
Critical Limitation: The SPDI Rules applied only to "bodies corporate" — private companies. Government agencies and public authorities were entirely outside their scope. This gap was a central motivation for comprehensive legislation.

The Rules were widely criticised for applying to SPDI collected electronically (consistent with the IT Act's scope) but excluding paper-based personal data, and for an ambiguous clarification in 2011 that made cross-border data transfer requirements unenforceable for outsourcing arrangements.

02

BN Srikrishna Committee & Expert Report (2017–18)

The foundational intellectual architecture of India's data protection law

Following the Puttaswamy judgment in August 2017, the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY) constituted a Committee of Experts on Data Protection under the chairmanship of retired Supreme Court Justice B.N. Srikrishna. The Committee comprised academics, technologists, industry representatives, and legal experts.

The Committee released a White Paper in November 2017 for public comments, received over 600 responses, conducted stakeholder consultations across India, and submitted its final Report and draft Bill — "A Free and Fair Digital Economy: Protecting Privacy, Empowering Indians" — to MeitY in July 2018.

Key recommendations in the Srikrishna Report:

  • A risk-based approach: obligations calibrated to the sensitivity of data and scale of processing.
  • A Data Protection Authority (DPA): an independent regulator with rule-making, adjudication and enforcement powers. (Note: This describes what earlier PDP Bill proposals called for. The enacted DPDP Act 2023 instead established a Board constituted by the Central Government, not a fully independent DPA.)
  • Data localisation: sensitive data to be stored in India; critical data to be processed only in India.
  • Purpose limitation and data minimisation as core principles.
  • An express right to be forgotten — the first Indian legislative proposal of this right.
  • Special provisions for children's data.
  • Government access to data subject to proportionality.
Doctrinal Innovation: The Report coined the concept of a "data fiduciary" — a body corporate or individual that determines the purpose and means of processing personal data — drawing on trust law analogies to impose a heightened duty of care toward data principals. This concept was adopted verbatim in the DPDP Act 2023.
03

Personal Data Protection Bills — 2018 to 2022

Four years of Parliamentary scrutiny, revision and withdrawal

JULY 2018
Draft PDP Bill 2018 — Srikrishna version
The Committee's draft Personal Data Protection Bill 2018 was submitted to MeitY. It closely followed the Committee's report — 112 sections covering data fiduciaries, data principals, the DPA, penalties, and cross-border transfer restrictions. Government kept the draft for revision.
DECEMBER 2019
PDP Bill 2019 introduced in Lok Sabha
The government's revised version was introduced in the Lok Sabha on 11 December 2019. Key changes from the 2018 draft: broader government exemptions (Clause 35 allowing blanket exemption for Central Government agencies); expanded data localisation requirements; social media platform obligations. Referred to a Joint Parliamentary Committee (JPC) the same day.
2019–2021
JPC deliberations — 81 sittings
The JPC, chaired by P.P. Chaudhary, held 81 sittings over two years. It examined over 170 written submissions and heard from regulators (SEBI, RBI, TRAI, UIDAI), industry bodies, and international experts including the UK ICO and EU regulators. The JPC's report was presented to Parliament in December 2021 — recommending 81 amendments and expanding the Bill's scope to include non-personal data.
AUGUST 2022
PDP Bill 2019 withdrawn
On 3 August 2022, the government withdrew the PDP Bill 2019 from Parliament — citing the need for a "comprehensive legal framework" that would address concerns raised during JPC deliberations. A new, simpler Bill was promised. This was widely seen as acknowledgment that the 2019 Bill had become too complex and politically contentious.
NOVEMBER 2022
Draft DPDP Bill 2022 — public consultation
MeitY released a new draft Digital Personal Data Protection Bill for public comment — drastically simplified to 30 clauses. The "non-personal data" provisions were dropped. Data localisation was de-emphasised. The DPA was replaced by the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI). The Bill adopted a principles-based approach, with details to be specified in Rules.
AUGUST 2023
DPDP Act 2023 enacted
The Digital Personal Data Protection Bill 2023 was passed by Lok Sabha on 7 August 2023, Rajya Sabha on 9 August 2023, and received Presidential assent on 11 August 2023. Published in the Gazette of India on 11 August 2023 (No. 22 of 2023). 44 sections. Came into force pending Rules.
04

DPDP Act 2023 — Key Structural Choices

What changed from earlier Bills and why

The DPDP Act 2023 represented a significant departure from the JPC-revised PDP Bill 2019 in both structure and philosophy:

  • Simplicity: 44 sections vs. 99 sections in the JPC-revised 2019 Bill. Details delegated to Rules — giving the government flexibility without legislative amendments.
  • No non-personal data: The Act covers only digital personal data, dropping the contentious non-personal data framework.
  • Consent Manager: A new category of intermediary — a Consent Manager (s. 2(g)) — to enable individuals to manage their consent across Data Fiduciaries.
  • Data Protection Board of India: The DPBI (s. 18) replaces the earlier DPA concept — styled as a quasi-judicial body rather than a full regulatory authority with rule-making powers.
  • Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDF): A risk-based category (s. 10) subject to enhanced obligations including Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs), data audits, and Data Protection Officers (DPOs).
  • Government exemptions (s. 17): Broader than the Srikrishna draft — allowing the Central Government to exempt any entity or class of entities by notification. Legally controversial but politically necessary.
Pending Commencement: As of publication, the DPDP Act 2023 has not been fully brought into force. The Central Government must notify the date(s) of commencement of different provisions under s. 1(2). The DPDP Rules 2025 have been notified (November 2025) but full enforcement is expected to be phased.
05

DPDP Rules, 2025 — Notified November 2025

Full operationalisation of the DPDP Act after nationwide consultations

The Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025 were notified on 14 November 2025 — completing the legislative framework initiated by the DPDP Act 2023. The Rules were finalised after a nationwide consultation process that received 6,915 public inputs, with physical consultations in Delhi, Mumbai, Guwahati, Kolkata, Hyderabad, Bengaluru and Chennai.

Key provisions of the DPDP Rules 2025:

  • Notice format: Data Fiduciaries must provide notices in plain language, available in all 22 scheduled languages listed in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution.
  • Consent Manager registration: Consent Managers to be registered with the Data Protection Board of India — minimum net worth requirements and technical standards prescribed.
  • Children's data protections: Verifiable parental consent mechanism prescribed — Fiduciaries must use "such measures as may be prescribed" to verify that the person providing consent is a parent or guardian.
  • Data Protection Officer: SDFs must appoint a DPO resident in India, responsible and accountable to the board of directors.
  • Cross-border transfers: Allowed to countries not restricted by Central Government notification — a "whitelist" approach replacing earlier "blacklist" proposals.
  • DPBI procedures: Complaints, investigation procedures, and adjudication timelines specified.
Citizen-centric design: The Rules introduce a Digital Locker concept for exercising rights — individuals can submit requests through a government-notified platform, with fiduciaries required to respond within prescribed timelines (typically 48 hours for critical requests, 30 days for others).
06

The Road Ahead — 2025 to 2027

Implementation milestones and the journey to full enforcement

With the Rules notified in November 2025, the focus shifts to implementation. Key milestones expected through 2027:

  • Data Protection Board of India constituted: The Central Government must notify the Chairperson and Members of the DPBI. The Board will be the primary enforcement body.
  • SDF notifications: The Central Government must notify which entities qualify as Significant Data Fiduciaries — expected in 2026, likely targeting large social media platforms, major e-commerce entities, and health/financial data processors.
  • Consent Manager ecosystem: Registration of Consent Managers expected to begin in 2026 — this will create a new regulated industry of consent intermediaries.
  • Phased enforcement: MeitY has signalled a phased approach — initial focus on capacity building and voluntary compliance, followed by formal enforcement beginning 2026-27.
  • Cross-border whitelist: The list of countries to which personal data may be freely transferred to be published. Countries not on the list will require contractual safeguards.
  • Amendment to IT Act: The DPDP Act 2023 amends s. 43A of the IT Act and the SPDI Rules will stand superseded once the DPDP Act is fully brought into force.
2011–27 in perspective: India's data protection journey spans 16 years from the SPDI Rules (2011) to projected full enforcement (2027). This is comparable to the EU's GDPR journey — the first EU data protection directive was adopted in 1995, GDPR became effective in 2018 — though India's timeline compressed the most controversial debates into a shorter period.
