Supreme Court at the Constitutional Crossroads: Privacy vs. Transparency — The DPDP Act's Section 44(3) Collision with the RTI Act Referred to Larger Bench

The most consequential digital governance litigation in India's post-Puttaswamy era is now before the Supreme Court. Consolidated writ petitions filed under Article 32 by RTI activists Venkatesh Nayak, Geeta Seshu, the National Campaign for Peoples Right to Information, and the Reporters Collective challenge core provisions of the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 — arguing it unconstitutionally erodes transparency and enables a state surveillance apparatus.

The central battleground is Section 44(3) of the DPDP Act, which amends Section 8(1)(j) of the RTI Act by replacing a nuanced three-pronged balancing test — public activity nexus, unwarranted invasion test, and a mandatory public interest override — with a blunt, blanket prohibition on disclosing "any information which relates to personal information." The petitioners argue this fails the proportionality doctrine established by the nine-judge bench in Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017), as it forecloses accountability for public functionaries behind a privacy shield.

The consolidated petitions also challenge Sections 17(1)(c) and 17(2) — granting the Central Government extraordinarily broad powers to exempt state instrumentalities from the Act's obligations — and Section 36, which allows the Government to demand data from the DPBI or data fiduciaries without bounded statutory limits. Rules 17 and 23(2) of the DPDP Rules, 2025 are challenged on separation of powers grounds, given executive dominance in DPBI member selection. The Supreme Court bench of Chief Justice Surya Kant, Justice Joymalya Bagchi, and Justice V.M. Pancholi heard the matter on February 16, March 23, and May 13, 2026 — declining to stay Section 44(3) and referring the constitutional questions to a larger bench. The Foundation of Data Protection Professionals in India (FDPPI) has filed an intervention petition defending the DPDP Act's core framework.

Compliance Deadline · Urgent May 2026 — MeitY Stakeholder Consultation
MeitY Compresses SDF Compliance Window by Six Months — Significant Data Fiduciaries Must Achieve Full DPDP Compliance by 13 November 2026

Following internal stakeholder consultations — including a January 2026 industry session attended by major technology enterprises, global capability centres (GCCs), and multinationals — the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY) has formally proposed slashing the compliance runway for Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs) from 18 months to 12 months. The revised hard deadline is 13 November 2026, six months earlier than the originally envisaged May 2027 date.

MeitY's rationale is pragmatic: many organisations that will be designated as SDFs — major social media platforms, large financial institutions, telecom providers, search engines, AI system operators, and entities processing vast quantities of sensitive behavioural data or children's data — already operate under the EU's GDPR and have the compliance infrastructure to move faster. The accelerated timeline will apply simultaneously with the formal notification of cross-border data transfer restrictions and the SDF designation criteria.

Under the compressed framework, designated SDFs must immediately operationalise: consent management architectures with granular purpose limitation; automated data purging workflows including the three-year limit for inactive data; cryptographically verifiable parental consent mechanisms for users under 18; robust data principal rights fulfilment infrastructure; DPIA completion for all high-risk processing activities; and the appointment of a Data Protection Officer based in India. Organisations not yet classified as SDFs will also face accelerating compliance expectations as the DPBI becomes operational.

Sources: Chambers & Partners — MeitY Timeline · S.S. Rana — Analysis · Business Standard
Implication for Practitioners

If your organisation processes data for more than 10,000 Indian data principals or handles sensitive categories (health, financial, biometric, children's data), assume SDF designation is imminent. The November 2026 deadline leaves approximately five months from now. Enterprises should treat this as a product engineering sprint — consent management, DPIA completion, and data deletion automation cannot be retrofitted at the last minute. Engage an AIDAI-accredited Independent Data Auditor for a gap assessment immediately.

Government · Enforcement Machinery 6 May 2026 — MeitY Gazette Notification
DPBI Recruitment Launched: MeitY Invites Applications for Chairperson and Four Members — India's Enforcement Era Formally Begins

On 6 May 2026, MeitY published a formal gazette notification (F. No. 2(1)/2026-Pers.I) inviting applications for the powerful offices of the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) Chairperson and four Member positions. The recruitment signals that the digital-by-design enforcement authority — statutorily mandated under Section 18 of the DPDP Act — will be fully constituted and adjudicating cases by late Q3 or early Q4 2026, perfectly aligned with the November SDF deadline. A Cabinet Secretary-led search panel will oversee the selection process.

The qualification bar is exceptionally high. The Chairperson must be equivalent in rank to an Additional Secretary to the Government of India, possess at least five years' expertise in data governance, digital economy, or techno-regulation, be at least 55 years of age, and will receive ₹4.5 lakh per month consolidated. Each of the four Members must be equivalent to a Joint Secretary, similarly aged, with at least one required to be a formal legal expert, receiving ₹4 lakh per month. All appointees are subject to a mandatory one-year cooling-off period before accepting private sector roles — a structural safeguard against regulatory capture. The application window closes in early June 2026.

The Digital India Corporation (DIC) — a Section 8 company under MeitY — simultaneously launched recruitment for Techno-Legal Consultants to staff the DPBI's physical "Digital Office" in Delhi/NCR. The digital-first office architecture envisions API-driven breach notification portals, automated adjudication pipelines, and grievance redressal systems operating within the proposed 72-hour breach reporting window.

Sources: MeitY Notification (PDF) · NASSCOM — DPBI Update · Storyboard18 · Naavi.org — DPBI Analysis
Implication for Practitioners

The DPBI's constitution is the single most important structural development in India's data protection enforcement landscape. Once constituted, the Board will have powers to investigate data breaches, issue binding directions, levy penalties up to ₹250 crore per violation, and order compensation to data principals. Enterprises that have not yet conducted formal breach notification readiness assessments — including incident response playbooks and the 72-hour notification workflow — are now operating in a high-risk posture.

New Professional Ecosystem 23 May 2026 — AIDAI National Webinar
AIDAI Formalises Empanelment: Three-Tier Auditor Certification Structure Targets CAs, CSs, Advocates and Infosec Professionals — DGPSI-GDPR Framework Launched for Dual Compliance

The Association of Independent Data Auditors of India (AIDAI) — established by the Foundation of Data Protection Professionals in India (FDPPI) in April 2026 — hosted a major national webinar on 23 May 2026 to formalise its professional empanelment structure. The three-tier certification model explicitly targets established practitioners in finance, law, and information security, offering a structured pathway into India's emerging data audit profession.

The tiers are: Tier 1 – Probationary Independent Data Auditor (PIDA) at ₹3,540 per annum (inclusive of GST) — open to all entrants, providing mentorship, lab access, and standardised audit materials; Tier 2 – Accredited Independent Data Auditor (AIDA) at ₹7,080 — for practitioners with existing privacy, infosec, legal, CA, CS, ICMA, or ISO Lead Auditor credentials; and Tier 3 – Certified Independent Data Auditor (CIDA) at ₹11,800 — requiring successful completion of the rigorous FDPPI CIDA examination or the legacy CDPODA credential, granting priority allocation for large SDF audits and exclusive authority to lead multi-auditor engagements.

Simultaneously, FDPPI launched the DGPSI-GDPR framework — an adaptation of 50 core Digital Governance and Protection Standard of India (DGPSI) specifications — enabling Indian IT firms and GCCs processing EU data to execute dual India-EU compliance using a domestically regulated audit framework. A new "Master of GDPR Compliance" certification was also announced, targeting compliance officers in India's technology export sector. DGPSI-Lite (for MSMEs), DGPSI-AI (for ML deployments), and DGPSI-HR (for HR data systems) continue to expand the standard's sectoral coverage.

Sources: FDPPI — AIDAI Webinar · FDPPI — New Certification 2026 · FDPPI.in
Implication for Practitioners

Chartered Accountants, Company Secretaries, and practising advocates with corporate clients in the technology, fintech, or healthcare sectors should treat AIDAI empanelment as a strategic practice expansion — not an elective. The DPDP Act's mandatory independent audit requirement for SDFs will create a substantial, recurring compliance services market. The DGPSI-GDPR framework is especially valuable for advocates advising GCCs and IT exporters who must simultaneously satisfy Indian and EU regulators — a rapidly growing client category.

Supreme Court · Corporate Liability 2026 — Ongoing Appeals
Supreme Court Condemns Meta's "Take-It-or-Leave-It" Consent Model — Appeals by Meta/WhatsApp and CCI Heard; Court Reaffirms Privacy as Non-Negotiable Fundamental Right

Parallel to the DPDP Act constitutional challenge, the Supreme Court is simultaneously hearing appeals filed by Meta/WhatsApp and the Competition Commission of India (CCI) regarding WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy and data-sharing framework with Meta. During the ongoing 2026 proceedings, the apex court has issued a sharp judicial rebuke against the deployment of "take-it-or-leave-it" consent models by global technology monopolies.

The Court has emphatically stressed that privacy is a fundamental human right — building on the Puttaswamy constitutional foundation — and that multinational corporations operating in India must provide real, uncoerced choice. Platform architectures that artificially pressure users into accepting expanded data sharing as the mandatory condition of digital access are incompatible with Indian constitutional norms. The judgment, when finalised, is expected to become the foundational corporate liability precedent for consent architecture design under the DPDP Act's "free, specific, informed, unconditional and unambiguous" consent standard.

Sources: Singhania Law — Privacy & Competition · LiveLaw — SC DPDP Hearing
Implication for Practitioners

Any enterprise currently bundling consent for unrelated data processing purposes into a single "accept all" framework — or conditioning app access on agreement to broad data sharing — should immediately audit their consent UX against the DPDP Act's granularity requirements. The Supreme Court's pending judgment against Meta will establish the judicial standard for what constitutes impermissible consent coercion under Indian law.

CERT-In · Critical Advisory 25 May 2026
CERT-In Mandates 12-Hour Patch Window: AI-Assisted Cyberattacks Compress Exploitation from Weeks to Hours — New Blueprint Redefines Enterprise Security Standards of Care

On 25 May 2026, the Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In) released a landmark 38-page advisory titled "Blueprint for Reducing Exposure and Defending against AI-Assisted Vulnerabilities Exploitation in Digital Infrastructure." The document reflects a paradigm-defining threat intelligence finding: sophisticated cyber threat actors now deploy Large Language Models (LLMs) and autonomous AI agents to compress the vulnerability exploitation timeline from weeks to mere hours after public disclosure.

The Blueprint establishes a highly aggressive, risk-tiered remediation framework that effectively sets a new de facto standard of care for enterprise security:

While the Blueprint operates as a strong regulatory recommendation — alongside the existing legally binding 6-hour cyber incident reporting obligation under the IT Act — non-adherence will directly expose CISOs and corporate officers to civil and criminal liability under the DPDP Act's enhanced penalty regime when a breach results. The document mandates a strategic shift from periodic penetration testing to continuous AI-enabled exposure management and adaptive defence architectures.

Sources: CyberSecurityNews — CERT-In 12-Hour Rule · Industrial Cyber — CERT-In AI Threats · CSO Online · Indian Express — Key Takeaways
Implication for Practitioners

The 12-hour window is a product engineering requirement, not a legal aspiration. Organisations must re-architect their vulnerability management workflows to support automated alert-to-patch pipelines for internet-facing systems. CISOs presenting cybersecurity posture to Boards should formally incorporate the CERT-In Blueprint's tiered windows into their risk registers — non-compliance will be the first thing the DPBI examines when investigating a data breach. Continuous threat exposure management (CTEM) platforms are no longer optional for any SDF-class organisation.

IT Rules · In Force In Force: 20 February 2026
IT Rules 2026 Amendment in Force: India's "Active Moderation" Regime for Deepfakes — 3-Hour Takedowns, Cryptographic Metadata, and Instant Safe Harbour Loss for Non-Compliance

The Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Amendment Rules, 2026, notified on 10 February 2026 and in full legal force from 20 February 2026, fundamentally rewrite the operating obligations of digital platforms relying on the Section 79 safe harbour. The Amendment transitions India from a reactive "due diligence" model to a proactive Active Moderation regime.

The rules introduce a formal legal definition of Synthetically Generated Information (SGI) under Rule 2(1)(wa) — audio, visual, or audio-visual content artificially created, modified, or altered using computer resources so as to deceive an ordinary user. Platforms are now required to implement mandatory pre-publication checkpoints requiring users to declare SGI uploads, deploy automated algorithmic verification tools to detect undeclared deepfakes before virality, and embed permanent cryptographic metadata with unique identifiers tracing every piece of synthetic content to its platform origin.

Takedown timelines are ruthlessly compressed: general unlawful content must be removed within 3 hours of a valid order; Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery (NCII) — deepfake-based or otherwise — must be eradicated within 2 hours. Complaint acknowledgement windows are halved from 15 to 7 days. All moderation notices must be maintained in English and all 22 Eighth Schedule languages. Significant Social Media Intermediaries (SSMIs) with over 5 million Indian users face the most intensive technical obligations. Failure to comply instantly strips Section 79 Safe Harbour, exposing platform executives to direct criminal prosecution under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita and civil damages.

Sources: Mondaq — IT Rules 2026 Analysis · LiveLaw — Platform Liability · India Policy Hub — Definitive Guide
Implication for Practitioners

For legal counsel advising digital platforms, the Safe Harbour loss provision is existential — a single non-compliant response to a deepfake NCII takedown notice can eliminate platform-level liability immunity and expose individual executives to BNS prosecution. Immediate actions: audit grievance officer capacity for the 7-day acknowledgement requirement; deploy or contract AI-based deepfake detection tools; implement cryptographic watermarking for SGI; and establish a 24/7 NCII escalation pathway capable of meeting the 2-hour window. These are engineering mandates, not policy commitments.

Landmark Judgments on E-Governance & Cyber Rights
Allahabad High Court 15 May 2026 — Smt. Shraddha Pathak v. State of U.P. & Ors.
Allahabad High Court Issues Show-Cause for Damages After Cyber Cell Deactivates Wife's Mobile Number on Bogus Matrimonial Complaint — Sets Precedent for Automated Enforcement Accountability

A Division Bench of the Allahabad High Court comprising Justice J.J. Munir and Justice Tarun Saxena delivered a landmark accountability ruling on 15 May 2026 in Smt. Shraddha Pathak v. State of U.P. and 4 Others. The case arose from a matrimonial dispute in which a husband filed a fabricated cybercrime complaint against his wife. Acting mechanically — entirely devoid of independent verification — the state cyber cell issued an order to the telecom operator to instantly deactivate the wife's mobile number, severing her primary digital identity and access to financial and government services.

The High Court expressed severe judicial displeasure with the "callous handling by State Agencies." Critically, the Court went beyond merely ordering the restoration of the mobile connection: it issued a formal show-cause notice demanding that the State explain why financial damages should not be directly awarded to the petitioner for the arbitrary deprivation of her digital rights under Article 21 of the Constitution. The ruling establishes a vital precedent: as cyber enforcement becomes increasingly automated and deeply integrated with telecom infrastructure, state instrumentalities that bypass principles of natural justice cannot shelter behind the opacity of a "cyber investigation." Judicial oversight and due diligence remain constitutionally non-negotiable regardless of the urgency of digital enforcement.

Sources: Law Beat — Allahabad HC Ruling · Bar & Bench — Cyber Cell Accountability
Implication for Practitioners

Law enforcement agencies and telecom compliance officers must note: this judgment signals that financial liability for arbitrary digital enforcement actions is now a live judicial risk, not a theoretical one. Organisations receiving cyber cell orders to deactivate SIMs or block accounts without a court order should maintain documented protocols for verifying the legal basis of such instructions before acting — and should provide a rapid grievance pathway for those wrongfully affected. Advocates handling matrimonial or commercial disputes should anticipate misuse of the cybercrime reporting infrastructure and prepare pre-emptive writ remedies.

Bombay High Court 8 May 2026 — WP No. 10771 of 2025
Bombay High Court Rules: Citizens Cannot Be Left Without Remedy for Aadhaar Biometric Mismatches — UIDAI Must Maintain Human-in-the-Loop Fallback Mechanisms

In a significant ruling on 8 May 2026 (WP No. 10771 of 2025), the Bombay High Court addressed a growing systemic challenge to digital governance — unexplained biometric mismatches within the Aadhaar authentication system that prevent citizens from accessing government services. The Division Bench ruled that Indian citizens cannot be left without an effective administrative or legal remedy simply because a centralized computer system registers a biometric mismatch.

The Court was emphatic that this protection is absolute, particularly where there is no allegation of fraud, identity impersonation, or suppression of facts. The judgment strikes at technological determinism within e-governance: the state cannot absolve itself of constitutional service delivery obligations by pointing to an opaque database error. UIDAI and all related administrative bodies are legally required to maintain robust, accessible, human-in-the-loop fallback mechanisms to resolve technical anomalies without disenfranchising lawful residents — irrespective of how automated the primary authentication system has become.

Sources: Bombay HC Judgment (PDF)
Implication for Practitioners

For organisations using Aadhaar-based KYC — banks, NBFCs, telecom operators, InsurTechs — this judgment mandates that biometric failure at the authentication layer cannot trigger automatic service denial without a manual review pathway. KYC workflows must incorporate an explicit "biometric failure escalation" process with a documented human review step and a defined resolution SLA. This is also directly relevant to DPDP Act compliance: denying access to services based on erroneous automated processing of biometric data (a sensitive personal data category) creates direct penalty exposure.

Delhi High Court 22 May 2026 — WP(CRL) 673/2024
Delhi High Court Reviews Aadhaar Verification for Prisoners and Sureties — UIDAI-MHA API Deployment Expanding Across All Court Premises to Eliminate Fraudulent Bail Sureties

On 22 May 2026, the Delhi High Court examined implementation of a directive for mandatory biometric verification of prisoners and jail visitors under the Aadhaar Authentication for Good Governance Rules, 2020. UIDAI has granted formal API approval to the Ministry of Home Affairs to verify Aadhaar credentials of inmates and mulakat (jail visit) applicants nationally. This implementation — building on a Punjab and Haryana High Court directive of May 2024 — is now being expanded across all court premises to verify the identity of bail sureties in real time.

The push for biometric authentication within judicial premises targets the chronic systemic problem of fraudulent professional sureties — a corrupt practice where individuals present false identities or fabricated asset claims to secure bail for accused persons. Real-time Aadhaar authentication eliminates this vector, significantly enhancing the integrity and efficiency of judicial release processes across the criminal justice system.

Sources: Delhi HC Order (PDF)
Implication for Practitioners

Criminal law practitioners should prepare their clients and professional sureties for mandatory Aadhaar verification at court premises — outdated or unlinked mobile numbers will cause authentication failures and delay bail processes. For UIDAI, this expansion into the judicial ecosystem requires robust uptime guarantees and a clear failure-fallback protocol consistent with the Bombay HC's human-in-the-loop mandate.

UIDAI · Strategic Partnership 5 May 2026 — Ahmedabad
UIDAI Signs 5-Year MoU with National Forensic Sciences University — Aadhaar's Digital Fortress Upgraded Through Academic Forensic Intelligence and AI Deepfake Detection Research

On 5 May 2026 in Ahmedabad, UIDAI CEO Vivek Chandra Verma and NFSU Director (Gujarat Campus) Prof. S.O. Junare signed a transformative five-year MoU that fundamentally upgrades Aadhaar's defensive cybersecurity posture. The collaboration integrates advanced academic digital forensics directly into the operational security of India's digital identity backbone.

The six strategic pillars of the partnership include: academic curriculum development in data protection; advanced information security and system integrity protocols; forensic laboratory infrastructure expansion; real-time cybersecurity technical support; joint research in emerging technologies including AI, blockchain, cryptography, and deepfake detection; and student outreach creating a pipeline of forensic talent into UIDAI's security operations. NFSU students will gain classified practical exposure to UIDAI infrastructure projects — building the human capital required to defend the nation's Digital Public Infrastructure against state-sponsored espionage and organised cybercriminal syndicates.

The partnership marks UIDAI's strategic transition from static perimeter defence to continuous forensic-grade threat hunting — an essential posture given that the Aadhaar database represents India's most critical identity asset, underpinning banking, telecom, government benefits, and increasingly the criminal justice system.

Sources: UIDAI Official Press Release · PIB — UIDAI-NFSU MoU · The Cyber Express · Drishti IAS — Analysis
Implication for Practitioners

The deepfake detection research component is directly relevant to DPDP Act compliance: synthetic identity fraud — using AI-generated facial imagery to bypass Aadhaar-linked KYC — is an emerging threat vector for banks and fintechs. Compliance teams should monitor UIDAI's forthcoming guidance on deepfake-resistant authentication standards that will likely emerge from this research partnership. NFSU's forensic frameworks may also become the baseline for DPBI-mandated security audits of biometric data processing systems.

State Law Enforcement · Karnataka June 2026 — DGP Pronab Mohanty
Karnataka Cyber Command: India's First Multi-Agency Fusion Platform Targets Mule Account Networks — ₹85 Crore Linked, 869 Accounts Identified, 68 Arrested in Statewide Drive

Karnataka's Cyber Command — operationalised under the strategic direction of DGP Pronab Mohanty and positioned as India's first dedicated multi-agency cyber fusion platform — has made mule account disruption the centrepiece of its enforcement strategy. Mule accounts are genuine bank accounts whose owners surrender control (including ATM cards, SIM cards, passbooks, and net banking access) to cybercriminal syndicates in exchange for payment, providing the critical domestic financial infrastructure for laundering transnational fraud proceeds.

Between December 2025 and February 2026, a statewide special drive under Cyber Command registered 60 cases, arrested 68 individuals, identified 869 mule accounts linked to 8,788 complaints on the National Cybercrime Reporting Portal, and traced ₹85.05 crore in linked crime proceeds — freezing ₹13.43 crore. DGP Mohanty's insight: "Targeting mule accounts can significantly weaken cybercrime networks" — because foreign threat actors and transnational syndicates cannot operate without domestic financial liquidity infrastructure. By attacking the money movement layer via the 1930 NCRP pipeline, law enforcement introduces insurmountable friction into cybercrime's business model.

The Cyber Command integrates cyber hygiene outreach, elite enforcement capacity, advanced threat intelligence, and public awareness into a unified operational command — a model that MHA is evaluating for national replication.

Sources: New Indian Express — DGP Mohanty Interview · The420.in — Mule Account Networks · Tribune India — Karnataka Crackdown
Implication for Practitioners

Banks and payment service providers have a direct compliance obligation under RBI's anti-money laundering framework to detect and report mule account activity — and the DPDP Act's security safeguard requirements further demand that financial data processed in fraud detection contexts is handled with appropriate technical and organisational measures. Compliance officers should review their transaction monitoring algorithms for mule account red flags (multiple inbound transfers immediately followed by full outward withdrawals, newly-KYC'd accounts receiving high-value credits) and ensure 1930 portal reporting workflows are operationalised.

Government Capacity · NIC / NICSI Mid-2026
NIC Notifies 243 Scientist-B Posts in AI, Data Science and Cybersecurity — NICSI Internship 2026 Builds Digital Governance Talent Pipeline Across AI, eKYC and Cyber Forensics Tracks

The National Informatics Centre (NIC) — India's government technology backbone — has issued recruitment notifications for 243 permanent Group-A (Gazetted) Scientist-B positions in Computer Science, Information Technology, Electronics, and critically, Data Science and Artificial Intelligence. This large-scale talent acquisition is directly essential for sustaining engineering requirements of national-scale sovereign projects: e-Governance platforms, cybersecurity monitoring of government infrastructure, and the sovereign AI compute initiative under the IndiaAI Mission.

Simultaneously, the National Informatics Centre Services Incorporated (NICSI) — a Section 8 company under MeitY — concluded its Summer Internship Programme 2026, providing top-tier university students with hands-on exposure to live government technology systems across AI, eKYC, cybersecurity, and digital governance architecture tracks. The programme reflects the government's long-term strategy of cultivating a digitally native, sovereignty-oriented public sector workforce — essential for maintaining India's technology stack independently as DPDP Act obligations extend to government data fiduciaries under the Act's phased implementation.

Sources: JobYaari — NIC Recruitment 2026 · NICSI Internship Portal · Times of India — NICSI Internship
Implication for Practitioners

The NIC's AI and Data Science recruitment directly supports the DPBI's anticipated digital-first adjudication infrastructure. As government bodies increasingly function as data fiduciaries under the DPDP Act, the NIC's internal technical capacity determines how effectively the state can self-audit compliance. Law and compliance professionals advising government entities on DPDP Act applicability — particularly regarding the Act's qualified exemptions for state instrumentalities — should factor in this accelerating internal technical sophistication when calibrating regulatory expectations.

On The Horizon · Advanced Drafting Stage
Digital India Act (DIA): The IT Act 2000's Successor Approaches — Adtech Regulation, User Harm Criminalisation, and the Platform Liability Overhaul

The highly anticipated Digital India Act (DIA) — designed to comprehensively replace the Information Technology Act, 2000 — remains in advanced drafting stages following multiple rounds of MeitY stakeholder consultations. The DIA focuses on three transformative pillars: user harm criminalisation (creating specific offences for online abuse, non-consensual deepfakes, and digital fraud beyond existing BNS coverage); adtech monopoly regulation (recalibrating revenue dynamics between global advertising giants and Indian content creators); and a comprehensive platform liability framework that supersedes the IT Rules 2026 amendment once enacted.

The IT Rules 2026 active moderation framework is explicitly designed as the tactical precursor and bridging mechanism until the DIA comes into force. Practitioners advising platform clients should structure current compliance investments as building blocks toward DIA readiness — rather than parallel, incompatible frameworks.
