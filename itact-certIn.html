CERT-In (S.70B) & NCIIPC (S.70A) — India's Cybersecurity Architecture

India's cybersecurity governance rests on two statutory pillars — CERT-In (Indian Computer Emergency Response Team, Section 70B) and NCIIPC (National Critical Information Infrastructure Protection Centre, Section 70A). This article analyses their mandates, the landmark 2022 CERT-In Directions, and how they interact with the DPDP Act's breach notification obligations.

01

India's Cybersecurity Architecture: Overview

Two pillars of institutional cyber governance

India's cybersecurity governance is built on two pillars established by the IT Act. Together, these agencies form the institutional backbone of India's response to cyber threats — from individual company data breaches to attacks on national critical infrastructure. Their mandates are complementary but distinct: CERT-In handles incident response across all sectors; NCIIPC focuses on protecting the most critical systems.

Section 70B
CERT-In — National Cybersecurity Agency
National nodal agency for cybersecurity incidents. Under MeitY. Issues security advisories, coordinates incident response, mandates 6-hour incident reporting. 2022 Directions expanded obligations significantly.
Section 70A
NCIIPC — Critical Infrastructure Protection
Protects Critical Information Infrastructure — power, banking, telecom, transport, energy. Operates under the PMO (National Technical Research Organisation / NTRO).
International coordination: CERT-In is also India's national Point of Contact (PoC) for international cybersecurity cooperation — it coordinates with counterpart agencies (US-CISA, UK-NCSC, ENISA) and participates in global forums like the Forum of Incident Response and Security Teams (FIRST).
02

CERT-In: Mandate & Powers (Section 70B)

India's national nodal agency for cybersecurity incident response

CERT-In was established as India's national nodal agency for cybersecurity under Section 70B (inserted by the 2008 Amendment). Under MeitY.

Core statutory functions:

Issue security advisories, vulnerability notes, and threat alerts
Coordinate responses to cybersecurity incidents at national level
Collect, analyse, and disseminate information on cyber threats and incidents
Forecast and provide early warning of imminent cybersecurity incidents
Take emergency measures for handling critical cybersecurity incidents
Coordinate cyber incident response activities across government and private sectors
Conduct and coordinate forensic analysis of IT systems

Powers: CERT-In may call for information from any service provider, intermediary, data centre, body corporate, or government entity. It can issue binding directions for security audit compliance, forensic analysis, and incident response. Non-compliance with CERT-In directions is punishable under the IT Act.

03

The 2022 CERT-In Directions

Landmark mandatory directions issued under Section 70B(6)

On 28 April 2022, CERT-In issued landmark mandatory directions under Section 70B(6) of the IT Act. These came into force on 27 June 2022 and significantly expanded compliance obligations.

Obligation Requirement Timeline/Duration
Incident Reporting Report 50 categories of cybersecurity incidents to CERT-In Within 6 hours of detection or being informed
Log Retention Maintain logs of all ICT systems and network devices 180 days (rolling); must be provided on CERT-In request
NTP Synchronisation Sync all ICT system clocks with NTP servers of NIC or NPTEL in India Ongoing compliance
VPN Provider KYC Maintain validated subscriber names, addresses, contact numbers, IPs, usage patterns 5 years; must share on request
Cloud Service Provider KYC Maintain KYC details of subscribers 5 years
Data Centre KYC Subscriber details including purpose of use 5 years

Covered entities: Service providers, intermediaries, data centres, body corporates, and government organisations in India.

Reportable incident categories (key examples): Targeted scanning/probing of critical networks, compromise of critical systems, unauthorised access to IT systems, website defacement, malware/ransomware deployment, DDoS attacks, attacks on internet infrastructure, data breaches involving personal data, attacks on critical infrastructure, IoT device compromise, and supply chain attacks.

6-hour reporting timeline: The 6-hour incident reporting requirement is among the shortest mandatory timelines globally. EU's NIS2 Directive allows 24 hours for early warning and 72 hours for initial notification. The 6-hour window places significant demands on incident response teams — organisations should have pre-drafted incident report templates and a designated CERT-In reporting officer.
Data localisation and compliance risk: Non-compliance with the 2022 Directions is punishable under the IT Act. Data localisation requirements for logs and KYC (must be stored in India) have implications for multi-national organisations with centralised log management outside India.
04

NCIIPC & Protected Systems (S.70 and S.70A)

Critical information infrastructure protection framework

Section 70 — Protected Systems: The Central Government may designate any computer resource as a "Protected System" by official notification. Unauthorised access to a Protected System is punishable with imprisonment up to 10 years plus fine. Notified Protected Systems span power grids, banking networks, critical government infrastructure, and defence systems.

Section 70A — NCIIPC: The National Critical Information Infrastructure Protection Centre was established under Section 70A as the national nodal agency for protection of Critical Information Infrastructure (CII). NCIIPC operates under the National Technical Research Organisation (NTRO) under the PMO.

What constitutes CII: Computer resources whose incapacitation or destruction would have a debilitating impact on national security, economy, public health, or safety.

Tier 1 Infrastructure
Power & Energy
National power grids, generation and distribution systems.

NCIIPC functions:

Identify, classify, and notify Critical Information Infrastructure
Issue guidelines and directions to CII operators
Coordinate vulnerability disclosure and patch management
Threat intelligence sharing with sector-specific CERTs and operators
Conduct CII protection exercises (like Synergy exercises)
05

Incident Reporting: Obligations & Timelines

Who must report to CERT-In and how

Who must report to CERT-In:

All service providers, intermediaries, data centres, body corporates
Government organisations
VPN service providers (including corporate VPNs)
Cloud service providers (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS)
Virtual asset service providers, virtual asset exchange providers, custodian wallet providers

How to report: CERT-In online portal (https://incident.cert-in.org.in) or email. Structured incident report form must be submitted.

Best practice for compliance: Designate a CERT-In Reporting Officer (CRO) within your organisation; maintain a pre-filled incident report template for each of the 50 reportable categories; integrate CERT-In reporting into your Security Incident Response Plan (SIRP); test the reporting workflow quarterly.

CERT-In vs DPDP Act: Data Breach Overlap

Concurrent obligations in personal data breach scenarios

A significant personal data breach will typically trigger obligations under BOTH frameworks simultaneously. Organisations must be prepared for concurrent compliance.

Aspect CERT-In Directions 2022 DPDP Act 2023 — S.8(6)
Who must notify Service providers, intermediaries, data centres, body corporates Data Fiduciaries
Notify whom CERT-In Data Protection Board + affected Data Principals
Timeline 6 hours from detection As prescribed in Rules (not yet notified)
Scope All 50 categories of cybersecurity incidents Personal data breaches only
Format CERT-In structured incident report As prescribed in Rules
Penalty for non-compliance IT Act provisions Up to ₹200 crore (DPB)
Confidentiality CERT-In treats reports as confidential DPB proceedings may be public
Unified breach response playbook: Design a unified breach response playbook. The CERT-In report (filed within 6 hours) should serve as the factual foundation for the DPDP Act notification to DPB and data principals. The CERT-In report is technical/operational; the DPDP notification to data principals must be in plain language explaining the breach, what data was affected, and what remedial steps the Data Fiduciary is taking.
