01

Puttaswamy v Union of India — The Root

2017 Supreme Court 9-judge bench ruling that changed everything

On 24 August 2017, a 9-judge constitutional bench of the Supreme Court of India delivered a unanimous ruling in Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India — the most significant privacy judgment in Indian legal history. The bench held that the right to privacy is an intrinsic part of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 of the Constitution, and also derives from Articles 14 and 19.

The ruling reversed decades of earlier jurisprudence (including M.P. Sharma v. Satish Chandra 1954 and Kharak Singh v. State of U.P. 1963) that had treated privacy as a creature of statute rather than a fundamental constitutional right.

Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017) — Core Test

"The right to privacy is an intrinsic part of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21..."

The bench laid down a three-part proportionality test for state interference with privacy: (1) Legality — the interference must have a legal basis; (2) Legitimate Aim — it must serve a valid state interest; (3) Proportionality — it must not exceed what is necessary.

Why this matters for the DPDP Act: The Puttaswamy ruling created the constitutional imperative for data protection legislation. It also established the proportionality standard — any law restricting data privacy (including the DPDP Act's own Section 17 exemptions) must satisfy this test. Civil society has argued that Section 17's broad government exemptions may not pass the Puttaswamy proportionality test.
02

Legislative Journey — 2018 to 2023

Six years from the first committee report to Royal Assent

2018 — Srikrishna Committee
First Blueprint — Data Fiduciaries and Data Principals
Justice B.N. Srikrishna chaired a committee that produced India's first formal data protection framework proposal. Key introductions: the terms 'Data Fiduciary' (entity controlling processing) and 'Data Principal' (individual), a consent-first model, data localisation requirements, and a standalone Data Protection Authority (DPA).
2019 — Personal Data Protection Bill introduced
Tabled in Lok Sabha; referred to JPC
The government introduced the PDP Bill 2019 in Parliament. Significant deviations from the Srikrishna draft included broader government exemptions and amendments to the RTI Act. The Bill was referred to a Joint Parliamentary Committee (JPC) for scrutiny.
2021 — JPC Report: 81 Amendments
Joint Parliamentary Committee recommendations
After nearly two years, the JPC submitted its report recommending 81 amendments to the PDP Bill. The report expanded the scope to cover non-personal data, recommended stronger localisation norms, and generated significant debate on the Article 12 state exemption provisions.
August 2022 — PDP Bill Withdrawn
Complete withdrawal — fresh legislative approach
The government unexpectedly withdrew the 2019 PDP Bill entirely, citing the need for a 'comprehensive legal framework' covering the broader digital ecosystem. This reset the entire process but also created an opportunity for a cleaner, more focused statute.
2023 — DPDP Act enacted
Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 — Royal Assent on 11 August 2023
The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 received Presidential assent on 11 August 2023. The Act is significantly shorter and more principles-based than the PDP Bill 2019, with implementation detail left to rules. It was notified in the Official Gazette on 11 August 2023.

Key Design Choices — What the DPDP Act Chose to Do Differently

Deliberate departures from the PDP Bill 2019 and from GDPR

Choice 1
Consent-First, Not Legitimate Interest
Unlike GDPR's six lawful processing bases, the DPDP Act primarily relies on consent and a narrow set of 'legitimate uses'. There is no broad 'legitimate interests' ground — this was a deliberate choice to give individuals clearer control.
Choice 2
Fixed Penalties, Not % of Turnover
The Act caps penalties at ₹250 Crore maximum — a departure from GDPR's 4% of global turnover model. Critics argue this makes the Act less punitive for large multinationals; proponents argue it provides legal certainty.
Choice 3
No Cross-Border Transfer Restrictions (by default)
Unlike the 2019 Bill's data localisation requirements, the DPDP Act allows cross-border transfers to all countries except those specifically blacklisted by the government — a significantly more liberal approach.
Choice 4
Rules-Heavy Implementation
The Act deliberately delegates substantial detail to rules notified by the Central Government — making the Act shorter and more flexible but also concentrating significant power in the executive to shape compliance requirements.
