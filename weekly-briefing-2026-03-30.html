DPDP Act & Rules — Implementation Watch
Supreme Court February – March 2026 · Ongoing
SC Constitutional Challenge to DPDP Act Proceeds Before Larger Bench — Stay Declined, RTI Amendment at Centre of Dispute

A bench of Chief Justice Surya Kant and Justices Joymalya Bagchi and Vipul M. Pancholi issued notice to the Centre in February 2026 on a batch of writ petitions challenging the constitutional validity of several provisions of the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 and the DPDP Rules 2025. The matter was referred to a larger bench with hearings continuing in March 2026.

The petitions, filed by Internet Freedom Foundation and allied civil society organisations, challenge Section 44(3) of the DPDP Act which amends Section 8(1)(j) of the RTI Act, effectively narrowing the Right to Information in respect of personal data. Further challenges target Sections 17(1)(c), 33(1), and 36 read with Rule 23, alleging unconstitutional concentration of surveillance powers in the Central Government and insufficient institutional independence of the Data Protection Board.

Critically, the Supreme Court has declined to grant an interim stay on the DPDP framework. Phase 1 — constituting the Data Protection Board — remains fully operational as of November 13, 2025.

Strategic Implication for Organisations

Businesses must not interpret ongoing litigation as reason to pause DPDP compliance preparation. Phase 2 (consent manager framework) activates November 13, 2026 — only 230 days away. Any constitutional reading-down of Section 17 government exemptions would, counterintuitively, increase obligations on private Data Fiduciaries. Legal teams must simultaneously build compliance architecture and track the larger bench proceedings.

Source: Internet Freedom Foundation · India TV News
DPDP Phase 2 Countdown Active · 230 Days to Phase 2
DPDP Enforcement Roadmap: Penalty Regime Up to ₹250 Crore — Data Protection Board Adjudicating from November 2025

With the Data Protection Board of India constituted and operational since November 13, 2025, India has entered the live enforcement era of data protection law. The DPBI can investigate complaints, issue compliance directions, and levy penalties up to ₹250 crore per contravention. Key penalty triggers include: failure to implement security safeguards (₹250 Cr), processing without valid consent (₹200 Cr), failure to notify data breaches (₹200 Cr), and inadequate protection of children’s data (₹200 Cr).

The Board operates as a “digital office” with virtual hearing capabilities, maintaining a public registry of enforcement actions. Penalties are not automatic — the Board evaluates nature, gravity, duration, data type, repetition, and mitigation effectiveness before levying fines. Full substantive compliance applies from May 13, 2027.

Board-Level Action Required Now

CISOs and General Counsel must treat the current period as the final window to build compliant infrastructure before penalties apply. Priority: (1) appoint a Data Protection Officer or equivalent, (2) implement breach notification workflows meeting the 72-hour standard, (3) complete a data inventory audit mapping personal data flows against DPDP categories, and (4) evaluate consent manager vendors ahead of the ₹2 crore net-worth registration threshold in Phase 2.

Court Rulings & Digital Rights
Supreme Court February 2026
Supreme Court Notice on DPDP Act Challenges Highlights Three Core Constitutional Fault-Lines

The notice issued by the Supreme Court bench to the Centre in February 2026 crystallises three fundamental constitutional objections to the DPDP architecture. First, the absence of a journalistic or public-interest exemption for processing personal data — a standard safeguard in GDPR and comparable frameworks — raises concerns under Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of expression). Second, the Board lacks structural independence comparable to constitutional authorities, raising Article 14 (equality before law) concerns about its capacity to adjudicate complaints against government Data Fiduciaries. Third, Section 36 read with Rule 23 empowers the government to seek any information from any Data Fiduciary, creating an unchecked executive access mechanism without judicial oversight.

Legal Significance & Watch Points

The larger bench proceedings will determine whether DPDP — India’s first comprehensive data protection statute — withstands constitutional scrutiny. A decision in favour of petitioners on Section 44(3) would re-open RTI disclosures of personal information currently being closed under the DPDP amendment, directly affecting PIOs, RTI applicants, and government departments. Advocates and compliance officers should file caveats in these proceedings where their clients’ interests are directly affected.

E-Governance, MeitY & UIDAI Updates
UIDAI / Aadhaar March 2026
UIDAI Completes Biometric Update for 1.2 Crore School Children; Launches Bug Bounty Programme & Mega MBU Drive 2.0 in J&K

UIDAI has accomplished a landmark milestone: completing mandatory biometric updates (MBU) for approximately 1.2 crore school children across 1 lakh schools within six months of launching the programme in October 2025. The drive, targeting children in the 7–15 age group, is being conducted free of cost and uses over 4,000 enrolment machines. UIDAI is in the process of expanding this fleet to accelerate coverage.

Separately, on March 12, 2026, UIDAI launched a Bug Bounty Programme to crowdsource security vulnerability discovery in its systems — a significant step toward proactive DPDP-aligned security posture for one of India’s most sensitive biometric databases. An Advanced Aadhaar Seva Kendra (ASK) was also inaugurated in Ranchi on March 11, 2026.

From March 23, 2026, UIDAI launched Mega MBU Drive 2.0 across Jammu & Kashmir, targeting 2,382 schools and over 2 lakh students. Additionally, from March 1, 2026, new rules allow Aadhaar address updates via the “Head of Family” (HOF) facial recognition mechanism through mobile — reducing physical centre dependency.

DPDP & UIDAI Intersection

The UIDAI Bug Bounty Programme is particularly significant given the DPDP Act’s categorisation of biometric data as a sensitive sub-category requiring heightened protections. Under the DPDP framework, UIDAI as a government Data Fiduciary has obligations around security safeguards — the Bug Bounty Programme aligns with Section 8(5) of the Act which mandates reasonable security safeguards. IT companies and security researchers should actively participate to both contribute to national security and establish credentials in the emerging Indian DPDP compliance ecosystem.

Source: India TV News · Kashmir Reader · UIDAI Press Releases
MeitY Initiative February – March 2026
MeitY Constitutes DigiLocker Harmonisation Committee; Blockchain India Challenge 2026 Underway

MeitY has constituted a high-level committee to harmonise the regulatory framework for DigiLocker, with a mandate encompassing a legislative review of the Digital Locker Rules, the roadmap for Entity Locker expansion, and the commercialisation strategy for ecosystem growth. This signals India’s intent to establish DigiLocker as a foundational digital document layer across public and private services — directly relevant to DPDP Act obligations around document-linked personal data.

The Blockchain India Challenge 2026, launched February 23 by MeitY Secretary S. Krishnan, is progressing with startups developing permissioned blockchain solutions for land records, Public Distribution System, healthcare, and document verification — all high-value government data repositories with significant personal data holdings. Evaluation criteria will reward DPDP-readiness.

Procurement & Policy Opportunity

Government technology vendors and legal tech companies should note that blockchain deployments handling personal data in public services create novel consent management challenges under the DPDP Rules — immutability of blockchain records conflicts with the right to erasure under Section 12 of the DPDP Act. MeitY’s Blockchain Challenge 2026 solutions should include a dedicated privacy-by-design architecture addressing this tension for regulatory review by the DigiLocker Harmonisation Committee.

Financial Regulation
RBI Digital Payments & Fraud Framework
RBI Regulation March 2026 · Effective April 1 & July 1, 2026
RBI Mandates 2FA for All Digital Payments from April 1; Proposes Fraud Compensation Framework for UPI & Mobile Banking from July 2026

The Reserve Bank of India has issued draft Third Amendment Directions, 2026 under its Responsible Business Conduct framework, introducing two landmark measures. First, mandatory two-factor authentication (2FA) for every digital payment — encompassing UPI, internet banking, mobile banking, debit/credit cards, and ATM withdrawals — takes effect from April 1, 2026.

Second, a novel Digital Fraud Compensation Framework takes effect from July 1, 2026. Under the framework, customers suffering genuine fraudulent transaction losses of up to ₹50,000 may claim compensation of 85% of net loss or ₹25,000 (whichever is lower) — once per lifetime — provided the incident is reported to the bank and the cyber crime portal/helpline (1930) within five days. The framework does not apply to cases of negligence or sharing of OTPs or PINs. Public feedback is invited until April 6, 2026.

Compliance Action Required — Banks & Fintechs

Banks, payment aggregators, and wallet operators must urgently audit their 2FA implementation across all channels for April 1 compliance. The fraud compensation framework creates direct financial liability for institutions and will drive improvements in fraud detection, customer notification, and complaint management systems. Under DPDP Rules, financial fraud incidents involving personal data exposure must additionally be reported to the DPBI within 72 hours — creating a dual reporting obligation alongside the 5-day RBI window. Legal teams should reconcile these conflicting timelines now.

Domestic Cyber Threats
India Cybercrime & CERT-In
Threat Intelligence Seqrite / Cyble Report · 2026
India Ranked No. 1 National Cyber Risk by WEF 2026; Ransomware Up 31%, APT36 & SideCopy Target Defence Networks

According to the World Economic Forum Global Risk Report 2026, cybersecurity has emerged as India’s number one national risk, surpassing economic downturns, climate disasters, and armed conflict. India is the second most targeted country in the world for cyberattacks, accounting for approximately 3% of global ransomware victims.

The Seqrite India Cyber Threat Report 2026 documents: (1) a 31% surge in ransomware attacks above the prior nine-month average as of January 2026, with healthcare leading at 27 incidents/month, followed by government (11) and manufacturing (10); (2) a documented coordinated hybrid warfare campaign by APT36 and SideCopy threat actors targeting Indian defence and government networks; and (3) investment and digital payment scams accounting for over 75% of total financial cybercrime losses. Data breaches now cost India an average of $2.18 million per incident — a 28% increase over three years.

DPDP & CERT-In Compliance Link

The APT36/SideCopy campaign targeting defence networks raises the urgency of India’s Space Cybersecurity Guidelines 2026 and CERT-In’s April 2022 mandatory reporting directions (6-hour breach notification). With healthcare as the top ransomware target, DPDP Act’s heightened protections for health data and the imminent penalty regime create a compounded risk profile for hospital chains, health-tech platforms, and insurance companies. CISOs in these sectors should escalate to Board level immediately.

International Cybersecurity Incidents — India Relevance
Global Breach March 2026
Stryker Medical Technology Hit by Iran-Aligned Hacktivists; 274 Ransomware Victims Globally in March 2026 Alone

Medical technology major Stryker Corporation disclosed a cyberattack attributed to an Iran-aligned hacktivist group in March 2026, underlining the acute vulnerability of medical device manufacturers and healthcare supply chains to state-aligned threat actors. The attack follows a pattern of healthcare-sector targeting that has surged globally in 2026.

The broader March 2026 ransomware landscape is severe: 274 victims globally this month as of late March, with the most active groups being Genesis (8 new attacks), Qilin (7), and Play News (6). The United States remains the primary geography, but Professional Services and Healthcare sectors experience the highest impact globally. Pro-Ukrainian group Bearlyfy has executed 70+ attacks against Russian companies using custom GenieLocker ransomware since January 2025, illustrating active geopolitical use of ransomware as hybrid warfare.

India Medical & BPO Sector Alert

India’s large medical device sector, hospital chains, and health-tech companies operating globally must take note: the Stryker incident model — state-aligned hacktivist attack on healthcare infrastructure — applies directly to Indian companies with cross-border operations. Under the DPDP Act, health data carries the highest protection obligations. Any ransomware breach involving patient health records would trigger both CERT-In 6-hour reporting and DPBI breach notification obligations, simultaneously. The first test case in this space will set the precedent for penalty quantum calibration under the DPDP framework.

Source: BlackFog Ransomware Report 2026 · PKWARE 2026 Breaches · ACI Learning Breach Tracker
Geopolitical Cyber January – March 2026
Geopolitical Hacktivist Campaigns Intensify Globally — India’s Hybrid Warfare Cyber Exposure Grows

The documented use of custom ransomware (GenieLocker by Bearlyfy against Russian targets) and state-aligned hacktivist campaigns (Iran-aligned groups against Stryker) signal an accelerating convergence of cyber and geopolitical conflict in 2026. India, simultaneously targeted by APT36/SideCopy from Pakistan-aligned actors and facing growing Chinese cyber activity, sits at the intersection of multiple active threat actor ecosystems.

January 2026 saw 50+ companies breached in a single coordinated campaign, with threat actors described as “just logging in” through compromised credentials — highlighting the continued dominance of identity-based attacks over zero-day exploits as the primary initial access vector.

Policy Implications for India

The pattern of geopolitically motivated cyberattacks targeting critical sectors reinforces the case for India to urgently accelerate its National Cybersecurity Policy update (2020 policy now six years old), formalise the Critical Information Infrastructure (CII) designation regime under Section 70 of the IT Act, and align CERT-In mandatory reporting timelines with the DPDP Board’s breach notification framework. The Ministry of Home Affairs and MeitY should establish a joint incident coordination cell drawing on the learnings from 2026 global incidents.
