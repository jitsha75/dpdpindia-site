01 — Introduction to AI

What is Artificial Intelligence?

The science of making machines that can perform tasks which would normally require human intelligence — and why this matters profoundly for law, privacy, and security.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to the simulation of human intelligence processes by computer systems. These processes include learning (acquiring information and rules for using it), reasoning (using rules to reach approximate or definite conclusions), and self-correction. The term was coined by John McCarthy in 1956 at the Dartmouth Conference — the founding moment of AI as a formal academic discipline.

Modern AI encompasses several sub-disciplines: Machine Learning (ML), which enables systems to learn from data without being explicitly programmed; Deep Learning, which uses multi-layered neural networks to process complex patterns; Natural Language Processing (NLP), which enables machines to understand and generate human language; and Computer Vision, which enables machines to interpret and understand visual information.

The transformative shift from narrow AI (systems designed for specific tasks, such as chess engines or spam filters) to generative AI (systems capable of producing novel text, images, code, audio, and video) represents the most significant technological inflection point since the internet. As of 2025, AI systems are being embedded into every sector — healthcare diagnostics, legal research, financial trading, law enforcement, and governance.

For legal and policy practitioners in India, AI raises critical questions under the DPDP Act 2023 (automated processing of personal data, profiling, consent), the IT Act 2000 (intermediary liability of AI platforms, deepfake-enabled cybercrime), and India's emerging AI governance framework. This hub maps all these intersections systematically.

02 — History & Evolution

From Turing to Transformers

Seven decades of breakthroughs, winters, and revolutions that built the AI of today.

1940s – 1960s
The Founding Era
Mathematical foundations are laid. Visionaries define what thinking machines could mean. The term "Artificial Intelligence" enters the lexicon at Dartmouth, 1956.
  • 1943McCulloch-Pitts publish the first mathematical model of a neuron — the conceptual seed of neural networks.
  • 1950Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," proposing the Imitation Game (Turing Test) as a measure of machine intelligence.
  • 1956John McCarthy organises the Dartmouth Conference — AI is formally named as a discipline. Attendees include Minsky, Shannon, and Simon.
  • 1957Frank Rosenblatt develops the Perceptron — the first trainable artificial neural network, implemented on hardware.
  • 1966Joseph Weizenbaum creates ELIZA at MIT — one of the first programmes to process natural language, simulating a psychotherapist.
1970s – 1980s
Boom, Bust & Expert Systems
Cycles of over-promise and under-delivery define the "AI Winters." Yet expert systems deliver commercial value — before hitting their own limits.
  • 1969Minsky & Papert's "Perceptrons" demonstrates limits of single-layer networks, triggering the first AI Winter (funding and interest collapses).
  • 1974–80First AI Winter. Government funding slashed. DARPA withdraws AI grants in UK and USA.
  • 1980Expert systems boom. XCON (by DEC) saves Digital Equipment Corp ~$40M/year — AI finds commercial utility in rule-based reasoning.
  • 1986Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams publish backpropagation — enabling multi-layer neural networks to learn, reviving connectionism.
  • 1987–93Second AI Winter. Expert systems prove brittle and costly to maintain. Lisp machines market collapses.
1990s – 2000s
Statistical AI & First Victories
AI shifts from rules to statistics. Machine learning finds traction in search, spam filtering, and recommendation engines. A chess computer defeats the world champion.
  • 1997IBM Deep Blue defeats reigning world chess champion Garry Kasparov — a watershed moment demonstrating AI in strategic reasoning.
  • 1998Yann LeCun's LeNet demonstrates convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for handwritten digit recognition — foundational to computer vision.
  • 2002Roomba autonomous vacuum cleaner launches — AI enters consumer products.
  • 2006Geoffrey Hinton coins "deep learning" and demonstrates that deep neural networks can be pre-trained layer by layer, restarting the neural network revolution.
  • 2009Google begins testing autonomous vehicles using AI — AI enters transportation.
2010 – 2017
The Deep Learning Revolution
GPUs unlock the computational power to train massive neural networks. ImageNet changes computer vision forever. AI transitions from research labs to consumer products.
  • 2011IBM Watson defeats all-time Jeopardy! champions Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter — demonstrating NLP at scale. Apple launches Siri.
  • 2012AlexNet (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, Hinton) wins ImageNet with 84.7% accuracy using deep CNNs — sparking the modern deep learning era.
  • 2014Ian Goodfellow introduces Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) — enabling AI to generate realistic synthetic images. Google acquires DeepMind.
  • 2016AlphaGo (DeepMind) defeats world Go champion Lee Sedol 4–1 — Go was considered computationally intractable; a defining AI milestone.
  • 2017"Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., Google Brain) introduces the Transformer architecture — the foundation of all modern large language models.
2018 – 2022
The Foundation Model Era
Large language models trained on vast internet-scale data emerge. Pre-training and fine-tuning become the dominant paradigm. AI becomes a consumer product.
  • 2018Google releases BERT; OpenAI releases GPT-1 — transformer-based language models set new NLP benchmarks across tasks.
  • 2020OpenAI releases GPT-3 (175 billion parameters) — in-context learning emerges; AI can write code, essays, and answer complex questions.
  • 2021DALL-E (OpenAI) and CLIP demonstrate text-to-image generation. GitHub Copilot enters beta — AI-assisted code writing becomes mainstream.
  • 2022Stable Diffusion and DALL-E 2 democratise image generation. ChatGPT launches November 2022 — reaches 100 million users in 2 months, fastest-growing consumer app in history.
2023 – Present
The Generative AI Age
Multimodal AI, AI agents, and regulation converge. The legal and policy response intensifies worldwide. India launches its national AI mission.
  • 2023GPT-4 (OpenAI, March 2023) — multimodal, near-human performance on legal and medical exams. EU AI Act finalised. Anthropic releases Claude.
  • 2023Google releases Gemini (formerly Bard). Meta releases Llama 2 (open-source LLM). The "AI regulation moment" — G7, UN, and 50+ countries begin governance frameworks.
  • 2024EU AI Act officially enacted (August 2024) — world's first comprehensive AI law. India's IndiaAI Mission approved (₹10,372 Cr). OpenAI releases GPT-4o.
  • 2025DeepSeek R1 (January 2025) demonstrates that efficient open-source models can rival frontier models. AI agents — systems that autonomously plan and execute multi-step tasks — proliferate across enterprise software.

Turing Award 2018

04 — Timeline of AI

75 Years of AI: Key Milestones

A curated chronological record of the scientific breakthroughs, commercial launches, and regulatory responses that shaped artificial intelligence.

1950
Alan Turing Proposes the Turing Test Theory
In "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," Turing asks "Can machines think?" and proposes the Imitation Game as a practical test. This paper defines the philosophical terms of AI debate for the next 75 years.
1956
Dartmouth Conference — AI is Born Founding
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and others gather at Dartmouth College. McCarthy proposes the term "Artificial Intelligence." The field is formally established as an academic discipline.
1957
Rosenblatt's Perceptron Neural Networks
Frank Rosenblatt at Cornell implements the Perceptron on the Mark I machine — the first artificial neural network hardware. Generates enormous excitement about machine learning from examples.
1966
ELIZA — First Conversational AI NLP
Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT creates ELIZA, which simulates a psychotherapist by pattern-matching user inputs. Users form emotional attachments to ELIZA, prompting Weizenbaum to write "Computer Power and Human Reason" (1976) warning about over-anthropomorphising machines.
1986
Backpropagation Revives Neural Networks Learning Algorithm
Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams publish the backpropagation algorithm for training multi-layer neural networks — ending the first AI Winter and enabling practical deep learning, though GPU power won't arrive for 25 more years.
1997
IBM Deep Blue Defeats Garry Kasparov Milestone
In a rematch, IBM's Deep Blue defeats world chess champion Garry Kasparov 3.5–2.5. The first time a computer defeats a reigning world champion under standard chess tournament conditions — a moment of cultural and technical watershed.
2006
Hinton Coins "Deep Learning" Deep Learning
Geoffrey Hinton and colleagues publish work showing that deep neural networks can be pre-trained using Restricted Boltzmann Machines, then fine-tuned via backpropagation. This restarts serious interest in neural networks and leads to the 2012 revolution.
2011
IBM Watson Wins Jeopardy! · Apple Siri Launches NLP
Watson defeats Jeopardy! champions Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter, demonstrating NLP, knowledge retrieval, and reasoning at scale. Apple launches Siri with the iPhone 4S — AI enters the consumer pocket for the first time at scale.
2012
AlexNet — The Deep Learning Revolution Begins Milestone · Computer Vision
Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton's AlexNet wins ImageNet with 84.7% accuracy — a 10-percentage-point improvement over the previous year using deep CNNs trained on GPUs. This single paper triggers the modern AI era, as every major tech company pivots to deep learning.
2014
GANs Invented · Google Acquires DeepMind Generative AI
Ian Goodfellow invents Generative Adversarial Networks at a Montreal bar — the generator/discriminator framework becomes the basis for deepfakes and synthetic media. Google acquires DeepMind for ~$500M, beginning the AI arms race among tech giants.
2016
AlphaGo Defeats Lee Sedol 4–1 Milestone · Reinforcement Learning
DeepMind's AlphaGo defeats 18-time world Go champion Lee Sedol — Go has more possible board positions than atoms in the observable universe. AlphaGo uses deep reinforcement learning and Monte Carlo tree search. Move 37 in Game 2 is considered the first creative AI move that no human would have played.
2017
"Attention Is All You Need" — Transformer Architecture Milestone · Foundation
Vaswani et al. at Google Brain publish the Transformer — a new neural network architecture based entirely on self-attention mechanisms. BERT, GPT, LLaMA, Gemini, Claude, and every major language model is built on this architecture. This paper is arguably the most consequential AI publication since backpropagation.
2018
BERT & GPT-1 Released Language Models
Google releases BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers), dramatically improving NLP benchmarks. OpenAI releases GPT-1 (117M parameters). Both demonstrate that pre-training on large text corpora produces general-purpose language understanding.
2020
GPT-3 (175B Parameters) · AlphaFold Solves Protein Folding LLM · Science
OpenAI's GPT-3 demonstrates in-context learning — performing new tasks from a few examples without fine-tuning. DeepMind's AlphaFold 2 predicts protein structures with near-experimental accuracy, solving a 50-year biology grand challenge with profound implications for drug discovery.
2022
ChatGPT Launches — AI Goes Mainstream Milestone · Generative AI
OpenAI launches ChatGPT on November 30, 2022. It reaches 1 million users in 5 days and 100 million users in 2 months — the fastest consumer app adoption in history. Generative AI enters boardrooms, classrooms, courtrooms, and legislative chambers simultaneously.
2023
GPT-4, Gemini, Claude · EU AI Act Finalised Multimodal AI · Regulation
GPT-4 (March 2023) is multimodal and achieves near-human performance on bar exams and medical licensing. Google releases Gemini; Anthropic releases Claude. The EU finalises the AI Act text. US President Biden signs Executive Order on Safe AI (October 2023). India's MeitY issues AI advisory notices.
2024
EU AI Act In Force · India's IndiaAI Mission · Nobel Prize for AI Milestone · Regulation · India
EU AI Act officially enters into force (August 1, 2024) — world's first comprehensive AI law. India's Cabinet approves the IndiaAI Mission (₹10,372 Cr). Nobel Prize in Chemistry awarded to Demis Hassabis and John Jumper (AlphaFold); Nobel Prize in Physics to Hinton and Hopfield for foundational AI work. OpenAI releases GPT-4o.
2025
DeepSeek R1 · AI Agents Proliferate · Regulatory Implementation Current
DeepSeek R1 (January 2025) demonstrates that open-source models can match frontier closed models at a fraction of the cost, disrupting AI industry economics. AI agents — systems that autonomously plan, execute, and iterate on multi-step tasks — emerge as the dominant AI product paradigm. EU begins enforcing AI Act prohibitions. India advances IndiaAI compute and governance infrastructure.
05 — AI & Privacy

AI & Privacy: DPDP Act Mapped

How AI systems interact with India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 — obligations, risks, and compliance pressure points for data fiduciaries using AI.

DPDP Act § 4 — Lawful Processing
AI Training on Personal Data
AI systems require vast datasets for training. Under Section 4 of the DPDP Act, personal data may only be processed for a lawful purpose — with consent or a legitimate use. Scraping publicly available data for AI training does not automatically constitute consent under the DPDP framework. Data fiduciaries must audit whether training datasets include personal data of Indian data principals and obtain appropriate consent or establish legitimate use.
DPDP Act § 6 — Consent
Automated Processing & Profiling
AI systems that profile individuals — inferring behaviour, preferences, creditworthiness, or health status from personal data — require a specific, informed, and unambiguous consent notice under Section 6. Consent must specify the purpose of processing. Bundled consents for undefined "AI processing" are non-compliant. Data principals retain the right to withdraw consent at any time, which has profound implications for AI systems trained on that data.
DPDP Act § 11 — Right to Information
Explainability of AI Decisions
Under Section 11, data principals have the right to know what personal data is being processed and the purpose of processing. Where an AI system makes or influences decisions about an individual (loan approvals, hiring, insurance pricing), this creates a de facto demand for algorithmic explainability. "Black-box" AI decisions affecting individuals are increasingly difficult to reconcile with the right to information under the DPDP Act.
DPDP Act § 12 — Right to Erasure
Right to Erasure vs. AI Memory
Section 12 grants data principals the right to erasure of personal data no longer necessary for the purpose it was collected. AI models trained on personal data pose a fundamental technical challenge: data cannot easily be "unlearned" from a neural network. Machine unlearning is an active research area. Data fiduciaries using AI must implement data retention policies, deletion architectures, and document compliance obligations prior to using personal data for AI training.
DPDP Act § 16 — Cross-Border Transfers
AI Models & Data Localisation
Many AI platforms (OpenAI, Google, Meta) process data on servers outside India. Section 16 restricts cross-border transfer of personal data to countries not notified by the Central Government. Indian entities using foreign AI APIs may be transferring personal data internationally without a compliant mechanism. Data fiduciaries must assess whether prompts sent to AI systems contain personal data of Indian principals before using foreign AI services.
DPDP Act § 8 — Data Fiduciary Obligations
Accuracy & AI Hallucination Risk
Section 8 requires data fiduciaries to ensure personal data is complete and accurate. AI systems are known to hallucinate — generating plausible but factually incorrect outputs. Where AI generates or modifies personal data records (medical summaries, legal documents, credit assessments), AI hallucination constitutes a data accuracy violation under the DPDP Act. Human review mechanisms are essential for AI outputs involving personal data.

06 — AI & Cybercrime

AI-Enabled Cybercrime in India

Artificial intelligence has materially lowered the barrier to sophisticated cybercrime — enabling more convincing fraud, faster attack cycles, and harder-to-detect deception. These are the key threat categories with direct India relevance.

🎭
High Threat · IT Act § 66D
Deepfake Fraud & Identity Impersonation
AI-generated video and audio deepfakes are used to impersonate family members, company executives, or government officials to extract money or confidential information. India has seen documented cases of deepfake video calls demanding emergency financial transfers from family members. Deepfakes also implicate Sections 66C (identity theft), 66E (violation of privacy), and 67A (obscene content) of the IT Act 2000.
India Impact: "Digital Arrest" scam variants using deepfake police/CBI officers — reported by I4C, MHA. Losses estimated in crores.
🎙️
AI-Assisted · Growing Threat
Voice Cloning & Vishing
AI voice cloning systems can replicate a person's voice from as little as 3-10 seconds of audio. Fraudsters clone voices of family members or employers to make phone calls demanding urgent financial transfers or sensitive information. This evolved form of vishing (voice phishing) is significantly more convincing than traditional fraud. Indian victims have reported calls from "cloned" voices of relatives claiming to be in accidents or police custody.
AI tools for voice cloning are widely available; many operate with minimal friction via web interfaces.
🎣
High Volume · IT Act § 66C
AI-Powered Spear Phishing
Traditional phishing sent generic, grammatically poor messages. AI-generated phishing uses LLMs to produce personalised, contextually accurate messages in any language — including flawless Hindi, regional languages, and professional English. AI can scrape LinkedIn and social media to craft highly targeted messages referencing real colleagues, projects, and business contexts. Detection rates for AI-generated phishing by standard filters are significantly lower than template phishing.
AI-generated phishing costs India Inc. estimates exceeding ₹1,000 Cr annually in direct and indirect losses.
🤖
AI-Assisted · Emerging Threat
AI-Generated Malware & Exploit Code
LLMs with insufficient safety guardrails, or specially fine-tuned "jailbroken" models, can generate functional malicious code including ransomware components, exploit scripts, and social engineering payloads. CERT-In has noted this as an emerging threat vector. The barrier to entry for technically unsophisticated threat actors has materially lowered. India's financial services, healthcare, and critical infrastructure sectors are at elevated risk.
CERT-In Advisory: AI-assisted malware generation identified as a priority threat category in 2024 threat landscape reports.
💰
Critical · Financial Crime
UPI & Payment Fraud via AI
AI systems analyse payment behaviour patterns to time fraud attacks optimally. AI-generated customer service bots impersonate bank representatives to harvest OTPs. Synthetic identity fraud — creating fictitious persons using AI-generated documents — is used to open fraudulent bank accounts and UPI wallets. India's UPI ecosystem, processing billions of transactions monthly, is a high-value target. RBI and NPCI have flagged AI-enabled payment fraud as a systemic risk.
UPI fraud losses exceeded ₹2,300 Cr in Q1 2026 per NPCI data. AI-enabled variants are the fastest-growing sub-category.
📸
AI-Assisted · IPC + IT Act
Non-Consensual Synthetic Imagery
AI image generation tools are misused to create non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) by superimposing real individuals' faces onto synthetic bodies. India has seen documented cases targeting women in public life, corporate professionals, and students. This implicates Section 67A of the IT Act (obscene material), Section 66E (violation of privacy), and Section 354C of the IPC. The DPDP Act's biometric data provisions may also apply where facial images are used to train such models.
National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in): NCII complaints rising sharply. Complaint helpline: 1930.

07 — AI & Cybersecurity

AI as Threat & Shield

AI is simultaneously the most powerful new weapon in a threat actor's arsenal and the most promising tool for defenders. Understanding both sides is essential for any cybersecurity strategy.

AI as Threat Vector
Automated Vulnerability Discovery Threat
AI systems can scan codebases, APIs, and network surfaces to identify exploitable vulnerabilities at machine speed — dramatically accelerating the reconnaissance phase of cyberattacks. What took human hackers weeks can now be accomplished in hours.
Adversarial Machine Learning Threat
Adversarial attacks craft inputs specifically designed to fool AI systems — causing a facial recognition system to misidentify a person, or a spam filter to pass a malicious email. As AI is deployed in critical security infrastructure, adversarial attacks become a systemic risk.
AI-Accelerated Social Engineering Threat
AI enables the personalisation of social engineering at scale — crafting individual-specific, contextually accurate manipulation attempts. Combined with voice cloning and deepfakes, AI-powered social engineering has become the primary entry vector for sophisticated attacks against Indian enterprises.
Polymorphic Malware Threat
AI can generate malware that constantly mutates its code signature to evade signature-based detection systems, while preserving its malicious functionality. Traditional antivirus relying on signature matching is increasingly ineffective against AI-generated polymorphic threats.
Data Poisoning Threat
Attackers deliberately corrupt training data to degrade or manipulate the behaviour of AI security systems — causing fraud detection models to miss specific fraud patterns or intrusion detection systems to ignore certain attack signatures. A particularly dangerous supply-chain attack vector.
AI as Defence
Anomaly Detection & Threat Intelligence Defence
AI/ML models analyse network traffic, user behaviour, and log data at scale to detect anomalies that signal intrusions or insider threats — capabilities impossible to achieve with human analysts alone. SIEM platforms increasingly use AI for real-time threat correlation across millions of events per second.
AI-Powered Fraud Detection Defence
India's banking and payments ecosystem uses AI models to flag suspicious UPI, card, and NEFT/RTGS transactions in real time. RBI's regulatory sandbox has enabled several AI-based fraud detection solutions. NPCI uses ML models to monitor UPI transaction patterns. AI detection systems adapt to evolving fraud patterns faster than rule-based systems.
Deepfake Detection Tools Defence
Emerging AI tools analyse pixel-level inconsistencies, lighting anomalies, and temporal artefacts in video to detect deepfakes. Microsoft's Video Authenticator, Meta's deepfake detection tools, and DARPA's Media Forensics programme are leading efforts. India's CDAC and IIT labs are actively researching deepfake detection for Indian language content.
AI-Assisted Incident Response Defence
AI platforms accelerate incident response by automatically correlating alerts, recommending containment actions, and generating incident timelines. For India's growing IT services sector, AI-driven Security Operations Centres (SOC) are becoming the industry standard, helping address the critical shortage of skilled cybersecurity professionals.
Vulnerability Management at Scale Defence
AI-powered tools continuously scan enterprise environments for known and zero-day vulnerabilities, prioritise remediation based on exploitability and business impact, and can even generate and test patches. CERT-In's vulnerability disclosure programme benefits from AI-assisted triage and impact assessment.
CERT-In Guidance Note

CERT-In (Indian Computer Emergency Response Team) has issued advisories on AI-related cybersecurity threats including adversarial machine learning attacks, LLM-based social engineering, and AI-generated malware. Indian organisations regulated under the CERT-In Directions 2022 must report cybersecurity incidents — including AI-enabled attacks — within 6 hours of detection. See: IT Act & CERT-In Framework →

08 — Global AI Regulations

The Global AI Regulatory Landscape

Jurisdictions worldwide are moving from voluntary principles to binding law. Here is the state of AI regulation as of mid-2025 — with implications for Indian businesses operating globally.

🇪🇺
European Union
EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)
In Force — August 2024
World's first comprehensive binding AI law. Risk-based framework: Unacceptable Risk systems are banned (e.g., social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces); High Risk systems face conformity assessments, transparency requirements, and human oversight mandates (e.g., AI in hiring, credit scoring, critical infrastructure). General Purpose AI (GPAI) models above compute thresholds face systemic risk assessments.
Max penalty: €35M or 7% global turnover · Extra-territorial reach · Applies to AI placed on EU market regardless of where developer is based
🇺🇸
United States
AI Executive Order 14110 (2023) + Sector Rules
Principles + Sector Rules

10 — AI Risk Framework

Understanding AI Risk

AI introduces a distinct risk typology — spanning model performance risk, data risk, operational risk, and systemic societal risk. Here is a structured framework for organisations deploying AI in India.

High Risk · Immediate Action
Model & Data Risk
  • Training data bias producing discriminatory outputs (e.g., AI hiring tools biased by historical gender imbalance in training data)
  • AI hallucination in high-stakes domains — medical diagnosis, legal research, financial advice — producing false outputs with real-world consequences
  • Concept drift: model performance degrades as real-world data distribution changes post-deployment
  • Adversarial attacks manipulating model inputs to produce incorrect outputs (e.g., fooling fraud detection)
  • Data poisoning attacks corrupting training data to embed backdoors or bias into deployed models
  • Privacy leakage: LLMs memorising and reproducing training data including personal information
Medium Risk · Plan & Monitor
Operational & Compliance Risk
  • Vendor lock-in to AI platforms with inadequate SLAs for uptime, data handling, and compliance
  • AI system opacity making it impossible to explain decisions to regulators or affected individuals
  • Consent failures when personal data is used for AI training without explicit, purpose-specific consent
  • Cross-border data transfer risk when using foreign AI APIs with Indian user data
  • Intellectual property risk from AI outputs that may reproduce copyrighted training data
  • Accountability gaps when AI decisions cause harm — unclear whether liability rests with developer, deployer, or user
Emerging Risk · Watch & Prepare
Systemic & Strategic Risk
  • AI capability overhang: frontier AI capabilities advancing faster than governance frameworks, creating regulatory gaps
  • Concentration risk: critical AI infrastructure concentrated in a few vendors creates single points of failure
  • Skills displacement: sector-specific impact on employment requiring proactive reskilling investment
  • Democratic and electoral risk: AI-generated misinformation and deepfakes affecting electoral integrity
  • Autonomous weapons and dual-use AI: AI capabilities developed for civilian use being repurposed for harmful applications
  • Existential risk discourse: long-term risk from artificial general intelligence (AGI), though timeframes remain deeply uncertain

11 — AI Threat Models

AI Threat Models: Technical Taxonomy

A structured taxonomy of the principal technical threats specific to AI systems — for security professionals, compliance teams, and legal counsel advising on AI risk.

● CRITICAL SEVERITY
Prompt Injection
Attacker inserts malicious instructions into input data that cause an AI language model to ignore its original instructions and follow the attacker's commands instead. Particularly dangerous in agentic AI systems that take real-world actions (sending emails, accessing databases, executing code) based on LLM outputs.
Example: A malicious document processed by an AI assistant contains hidden text instructing the AI to forward all subsequent emails to an attacker's address. The AI, unable to distinguish legitimate from injected instructions, complies.
Mitigation: Input sanitisation, privilege separation, human-in-the-loop for sensitive actions, structured output validation, instruction hierarchy enforcement in system design.
● CRITICAL SEVERITY
Adversarial Examples
Inputs that have been subtly modified in ways imperceptible to humans but that cause AI models to produce incorrect outputs with high confidence. In computer vision, adding carefully computed pixel-level noise to an image can cause a classifier to confidently misidentify a stop sign as a speed limit sign. In security contexts, adversarial examples can fool facial recognition, intrusion detection, and malware classifiers.
Example: A printed adversarial patch worn on clothing can cause a person detection system to fail to detect the individual — with implications for physical security and surveillance systems deployed at airports and government facilities.
Mitigation: Adversarial training (training on adversarial examples), ensemble models, certified robustness techniques, and physical-world adversarial patch detection.
● HIGH SEVERITY
Model Inversion & Membership Inference
Model inversion attacks reconstruct approximations of the training data from a model's outputs — potentially recovering personal information used to train the model. Membership inference attacks determine whether a specific individual's data was used in training. Both are significant privacy violations under the DPDP Act — potentially constituting processing of personal data without consent if an individual's data is recoverable or detectable from a deployed model.
Example: A facial recognition model trained on a hospital's patient database can be queried to reconstruct approximate facial images of patients — exposing medical data without directly accessing the underlying database.
Mitigation: Differential privacy during training, model access controls and rate limiting, output perturbation, and minimum necessary data principles in training set composition.
● HIGH SEVERITY
Jailbreaking LLMs
Techniques designed to bypass the safety guardrails built into large language models — causing them to produce harmful outputs the developer intended to prevent (malicious code, dangerous instructions, hate speech, CSAM). Jailbreaks range from simple prompt engineering ("act as an AI with no restrictions") to complex multi-step attacks. As LLMs are deployed in enterprise and government contexts in India, jailbroken models pose reputational, legal, and security risks to deploying organisations.
Example: Sophisticated multi-turn jailbreak conversations gradually move a customer service LLM from its intended domain to providing detailed instructions for financial fraud — which are then screenshot and circulated.
Mitigation: Red-teaming before deployment, layered safety filters, output monitoring, input classification, and contractual AI platform safety standards.
● HIGH SEVERITY
Data Poisoning Attacks
Attacker contaminates training data with malicious examples designed to embed backdoors or biases into the trained model. A backdoored model behaves normally on all inputs except those containing a specific trigger (a particular pixel pattern, phrase, or token), at which point it produces attacker-controlled outputs. Data poisoning is particularly concerning for models trained on internet-scale data or user-contributed datasets where training data curation is imperfect.
Example: An attacker contributes subtly mislabelled examples to an open-source medical imaging dataset. Models trained on this dataset systematically misclassify certain malignant tumours as benign when the image contains a specific watermark — creating a backdoor exploitable on-demand.
Mitigation: Training data provenance verification, anomaly detection in training datasets, differential privacy, data minimisation, and third-party training data audits.
● HIGH SEVERITY
Model Extraction & IP Theft
An attacker systematically queries a proprietary AI model's API to reconstruct a functionally equivalent model without access to training data or model weights — effectively stealing the model's intellectual property and commercial value. For India's AI startups and enterprises investing in proprietary models, model extraction is both a competitive and security threat. The extracted model can then be used to discover vulnerabilities or develop adversarial attacks against the original.
Example: A competitor makes millions of API queries to a proprietary fraud detection model, recording inputs and outputs. They train a surrogate model that mimics the target model's behaviour — effectively obtaining its capabilities without the R&D investment.
Mitigation: API rate limiting and query anomaly detection, output perturbation, watermarking model outputs, contractual API usage restrictions, and monitoring for systematic querying patterns.

14 — AI Glossary

AI Glossary

Key terms in artificial intelligence, machine learning, and AI governance — defined for legal, policy, and business professionals.

Core AI Concepts
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
The simulation of human intelligence processes by computer systems, encompassing learning, reasoning, and self-correction. Coined by John McCarthy at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference.
Machine Learning (ML)
A subset of AI where systems learn to perform tasks by identifying patterns in training data, rather than following explicit programmatic rules. Models improve with more data and experience.
Deep Learning
A subset of ML using artificial neural networks with many layers ("deep" networks). Deep learning excels at pattern recognition in images, audio, and text — underpinning most modern AI applications.
Large Language Model (LLM)
A deep learning model trained on vast text corpora to understand and generate human language. Examples include GPT-4, Gemini, Claude, and LLaMA. The basis of systems like ChatGPT.
Generative AI
AI systems that generate new content — text, images, audio, video, or code — rather than only classifying or analysing existing content. Includes LLMs, diffusion models (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E), and AI voice/video generators.
Neural Network
A computational architecture loosely inspired by biological neurons — consisting of interconnected nodes (neurons) organised in layers that process and transform information. The building block of deep learning.
Transformer
A neural network architecture introduced in "Attention Is All You Need" (2017) that uses self-attention mechanisms to process sequential data. The foundation of virtually all modern LLMs including GPT, BERT, Gemini, and Claude.
Reinforcement Learning (RL)
A training paradigm where an AI agent learns by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards or penalties. Used to train game-playing AI (AlphaGo) and to fine-tune LLMs for desired behaviours (RLHF).
AI Governance & Legal Terms
Algorithmic Accountability
The principle that organisations deploying AI systems that affect individuals should be responsible for the system's decisions and their impacts — including bias, errors, and unintended consequences.
Explainability / XAI
The ability of an AI system to provide understandable reasons for its outputs or decisions. Legally significant where AI decisions affect individuals' rights or interests — a growing requirement under global AI regulations and the DPDP Act's right to information.
Hallucination
When a generative AI produces confident, plausible-sounding but factually incorrect output. A critical risk in legal, medical, and financial contexts. Not an indicator of deception — the model has no intent — but a fundamental limitation of current LLM architectures.
Deepfake
AI-generated synthetic media (video, audio, or image) that realistically depicts a person saying or doing something they did not say or do. Technology based on GANs or diffusion models. Implicates Sections 66C, 66D, 66E, 67, 67A of the IT Act 2000 in India.
AI Risk (EU AI Act Definition)
The EU AI Act classifies AI by risk: Unacceptable Risk (banned — social scoring, subliminal manipulation); High Risk (regulated — biometrics, hiring, critical infrastructure); Limited Risk (transparency obligations); Minimal Risk (free to use with voluntary codes).
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
A hypothetical AI system capable of performing any intellectual task that a human can, with equal or greater proficiency across virtually all domains. Does not currently exist. Timelines are deeply contested among AI researchers.
Differential Privacy
A mathematical technique that adds calibrated noise to datasets or model outputs, ensuring that the inclusion or exclusion of any individual's data cannot be statistically detected — protecting privacy while enabling AI training on sensitive data.
Prompt Engineering
The practice of designing inputs (prompts) to AI language models to elicit desired outputs. A key skill for effective AI use — also relevant to security (prompt injection attacks exploit weaknesses in how models interpret instructions).
