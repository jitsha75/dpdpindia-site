Geospatial Data Under the DPDP Act:
When the Map Becomes the Risk

With the DPDP Act enacted and phased enforcement underway, can governments still manage geospatial data the way they always have? The answer is prompting a fundamental rethink of how spatial data is created, stored, and governed.

Published 27 March 2026
Category DPDP Act · Data Governance
Read time 7 min
DPDP Act 2023 IT Act 2000 Geospatial Data Data Fiduciary E-Governance GIS Policy

Government geospatial platforms have long been built on a foundational belief: maps and spatial data are technical public assets. They are tools of administration, instruments of planning, and the backbone of service delivery — not personal data. That belief is being tested, and the DPDP Act is the instrument doing the testing.

The Decentralisation Problem

Across government today, geospatial data is being created everywhere — and by everyone:

  • ▸ Individual departments commissioning their own drone surveys
  • ▸ Multiple agencies running dashcam and CCTV-based analytics
  • ▸ Project-specific GIS layers sitting on isolated systems, often managed by a single project team or vendor
  • ▸ Service providers without mature information security management frameworks holding critical spatial datasets

This decentralised model evolved for speed and convenience. Each department controlled its own data, acquired its own tools, and operated within its own perimeter. The logic was pragmatic: if each agency can create and manage its data, projects move faster and don't wait on central bottlenecks.

⚠ Legal Risk

Under the DPDP Act and the IT Act, this decentralised model creates significant compliance risk — particularly where fiduciary accountability is unclear. What was once an operational convenience may become a compliance liability. (Note: This is a policy compliance perspective. The DPDP Act does not prohibit decentralisation; it assigns accountability to the Data Fiduciary for all processing under their control.)

The Hard Questions Governments Must Ask

The legal exposure surfaces quickly once you start asking the right questions. These are not hypothetical — they are operational realities in virtually every state government running a digitised GIS function today:

  • Q If every department creates and stores its own geospatial data, who is accountable when that data enables personal or inferable identification?
  • Q When drone imagery and video surveys sit outside a common platform, who controls access, reuse, and retention?
  • Q How do we prevent unintended data sharing when the same spatial layer quietly flows across schemes and departments without formal data-sharing agreements?
  • Q What happens when one weak dataset becomes the entry point that exposes the entire ecosystem?
  • Q Are there geospatial datasets where audit, access control, or revocation mechanisms are unclear or fragmented?

Every one of these questions points to the same structural flaw: a patchwork of siloed ownership with no clear data fiduciary accountability at the centre.

Why Decentralisation Is Now a Liability

Under the combined force of the DPDP Act 2023 and the IT Act 2000, the legal consequences of the existing model are no longer abstract. Three specific dynamics are now in play:

  • § Data fiduciary responsibility shifts to the government platform owner. If a government entity — a department, an authority, a board — collects, processes, or stores personal or inferable data in a geospatial form, it is the data fiduciary under the DPDP Act. The vendor who built the GIS, the department that commissioned the drone survey, the agency running the CCTV analytics: accountability now attaches to them.
  • § Security failures are no longer local — they cascade across departments. When geospatial layers are shared informally across schemes, a breach in one dataset is a breach in all datasets that consumed it. The IT Act's provisions on security, and the DPDP Act's breach notification obligations, apply to the downstream consequence, not just the originating point.
  • § Breach response, audit trails, and accountability break down without central control. DPDP Act compliance requires that data principals can exercise their rights — including the right to erasure and grievance redressal. With data scattered across departments, systems, and vendors, exercising or enforcing these rights becomes operationally impossible.

"In the DPDP era, decentralised geospatial data creation without clear fiduciary accountability may no longer be agility — it may be compliance exposure." (Compliance policy perspective, not a legal determination.)

Taking Control: The Platform Imperative

The answer is not less data — but better governed data. The DPDP Act does not prohibit government from collecting and using geospatial information; it requires that such collection and use be lawful, purposeful, and accountable.

Governments now need a fundamentally different approach to how spatial data infrastructure is designed:

  • ✓ An enterprise-grade, role-based geospatial platform designed for secure data access, collaboration, and governance — not a collection of individual GIS tool licenses operated in silos.
  • ✓ Role-based, purpose-bound access controls — so that the officer in Department A who needs to see roads data cannot by default see residential survey data held by Department B.
  • ✓ Clear separation of data ownership, custodianship, and processing roles — so that the fiduciary, the processor, and the consuming department are formally distinguished in policy and in system design.
  • ✓ Built-in audit logs, retention policies, and breach readiness — so that when (not if) an incident occurs, the government can demonstrate it took all reasonable steps required under both the DPDP Act and the IT Act.
📌 Key Principle

The platform is not a technology project. It is a governance architecture decision. The technology follows the governance design — not the other way around. Governments that procure GIS platforms without embedding the fiduciary and processor framework into the contract and system design will face the same liability questions in three years that they face today.

Food for Thought

In the DPDP era, decentralised geospatial data creation without clear fiduciary accountability may no longer be pure agility — it may carry compliance exposure. The question governments should also be asking alongside "who can create data fastest?" is "who is accountable for the data created?"

"The real question is: who can create and govern it responsibly?"
