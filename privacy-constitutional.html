Constitutional Right to Privacy — Puttaswamy & the 4-Part Test

The 2017 Puttaswamy judgment by a unanimous 9-judge Constitution Bench established privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21 and Part III of the Constitution. This article analyses the judgment, its three-dimensional privacy framework, the four-part proportionality test, and its direct constitutional basis for the DPDP Act and Aadhaar jurisprudence.

01

Background & Constitutional History

The journey to constitutional recognition

Before 2017, the constitutional status of the right to privacy in India was deeply contested — two older judgments had cast doubt on its existence as a fundamental right.

Case Year Bench Holding on Privacy
M.P. Sharma v. Satish Chandra 1954 8-judge Privacy NOT a fundamental right; no constitutional right against search and seizure
Kharak Singh v. State of UP 1963 6-judge Divided bench; majority held domiciliary police visits not a fundamental rights violation. Justice Subba Rao dissented — powerfully argued privacy as part of Art. 21 personal liberty

For decades, these decisions created uncertainty — particularly as technology evolved and governments began collecting vast amounts of personal data. The Aadhaar project (launched 2009) and subsequent government mandates to link Aadhaar with bank accounts, mobile SIMs, and income tax filings brought the question to a crisis point.

The trigger: Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.), a former Karnataka High Court judge, challenged the constitutional validity of the Aadhaar project. The Government of India argued that Indians had no fundamental right to privacy. This extraordinary argument was referred to a 9-judge Constitution Bench to definitively resolve the conflict with M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh.

02

The 9-Judge Bench Decision (2017)

Puttaswamy and the core holding on fundamental rights

Case Details
Case: Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v. Union of India — (2017) 10 SCC 1
Decided: 24 August 2017
Bench: 9 judges (DY Chandrachud, RF Nariman, SA Bobde, AM Sapre, SK Kaul, Sanjay Kishan Kaul, and others)
Result: UNANIMOUS on the core holding; 6 separate concurring opinions

Core holding (UNANIMOUS): The right to privacy is an intrinsic part of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 of the Constitution, and is also protected by the freedoms in Part III (Articles 14, 19, and 21 read together). M.P. Sharma (8-judge) and Kharak Singh (6-judge) are overruled.

"Privacy is the ultimate expression of the sanctity of the individual. It is a constitutional value which straddles across the spectrum of fundamental rights and protects for the individual a zone of choice and self-determination." — Justice D.Y. Chandrachud (as he then was), Puttaswamy (2017)
Binding effect: As a 9-judge Constitution Bench decision, Puttaswamy is binding on all Indian courts without exception. It can only be overruled by another bench of 9 or more judges. The right to privacy is now as firmly established in Indian constitutional law as the right to equality (Art.14) or freedom of speech (Art.19).
03

Three Dimensions of Privacy

The framework established by the 9-judge bench

The Supreme Court identified three distinct dimensions of the right to privacy:

Dimension 1
Privacy of Place & Body
The right to be free from unwarranted intrusion into one's home and physical space. The state cannot conduct searches, domiciliary visits, or physical surveillance without lawful authority and due process. Protects against unreasonable search and seizure — the foundation of the 4th Amendment equivalent in Indian constitutional law.
Dimension 2
Informational Privacy — Control Over Personal Data
The right of individuals to control information about themselves — personal data, medical records, financial information, communications, sexual orientation, and political beliefs. This dimension is the constitutional foundation of the DPDP Act 2023. Any collection, processing, or retention of personal data by the state or private parties must be justified, proportionate, and accompanied by procedural safeguards.
Dimension 3
Decisional Autonomy — Privacy of Choice
The right to make autonomous decisions about matters of personal concern — lifestyle choices, sexual orientation, food preferences, religious practice, personal relationships, and reproductive decisions. The state cannot criminalise or penalise private choices that do not harm others. This dimension was directly applied in Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018) — decriminalising consensual same-sex relations.
04

The Four-Part Proportionality Test

How state restrictions on privacy must be justified

Any state action that restricts privacy must satisfy a four-part proportionality test unanimously endorsed by the Puttaswamy bench (drawn from German constitutional law and international human rights doctrine):

Prong 1
Legality
The restriction must be sanctioned by a valid enacted law passed by Parliament or a State Legislature. Executive action, administrative orders, or departmental circulars without legislative backing cannot restrict the right to privacy. A government notification mandating data collection must trace back to a parliamentary statute.
Prong 2
Legitimate Aim
The law must pursue a legitimate state objective — national security, public order, public health, crime prevention, or another constitutionally permissible aim. Mere administrative convenience, fiscal efficiency, or revenue maximisation are not sufficient legitimate aims to justify privacy restrictions.
Prong 3
Proportionality (Necessity)
The extent of the privacy restriction must be proportionate to the aim — the state must use the least intrusive means necessary to achieve the objective. A blanket, universal surveillance or data collection mandate that goes far beyond what is necessary fails this test, even if the underlying aim is legitimate.
Prong 4
Procedural Guarantees
The law must contain safeguards against abuse — independent oversight, judicial review mechanisms, grievance redressal, sunset clauses, and transparency requirements. A law that grants unchecked discretion to the executive, with no procedural accountability, fails this prong.
Constitutional challenge to DPDP Act Section 17: The Puttaswamy 4-part test is the constitutional yardstick against which the DPDP Act's broad government exemption under Section 17(1)(a) must be measured. A blanket exemption for all state processing "in the interests of sovereignty and security" — without proportionality limits or procedural safeguards — raises serious questions about whether it passes the third and fourth prongs of the Puttaswamy test. This remains a live constitutional challenge.
05

Puttaswamy II: The Aadhaar Judgment (2018)

Application to biometric identification and data protection

Case: K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India — (2019) 1 SCC 1 | Decided: 26 September 2018 | Bench: 5-judge

Upheld
Aadhaar Act as a means of identification for government subsidies and welfare benefits. The state's legitimate interest in preventing ghost beneficiaries and corruption in welfare delivery justifies proportionate biometric authentication.
Struck Down
(a) Section 57 — private entity use of Aadhaar authentication: STRUCK DOWN; (b) Mandatory Aadhaar-bank account linking: struck down as disproportionate; (c) Mandatory Aadhaar-SIM linking: struck down (no legislative backing); (d) Authentication log retention beyond 6 months: struck down.

Key data protection principles established by Puttaswamy II:

Data minimisation: Collect only what is necessary for the specific purpose — Aadhaar authentication should not be extended beyond its statutory purpose.
Purpose limitation: Authentication for welfare delivery ≠ authentication for commercial services.
Proportionality in private sector: Private companies cannot compel Aadhaar — the state-citizen relationship does not extend to private commercial relationships.
Log retention limits: Authentication logs (which can reveal movement and behaviour patterns) cannot be retained indefinitely.
06

Impact on DPDP Act & Future Challenges

The constitutional framework for data protection law

Puttaswamy established the constitutional architecture within which ALL Indian data protection law must operate. The DPDP Act is directly shaped by it.

Puttaswamy Principle DPDP Act Provision Status
Legality Parliamentary enactment (the Act itself) ✓ Met
Legitimate aim Consent + 7 deemed consent grounds (S.6–7) ✓ Largely met
Proportionality / Data minimisation S.8(2) — collect only necessary data Partially met — Rules to specify
Procedural safeguards DPB, grievance officer, appeal to TDSAT Concerns about DPB's independence from Govt
State exemption limits S.17(1)(a) — broad state exemption ⚠️ Constitutional challenge likely
Private sector prohibition on Aadhaar S.57 struck down in Puttaswamy II ✓ Aadhaar not used as mandatory private ID
Compliance framework: For compliance teams, the Puttaswamy 4-part test provides a first-principles privacy impact framework. Before collecting personal data, ask: (1) Is there a law authorising this? (2) What is the legitimate purpose? (3) Are we using the minimum data necessary? (4) What oversight and grievance mechanisms exist? If all four are answered satisfactorily, the processing is constitutionally grounded.
