DPDP 2023 Era — India's Data Protection Journey

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 and Rules 2025 represent India's entry into the era of comprehensive data protection. This spoke covers the SARAL framework, consent architecture, the Data Protection Board, cross-border data flows, penalties, and the implementation roadmap.

01

Overview & Structural Architecture

44 sections, principles-based, with details in Rules

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (No. 22 of 2023) received Presidential assent on 11 August 2023. It is India's first comprehensive, standalone data protection legislation. With 44 sections — deliberately compact — the Act adopts a principles-based architecture: broad obligations are set in the Act, with operational details delegated to Rules made by the Central Government.

Scope of application (s. 3):

  • Processing of digital personal data within India — whether collected digitally or collected in non-digital form and subsequently digitised.
  • Processing of digital personal data outside India, where such processing is in connection with any activity related to offering of goods or services to Data Principals within India.
  • Not applicable to: personal data processed for personal or domestic purposes; personal data made publicly available by the Data Principal or under law.
Key structural choice: Unlike GDPR (which has 99 Articles, 173 Recitals, and subsidiary regulations), the DPDP Act delegates extensively to Rules. This means the Act's practical compliance burden is defined primarily by the DPDP Rules 2025 — which are themselves subject to future amendment without Parliamentary approval.

The SARAL Framework

Seven core data protection principles embedded in the Act

The Act is built on the SARAL acronym — a set of data protection principles derived from international standards but tailored for India's context:

S — Safeguarding (Security)

Data Fiduciaries must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure security of personal data — including against breach, loss, or unauthorised processing. s. 8(5)

A — Accountability

Data Fiduciaries are accountable for compliance with the Act, including obligations of their Data Processors. Significant Data Fiduciaries face enhanced accountability obligations. s. 8, s. 10

R — Rights of Data Principals

Four fundamental rights — to information, correction/erasure, grievance redressal, and nomination — are enforceable against Data Fiduciaries. ss. 11–14

A — Accuracy & Data Quality

Fiduciaries must make reasonable efforts to ensure personal data is accurate and complete, especially where its use may affect the Data Principal significantly. s. 8(3)

L — Limitation (Purpose & Storage)

Personal data may only be processed for the specified, lawful purpose for which it was collected. Once that purpose is served, the data must be erased. s. 8(7)

What SARAL is not: The Act does not expressly codify all GDPR-equivalent principles. Notably, there is no explicit "transparency" principle separate from the notice requirement, and no express "fairness" principle. These gaps have attracted academic criticism but are partially addressed by the broad consent and notice obligations in ss. 5–6.
03

Consent & Notice Architecture

Sections 4–7 — the lawful basis framework

The DPDP Act provides two primary lawful bases for processing personal data:

1. Consent (s. 6): The Data Principal's free, specific, informed, unconditional and unambiguous consent, signified by a clear affirmative action. Key requirements:

  • Consent must be preceded by a notice (s. 5) describing what data will be processed, for what purpose, and the Data Principal's rights.
  • Notice must be in clear, plain language — available in the languages specified in the Eighth Schedule (all 22 official languages).
  • Consent may be withdrawn at any time — withdrawal does not affect the legality of processing already undertaken.
  • Data Fiduciaries must maintain records of consent given.
  • Consent Managers (s. 2(g)) — registered intermediaries — may act as a single window for a Data Principal to manage consents across multiple Fiduciaries.

2. Legitimate Uses (s. 7): Processing without consent is permitted for:

  • Performance of a function of the State — welfare benefits, issuance of licenses, permits, certificates under law.
  • Compliance with a court/tribunal order or judgment.
  • Responding to a medical emergency or epidemics.
  • Employment purposes — maintaining safety of employees, performing obligations under law.
  • Processing for public interest by the State and its instrumentalities.
No "legitimate interests" basis: Unlike GDPR Article 6(1)(f), the DPDP Act does not include a general "legitimate interests" lawful basis for private-sector processing. Indian companies cannot rely on a balancing test — processing must be based on consent or an express s. 7 category. This is a significant compliance difference.
04

Data Protection Board of India

Sections 18–33 — constitution, powers and adjudication

The Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) — established under s. 18 — is the central enforcement and adjudication body under the Act. It is distinct from the earlier proposed Data Protection Authority (DPA) concept: the DPBI is primarily a quasi-judicial body with adjudicatory and enforcement powers, rather than a full regulatory authority with rule-making powers (which are reserved for the Central Government).

Constitution (s. 19): The Chairperson and other Members are appointed by the Central Government on the recommendation of a Search Committee. Members must have significant expertise in data protection, information technology, law, or governance.

Powers of the DPBI:

  • Receive and inquire into complaints from Data Principals or references from the Central Government.
  • Direct Data Fiduciaries to take remedial measures.
  • Impose financial penalties after giving the Fiduciary an opportunity to be heard.
  • The DPBI may conduct proceedings digitally — complaints can be filed and hearings conducted online.
Digital-native design: The DPBI is designed to operate as a digital-first body — all proceedings, notices, orders and appeals are envisaged to be conducted through a technology platform. This reflects India's commitment to making data protection enforcement accessible without physical hearing requirements.

Appeals (s. 29): Orders of the DPBI may be appealed to the High Court having jurisdiction. This provides judicial oversight over the Board's enforcement actions.

Cross-Border Data Flows

Section 16 — the whitelist approach

Section 16 governs the transfer of personal data outside India. The Act adopts a whitelist approach — personal data may be transferred to any country or territory not restricted by the Central Government by notification. This is a significant departure from earlier Bills (which proposed localisation requirements) and from the GDPR's adequacy decision model.

The practical implications:

  • Transfers are permitted to all countries unless the Central Government publishes a restricted list. No restricted countries have been notified as of early 2026.
  • No requirement for standard contractual clauses (SCCs), binding corporate rules (BCRs), or adequacy assessments — unlike GDPR Chapter V.
  • The Central Government retains flexibility to restrict transfers to specific countries based on sovereignty, security, or diplomatic considerations.
Global trade impact: The whitelist approach is trade-friendly and addresses concerns raised by the IT/ITES industry about data localisation affecting cloud services and outsourcing. However, it also means Indian Data Principals' data may be transferred to jurisdictions with weaker privacy protections — a gap that may require contractual safeguards in practice.

Penalties & Enforcement

Section 33 — financial penalties up to ₹250 crore per breach

BreachMaximum Penalty
Failure to implement reasonable security safeguards resulting in a data breach s. 33(1)₹250 crore
Failure to notify the DPBI and affected Data Principals of a data breach s. 33(2)₹200 crore
Non-fulfilment of additional obligations for Significant Data Fiduciaries s. 33(3)₹150 crore
Failure to fulfil children's data obligations s. 33(3)₹200 crore
Non-fulfilment of Data Principal rights obligations s. 33(3)₹10 crore
Breach of Data Principal duties s. 33(4)₹10,000
Any other breach not specified above s. 33(7)₹50 crore
Penalty principles (s. 33(5)): The DPBI must consider: nature, gravity, duration of breach; type of personal data affected; repetitive nature; whether gain was derived; mitigating actions; whether the breach was deliberate or negligent; and timely reporting. The penalties are per-breach — a systemic failure affecting many Data Principals could attract multiple penalty orders.

DPDP Act vs GDPR — Key Differences

Understanding where India's law converges and diverges from the EU model

  • Scope: GDPR covers any personal data; DPDP Act covers only digital personal data (or data that has been digitised).
  • Lawful basis: GDPR has six lawful bases including "legitimate interests"; DPDP Act has only consent and enumerated "legitimate uses" — no balancing test.
  • Data Protection Officer: GDPR requires DPOs in many contexts; DPDP Act requires DPOs only for Significant Data Fiduciaries.
  • Data localisation: GDPR has adequacy decisions and transfer mechanisms; DPDP Act uses a simpler whitelist (restricted countries) approach.
  • Right to data portability: GDPR expressly provides this; DPDP Act does not — though it may be introduced via Rules.
  • Fines: GDPR: up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever higher); DPDP Act: up to ₹250 crore (~€28M) — comparable for smaller companies, lower for large multinationals.
  • Enforcement body: GDPR — independent Data Protection Authorities with rule-making power; DPDP Act — DPBI is primarily adjudicatory, rule-making is with Central Government.
Adequacy assessment: The EU has not yet granted India an adequacy decision under GDPR Article 45. This means Indian companies receiving personal data from the EU must rely on SCCs or BCRs for transfer legality. The DPDP Act's enactment strengthens India's case for adequacy, but a formal decision remains pending.
