Comparative Legal Analysis GDPR vs DPDP

India vs Europe:
What the GDPR Gets Right That the DPDP Act Still Needs to Address

The EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) set the global standard for data protection. As India's DPDP Act 2023 enters force, a gap analysis reveals where the Indian framework falls short — and what must be resolved in the Rules.

Published 27 March 2026
Category Comparative Law
Read time 9 min
GDPR DPDP Act 2023 Comparative Law Data Protection EU-India Regulatory Analysis

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which came into force on 25 May 2018, has become the global gold standard for privacy protection. No other regime has matched its scope, enforceability, or impact on corporate practice. Now, as India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 enters its operational phase, policymakers and practitioners are asking a legitimate question: Does India's framework match GDPR's rigour? And if not, where are the material gaps? The answer is nuanced — but it reveals important work that India's Rules framework must still complete.

Editorial Note: This article presents a comparative policy analysis of GDPR and the DPDP Act 2023. All comparisons reflect policy-level observations and are not statements of legal deficiency. The DPDP Act and GDPR operate in different constitutional, regulatory, and enforcement contexts. This is a comparative policy perspective, not a legal gap assessment.

Consent: Burden of Proof and the Pre-Ticked Box Problem

The GDPR's approach to consent is notoriously exacting. Article 7 places the burden of proof squarely on the data controller: the controller must be able to demonstrate, at any time and to the regulator, that consent was obtained. Pre-ticked boxes, opt-out mechanisms, and inactivity-as-consent are all invalid under the GDPR. In the landmark Planet49 GmbH case (CJEU 2019), the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled that consent must involve an affirmative action — silence or inaction does not suffice.

The DPDP Act s. 6 mirrors this requirement on paper: consent must be obtained through "clear affirmative action." However, the Act then creates a significant exception at s. 7: deemed consent. Under s. 7, deemed consent may arise in specific, limited statutory scenarios — it is not a general alternative to consent under s. 6, and its scope is expected to be narrowly interpreted by the Board. This is fundamentally different from the GDPR model.

  • ▸ GDPR burden: Controller must prove affirmative consent; silence is not consent. Default position: NO consent.
  • ▸ DPDP position: Consent is assumed unless the principal objects. Default position: YES consent (with notice). A critical inversion.
⚠ Gap Identified

The DPDP Act does not explicitly place the burden of proof on the data fiduciary in the way GDPR does. The Rules framework must clarify: (1) what constitutes "clear affirmative action" under s. 6; (2) the scope and permitted use cases for deemed consent under s. 7; and (3) whether the burden of proof lies with the fiduciary (as in GDPR) or with the principal challenging the consent.

Rights: What India's Framework Grants and What It Withholds

The GDPR grants data subjects a comprehensive suite of rights: right of access (Art. 15), right to erasure (Art. 17), right to rectification (Art. 16), right to restrict processing (Art. 18), right to object (Art. 21), right to data portability (Art. 20), and rights relating to automated decision-making (Art. 22). These rights are not advisory — they are enforceable, with fines of up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover for violations.

The DPDP Act grants weaker, narrower rights:

  • ✓ Right of Access (s. 11): Data principals can request access to personal data. Comparable to GDPR Art. 15.
  • ✗ Right to Erasure (s. 12): Granted only under narrow conditions (data no longer necessary, consent withdrawn, legal basis removed). GDPR Art. 17 is broader and includes a right to erasure as a standalone entitlement.
  • ✗ Right to Data Portability: ABSENT from the DPDP Act. GDPR Art. 20 grants the right to receive personal data in a structured, commonly used format. This is a major omission.
  • ✗ Right to Restrict Processing: ABSENT from the DPDP Act. GDPR Art. 18 allows data subjects to restrict processing even if the data fiduciary contests the restriction.
  • ✗ Right to Object / Automated Decision-Making: ABSENT from the DPDP Act. GDPR Art. 22 grants data subjects the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing. The DPDP Act has no equivalent.
  • ✓ Grievance Redressal (s. 13): Data principals can lodge grievances. Comparable to GDPR procedures but narrower in scope.
📌 Data Portability Absence

The absence of a data portability right in the DPDP Act is a significant structural gap. In the digital economy, the ability to extract and transfer one's data between services is essential to consumer autonomy and prevents lock-in. India's Rules framework should introduce this right, either through amendment or through regulatory elaboration of existing grievance mechanisms.

Enforcement: Where India's Penalties Fall Short

The GDPR's enforcement power is legendary — and deliberately so. Article 83 sets a two-tier penalty structure: up to €10 million or 2% of annual global turnover (whichever is higher) for less serious violations, and up to €20 million or 4% of annual global turnover for serious breaches. This formula has produced some of the largest privacy fines in history: Meta faced €1.2 billion in 2023; TikTok, €12.7 million in 2023; Amazon, €746 million in 2021.

The DPDP Act, by contrast, uses a Schedule of penalties that is significantly more modest:

  • ▸ GDPR Formula: Up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher). Applies to all controllers and processors.
  • ▸ DPDP Schedule: Maximum ₹250 crore (approximately €28 million) per breach. No turnover-based scaling; no global revenue formula; fixed ceiling regardless of fiduciary size.

While the DPDP's ₹250 crore ceiling is numerically higher than €20 million in EUR terms, it lacks the GDPR's crucial feature: percentage-of-turnover scaling. A multinational tech company processing data for 500 million Indians faces the same maximum penalty ceiling as a small fintech startup — a misalignment of incentives that weakens enforcement.

⚠ Enforcement Gap

India's Rules framework should consider introducing a percentage-of-turnover component to penalties for large-scale data fiduciaries. A tiered approach (e.g., smaller fiduciaries: fixed penalty; large processors: fixed + percentage) would improve deterrence and align DPDP enforcement with global standards.

Data Protection Officer: Mandatory Independence vs Optional Assignment

Under GDPR Article 37, a Data Protection Officer (DPO) is mandatory for: (1) public authorities and agencies (all of them), and (2) organisations whose core activities involve large-scale systematic monitoring of data subjects. The DPO must be independent, qualified, and directly accountable to the board or senior management. The DPO has specific powers to conduct audits, advise on compliance, and handle data subject requests.

The DPDP Act has no equivalent mandate. Instead, s. 6(5) references a Consent Manager — a different role focused on managing consent collection and withdrawal. The Consent Manager role is narrower and is not framed as an independent governance position. Moreover, the DPDP Act does not mandate a DPO or equivalent role for government entities or large-scale data processors.

  • ▸ GDPR approach: Mandatory DPO for public authorities; independent role with board-level visibility and audit powers.
  • ▸ DPDP approach: Optional Consent Manager for managing consent flows; no board-level mandate; no audit or independence requirements clearly articulated.

This gap is particularly significant for large government data platforms (e.g., Aadhaar, state health registries, financial databases) and large private-sector processors (e.g., fintech platforms, e-commerce giants). The absence of an independent DPO-equivalent role means accountability for compliance rests with the organisation itself — creating potential conflicts of interest.

Cross-Border Data Flows: Adequacy Framework vs Negative List

The GDPR's Chapter V establishes an intricate framework for cross-border data transfers. The key mechanism is the adequacy decision: the European Commission can determine that a third country provides a level of protection adequate to that in the EU. If an adequacy decision exists, data can flow freely; if not, controllers must implement standard contractual clauses (SCCs), binding corporate rules (BCRs), or other specific safeguards. The CJEU's Schrems II decision (2020) imposed additional supplementary measures on SCCs, recognising risks of government surveillance in third countries.

The DPDP Act takes a different approach. Section 16 authorises the Central Government to restrict cross-border flows to a notified list of countries. This is a negative-list model: flows are permitted by default unless specifically restricted. The Act does not establish an affirmative adequacy framework, nor does it mandate specific contractual safeguards or supplementary measures.

  • Q GDPR Model: White-list (adequacy decisions) + contractual safeguards (SCCs/BCRs) + supplementary measures. Burden on controller to justify transfer.
  • Q DPDP Model: Black-list (restricted countries) + default permission. Burden on government to restrict, not on controller to justify.
📌 Adequacy Framework Need

India's Rules should establish clarity on which countries/data transfer mechanisms (SCCs-equivalent) are compliant under the DPDP Act. Without an articulated adequacy or SCC-equivalent framework, Indian fiduciaries will lack clear guidance on cross-border transfers — a critical gap for multinational enterprises and India's digital services exports.

Children's Data: Age of Consent and Targeting Restrictions

The GDPR, in Article 8, sets the age of digital consent at 16 years (though member states can lower it to 13 with national legislation). More importantly, Article 8 establishes parental responsibility: processing of children's data requires verifiable parental consent. Additionally, GDPR Chapter II (Principles) and Article 21 (Right to Object) apply to children's data just as to adults'.

The DPDP Act addresses children's data in s. 9, but with important caveats. Children are defined as persons under 18; verifiable parental consent is required; and processing must not involve targeting or tracking children. However, the Act does not clearly specify:

  • ▸ Age threshold for digital autonomy: At what age can a child consent without parental involvement? The DPDP Act is silent.
  • ▸ Definition of "targeting": What constitutes prohibited targeting or tracking? The DPDP Act uses the term but provides no regulatory definition.
  • ▸ Scope of parental consent: Is parental consent required for all processing, or only for specific high-risk categories?

The Rules framework should elaborate these definitions with specificity. GDPR's approach — combining age-gated consent with a broader prohibition on profiling and targeting — is a better model than the DPDP Act's current formulation.

"The GDPR places the burden on those who collect data. The DPDP Act, in several areas, places the burden on those who must prove they had no choice. That inversion is the framework's central weakness."

Supervisory Authority: Independence and One-Stop-Shop

The GDPR vests enforcement authority in independent Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) — in Europe's case, one per member state, coordinated through the European Data Protection Board. These authorities are designed to be independent from government; they have budget autonomy; and they can impose fines directly without political approval. The GDPR's Article 60 "one-stop-shop" mechanism allows businesses operating across the EU to interact with one DPA rather than navigating 27 separate regimes.

The DPDP Act establishes the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) — but with important governance constraints. The DPBI is appointed by the Central Government; its independence is dependent on government's commitment to that independence (unlike the GDPR's statutory independence framework); and there is no elaborated "one-stop-shop" mechanism for cross-border or multi-state matters.

  • ▸ GDPR structure: Independent statutory DPAs with budget autonomy, direct fine-imposition power, and board-level accountability.
  • ▸ DPDP structure: DPBI appointed by Central Government; question of institutional independence if government disagrees with enforcement decisions; no articulated one-stop-shop mechanism.
📌 Independence as Implementation Challenge

The Rules framework cannot unilaterally resolve the DPBI's structural independence — that requires legislative amendment. However, the Rules can establish clear procedural safeguards: transparent appointment criteria, term protections, and publication of all enforcement decisions. These procedural guardrails can partially substitute for statutory independence.

What India's Second Iteration of Rules Must Resolve

The gaps identified above are not minor. They reflect structural choices in the DPDP Act that, while intentional, leave significant regulatory vacancies. India's Rules framework — currently being drafted by the Department of Personnel and Training in consultation with the DPBI and stakeholder ministries — has the opportunity to fill these gaps through regulatory specification:

  • ✓ Explicit Burden of Proof: Clarify that the burden of proving affirmative consent rests with the fiduciary, not with the data principal. Define what constitutes "clear affirmative action" under s. 6 with specificity.
  • ✓ Data Portability Right: Introduce a regulatory right of data portability (even if not in the Act) through elaboration of grievance redressal mechanisms (s. 13). This would align DPDP with GDPR on this critical right.
  • ✓ Turnover-Based Penalties: Establish a tiered penalty schedule that includes a percentage-of-turnover component for large-scale processors and public-sector fiduciaries. This would increase deterrence for sophisticated actors.
  • ✓ DPO Equivalent Mandate: Require large-scale data fiduciaries (above a specified data volume threshold) to appoint an independent Data Protection Officer with audit and board-reporting functions.
  • ✓ Cross-Border Adequacy Framework: Establish a list of countries/jurisdictions recognised as providing adequate data protection, and specify permitted transfer mechanisms (SCCs-equivalent) for non-adequate jurisdictions.
  • ✓ Children's Data Safeguards: Define "targeting" and "tracking"; set age thresholds for independent vs parental consent; and establish specific prohibitions on profiling children under 13.
Where the Gap Matters Most

The DPDP Act is not deficient — it is incomplete. It establishes foundational principles that many global regimes lack: clarity on fiduciary responsibility, purpose limitation, and transparency. But it lacks the architectural rigour of the GDPR in critical areas: burden of proof, comprehensive rights, enforcement proportionality, and supervisory independence.

"India's second Rules framework is the chance to close these gaps before they become embedded in practice."
