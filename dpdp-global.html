01

GDPR (EU) — The Gold Standard

Privacy as Dignity — six lawful bases, 4% turnover penalties

Lawful Bases
Six Grounds for Processing
GDPR provides six lawful bases: consent, contract necessity, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, and legitimate interests. The broad 'legitimate interests' ground is absent from India's DPDP Act — a deliberate divergence giving Indian data principals stronger protection against non-consensual processing.
Penalties
4% of Global Turnover or €20M
GDPR's tiered penalty structure — up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover for tier-1 violations — produces penalties orders of magnitude larger than the DPDP Act's ₹250Cr cap for large multinationals. Meta's €1.2 billion fine (2023) illustrates the scale difference.
Rights
Broader Rights Including Portability
GDPR includes a data portability right (Article 20), an automated decision-making right (Article 22), and the 'right to be forgotten' — broader than India's Section 12. The DPDP Act's rights framework is more limited but accessible without legal fees.
Cross-Border
Standard Contractual Clauses
GDPR requires specific mechanisms for cross-border data transfers — adequacy decisions, SCCs, Binding Corporate Rules. The DPDP Act takes a whitelist approach — significantly more liberal for outbound transfers.
DPDP vs GDPR — key divergences: India chose fixed-cap over turnover-based penalties; no legitimate interests ground; no portability right; liberal cross-border transfer defaults; broader government exemptions; and a shorter, more principles-based statute with delegation to executive rules.
02

US Sectoral Framework & California CCPA

HIPAA, GLBA, and the state-level consumer rights patchwork

Federal Sectoral
HIPAA (Healthcare) & GLBA (Finance)
HIPAA governs health data; GLBA governs financial data. Both predate GDPR and lack the comprehensive scope of modern privacy laws. Neither covers social media, e-commerce, or data brokers — major gaps that state laws have attempted to fill.
California CCPA/CPRA
The US 'GDPR' Equivalent
California's Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and CPRA expansion provide the most GDPR-like rights in the US: right to know, right to delete, right to opt out of sale of data, and non-discrimination rights. A California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) enforces the law.
Federal Proposals
ADPPA — Still in Draft
The American Data Privacy and Protection Act (ADPPA) has been debated in Congress but not enacted. If passed, it would create a federal comprehensive framework and preempt state laws — but political consensus has been elusive.
India Comparison
India More Uniform
India's DPDP Act is more comprehensive and nationally uniform than the US approach — a single federal law applies to all sectors (subject to exemptions). However, India's fixed-cap penalties are lower than California's per-violation structure for large breaches.
03

DIFC Dubai & China PIPL — Emerging Frameworks

The Middle East corridor and China's strict localisation regime

DIFC Law No.5 of 2020
Dubai's GDPR-Aligned Corridor
The Dubai International Financial Centre's Data Protection Law No.5 of 2020 is explicitly GDPR-equivalent — enabling DIFC-registered entities to serve as a compliant bridge between MEASA and Western markets. It uses the same six lawful bases as GDPR, has a dedicated Commissioner, and mirrors GDPR's SCCs for cross-border transfers.
China PIPL (2021)
Strict Localisation & State Access
China's Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL, 2021) mandates strict data localisation for Critical Information Infrastructure operators and requires a Personal Information Protection Impact Assessment for cross-border transfers. India consciously chose not to follow PIPL's strict localisation model.
