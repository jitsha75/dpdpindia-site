Cryptocurrency & VDA
Law in India

From the RBI's 2018 banking ban — struck down by the Supreme Court in IAMAI v RBI — to the Finance Act 2022 VDA tax framework, PMLA reporting obligations, and the CBDC digital rupee: the complete legal landscape for virtual digital assets in India.

VDA India RBI v IAMAI 2020 §115BBH Tax PMLA Crypto CBDC Finance Act 2022
Regulatory History

The Indian Crypto Regulatory Timeline

2013

First RBI Cautionary Press Release

RBI issued its first public advisory warning against dealing in Virtual Currencies, noting their lack of legal tender status and associated risks. No regulatory ban at this stage.

6 April 2018

RBI Circular — Banking Ban

RBI directed all regulated entities (banks, NBFCs, payment system providers) to stop providing services in relation to Virtual Currencies. Effectively cut off crypto exchanges from the formal banking system.

4 March 2020

IAMAI v RBI — SC Strikes Down Ban

Supreme Court 3-Judge Bench set aside the 2018 circular. Held: RBI had the competence to regulate, but the total banking ban failed the proportionality test — RBI did not demonstrate actual harm to regulated entities from crypto trading.

1 February 2022

Finance Act 2022 — VDA Tax Framework

Section 115BBH: flat 30% tax on income from transfer of VDAs. Section 194S: 1% TDS on consideration for VDA transfers above threshold. No deductions, no set-off of losses against other income.

7 March 2023

PMLA Notification — Crypto Exchanges as Reporting Entities

VDA service providers — exchanges, VDA transfer services, VDA safekeeping — brought under PMLA 2002 as reporting entities. Must maintain KYC, file STRs, conduct due diligence.

December 2022–2024

RBI CBDC Pilot — Digital Rupee (e₹)

RBI launched retail CBDC pilot in December 2022. By 2024, expanded to multiple banks. The digital rupee is legal tender — categorically different from VDAs.

Statutory Definition

What Is a Virtual Digital Asset?

§ 2(47A) Income Tax Act 1961 (inserted by Finance Act 2022)

"Virtual Digital Asset" means — (a) any information or code or number or token (not being Indian currency or foreign currency), generated through cryptographic means or otherwise, by whatever name called, providing a digital representation of value exchanged with or without consideration, with the promise or representation of having inherent value, or functions as a store of value or a unit of account including its use in any financial transaction or investment, but not limited to investment scheme; and can be transferred, stored or traded electronically; (b) a non-fungible token or any other token of similar nature, by whatever name called; (c) any other digital asset, as the Central Government may, by notification in the Official Gazette, specify.

The definition is intentionally broad. Bitcoin, Ethereum, all altcoins, and NFTs are VDAs. The Central Government retains residual power to specify additional assets. Importantly, the definition excludes Indian currency, foreign currency, and any Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) — so the digital rupee is not a VDA.

Landmark Judgment

Internet & Mobile Association of India v RBI (2020)

Supreme Court of India · (2020) 10 SCC 274 · 3-Judge Bench

Internet & Mobile Association of India v Reserve Bank of India

Challenge: Crypto exchanges and IAMAI challenged the April 2018 RBI circular that directed regulated entities to exit all VC-related business.

RBI's power conceded: The bench confirmed that RBI has competence to regulate under §35A Banking Regulation Act, §45JA RBI Act, and §10(2) Payment & Settlement Systems Act. RBI's regulatory reach over activities that may affect the financial system was not in dispute.

Proportionality test failed: The circular failed because RBI could not demonstrate that Virtual Currency trading had caused any harm to regulated entities. The prohibition was held disproportionate — less restrictive measures (KYC, reporting, escrow, ring-fencing) were available but not tried.

Effect: Banks may again serve crypto exchanges. Trading is legal but remains unregulated except through the tax and PMLA frameworks introduced in 2022–23.

RBI circular set aside — Crypto banking restored
Tax Framework

VDA Taxation — Finance Act 2022

ProvisionRate / RuleKey Points
§115BBH — Income TaxFlat 30% + surcharge + cessNo deductions allowed except cost of acquisition. No set-off of VDA losses against any other income. No carry-forward of losses to next year.
§194S — TDS1% TDS on considerationApplicable on transfer of VDA. Threshold: ₹10,000 per year (₹50,000 for specified persons). Deducted by buyer/exchange.
GST on exchange fees18% GSTApplies to exchange/brokerage fees. The VDA transfer itself is not treated as a "supply of goods/services" for GST purposes.
Gift of VDATaxable as income in recipient's handsFMV of VDA on date of gift taxed as "income from other sources" unless exempt under §56(2)(x) exceptions.
Tax Planning Note

The 30% rate and prohibition on loss set-off make VDA taxation structurally different from equity. A trader cannot offset crypto losses against salary income or business profits. Each transaction is treated independently for loss purposes.

AML/CFT Compliance

PMLA 2023 Notification — Crypto Exchanges as Reporting Entities

With effect from 7 March 2023, the Ministry of Finance notification extended PMLA 2002 obligations to Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) — the Indian implementation of FATF Recommendation 15.

Who Is Covered

  • VDA exchanges (spot and derivatives)
  • VDA transfer services
  • VDA safekeeping / custodian services
  • Administration of VDAs or instruments enabling control over VDAs
  • Participation in financial services related to VDA issuance or sale (ICOs, IDOs)

Key PMLA Obligations

  • KYC / CDD: Full customer due diligence before onboarding; enhanced due diligence for high-risk customers
  • Record keeping: All transaction records for 5 years
  • STR filing: Suspicious Transaction Reports to FIU-IND within prescribed time
  • Travel Rule: Originator and beneficiary information must accompany transfers above threshold
  • Registration: FIU-IND registration required (portal: fiuindia.gov.in)

Comparative Framework

Global Crypto Regulation — Where India Stands

  • EU — MiCA Regulation (2024): Comprehensive licensing framework for crypto asset service providers. Stablecoin issuers face capital and reserve requirements. India's approach is lighter-touch by comparison.
  • US — Executive Order 14067 (2022): Coordinated R&D and regulatory analysis; SEC enforcement actions against exchanges; ongoing Congressional battles on classification of crypto as securities vs commodities.
  • G20 Roadmap (2023): India as G20 President championed the FSB/IMF roadmap for global VDA regulation; adopted FATF Travel Rule as standard.
  • China: Complete ban on crypto trading and mining since 2021.
  • El Salvador: Bitcoin as legal tender (2021) — the only country to do so; IMF conditions required narrowing the programme in 2024.
