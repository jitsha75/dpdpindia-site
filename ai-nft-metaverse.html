AI, NFTs & Metaverse
Legal Framework India

India has no standalone AI law — but AI is already regulated through the DPDP Act, IT Act, Consumer Protection Act, Copyright Act, and the 2026 deepfake rules. NFTs are VDAs under the IT Act tax regime. The metaverse raises jurisdiction and property questions that existing law imperfectly addresses.

AI Regulation

India's AI Legal Framework — Cross-Cutting Statutes

India currently has no dedicated AI statute. Regulation is achieved through a combination of existing laws applied to AI-specific fact patterns, supplemented by advisory frameworks and sectoral guidance. The MeitY AI Governance Report 2025 recommends a risk-tiered, sector-specific approach — mandatory provenance for synthetic media, voluntary codes of practice for developers — rather than a horizontal AI Act on the EU model.

Data Protection

DPDP Act 2023

AI systems that process personal data must comply with DPDP Act consent, purpose limitation, and data minimisation obligations. §11(2) gives Data Principals the right to seek correction of inaccurate data — relevant where AI-driven profiling produces errors. Section 9 imposes strict rules on children's data processing by AI systems.

Cybersecurity

IT Act 2000 + IT Rules

§66C and §66D apply to AI-enabled identity fraud (deepfake voice/video). IT Rules 2026 mandate 24-hour takedown of non-consensual synthetic intimate imagery and require provenance metadata (watermarking/C2PA) for AI-generated content on major platforms.

Consumer Protection

Consumer Protection Act 2019

§83 — product liability applies to AI systems. Manufacturer, service provider, and product seller are all potentially liable for harm caused by defective AI. "Defect" includes unsafe design, failure to warn, and manufacturing defect. A medical diagnostic AI that misdiagnoses can attract product liability under CPA.

Financial Sector

RBI & SEBI Guidance

RBI Working Group on Ethical AI in BFSI (2023) — internal use guidance for banks on AI fairness, explainability, and risk. SEBI Cybersecurity Framework (2024) extended to AI-driven trading systems. Digital lending guidelines prohibit discrimination on protected attributes in AI credit-scoring.

Liability Analysis

AI Liability — Applicable Doctrines

Product Liability (Consumer Protection Act §83)

The CPA 2019 product liability framework applies to AI products and services. Three potential defendants: (1) the manufacturer/developer of the AI system; (2) the service provider who deploys it; (3) the product seller/distributor. The claimant must show the AI product was defective and that defect caused harm. In a medical AI context: the developer, the hospital deploying it, and any intermediary distributing the software could all be jointly liable.

Negligence

Standard tortious negligence — duty of care, breach, causation, damage — applies to AI deployment. Deploying an autonomous vehicle, a credit-scoring AI, or a medical diagnostic tool without adequate testing and safeguards creates a foreseeable risk of harm sufficient to establish duty of care. Indian courts have not yet decided an autonomous-system negligence case, but the doctrinal path is clear.

Vicarious Liability

An employer may be vicariously liable for AI agent actions taken within the scope of authorised use. Where AI is deployed by an employee as an organisational tool, the employer-principal relationship with the AI-agent creates exposure similar to vicarious liability for employee acts.

Strict Liability — The MC Mehta Analogy

For "ultra-hazardous" AI applications — autonomous weaponry, AI controlling critical infrastructure — the strict liability rule from MC Mehta v Union of India (1987 Oleum Gas case) may apply: those who deploy hazardous activities are absolutely liable for harm, with no exceptions for acts of God or third parties.

Intellectual Property

AI & Intellectual Property in India

Copyright — AI Cannot Be an Author

The Copyright Act 1957 §17 vests first ownership in the "author." An "author" is a natural person. AI systems are not natural persons and cannot be authors or first owners of copyright in India. The human who provides creative direction, selects outputs, and makes creative choices retains authorship, but the degree of human creativity required to attract copyright protection in AI-assisted works remains unresolved.

Patent Inventorship

The Patents Act 1970 requires an inventor to be a natural person. Following Thaler v Comptroller-General of Patents (UK SC 2023) — which held AI cannot be an inventor under UK law — India is likely to reach the same conclusion. The DABUS cases in India have not reached the Patent Office yet, but the statutory text points clearly toward a human-only inventorship requirement.

Training Data Copyright

The use of copyrighted works to train AI models is the most active global litigation area. Key cases — Andersen v Stability AI, Getty Images v Stability AI (both US/UK) — are pending. Indian copyright law has no explicit text-and-data mining exception (unlike the EU AI Act / DSM Directive), creating legal uncertainty for AI developers using Indian-language training data scraped from the internet.

Synthetic Media

Deepfake Regulation — IT Rules 2026

India's 2026 IT Rules amendment specifically addresses synthetic media:

  • 24-hour takedown: Significant Social Media Intermediaries (SSMIs) must remove flagged deepfake content within 24 hours of complaint
  • Provenance metadata: AI-generated content must carry provenance metadata (C2PA or equivalent standard) identifying it as synthetic
  • No non-consensual intimate imagery: NCII generated by deepfake tools falls under both IT Rules and §66E IT Act (voyeurism)
  • Electoral deepfakes: Election Commission guidelines prohibit AI-generated political content that misrepresents candidate statements

Non-Fungible Tokens

NFTs in Indian Law

NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) are unique digital tokens on a blockchain identifying ownership of a specific asset. India has chosen to regulate NFTs primarily through the tax framework, treating them as Virtual Digital Assets.

Tax Classification

NFTs are explicitly included within the definition of VDA under §2(47A) Income Tax Act 1961 (Finance Act 2022). Transfer of NFTs attracts the same 30% flat tax and 1% TDS regime as other VDAs. Gifting an NFT creates income in the recipient's hands at FMV on date of receipt (subject to §56(2)(x) exceptions).

PMLA Treatment

The 7 March 2023 PMLA notification covers NFT service providers — platforms that facilitate NFT creation, trading, and transfer — as VASPs with full AML/KYC obligations.

Copyright — What NFT Ownership Does NOT Give You

This is the most misunderstood aspect: buying an NFT does not transfer copyright in the underlying artwork, music, or other content unless there is an express written copyright assignment. NFT ownership is a blockchain record of token ownership, not ownership of the intellectual property. The artist retains all copyright unless contractually assigned.

SEBI's Position on Fractional NFTs

SEBI has flagged that fractional NFTs representing shares of physical assets (art, real estate) may constitute a "Collective Investment Scheme" or "security" under SCRA 1956, requiring SEBI registration. This remains an evolving regulatory area.

Virtual Worlds

Metaverse — Jurisdiction & Legal Issues

What Is the Metaverse?

The metaverse refers to interconnected virtual spaces with persistent avatars, social interaction, and economic transactions. Major platforms (Decentraland, Roblox, Meta Horizon) allow users from multiple jurisdictions to transact, socialise, and own virtual property simultaneously.

Jurisdiction

Where an act causes effects in India, Indian law applies under the "targeting" and "effects" tests — even if the server and the other user are abroad. §75 IT Act explicitly extends Indian jurisdiction to offences committed outside India where they involve a computer, computer system, or computer network in India.

Contracts in the Metaverse

§10A IT Act applies — electronic form alone does not make a contract unenforceable. Transactions in the metaverse are contractually binding if the essential ICA requirements (offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity) are met. Smart contracts on blockchain platforms are contracts, albeit with interpretation challenges where the code does not match the parties' intent.

Virtual Property

Virtual land, avatars, and in-game items are contractual rights in Indian law — not in rem property rights. Ownership means the platform's terms of service grant you specific rights, not absolute property ownership enforceable against the world. Platform bankruptcy or shutdown extinguishes virtual property rights without recourse under property law.

Harassment in Virtual Spaces

Sexual harassment and assault of avatars in virtual spaces: §§75–79 BNS 2023 on offences against women likely apply where the conduct causes real-world distress to an identifiable person, though this is an emerging and untested area. IT Act §66E (voyeurism) may apply to virtual spaces where intimate imagery is captured or transmitted.
