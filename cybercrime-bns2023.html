BNS 2023 & Cyber Crimes — New Legal Framework Post-IPC

This article analyses every BNS provision relevant to cyber crimes — new offences, modified sections, enhanced penalties, the landmark Section 111 organised crime provision, and how the BNS interacts with the IT Act for dual prosecution.

BNS 2023: Overview & Commencement

The historic transition from IPC to the new statutory framework

The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS) received Presidential assent on 25 December 2023 and came into force on 1 July 2024, replacing the Indian Penal Code 1860. The Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita 2023 (BNSS) replaced CrPC, and the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023 (BSA) replaced the Indian Evidence Act — the three statutes together modernise India's criminal justice framework.

Key Structural Changes for Cyber Crime:

Organised crime is now a standalone central law offence (Section 111) — previously only under state laws like MCOCA
Terrorism explicitly defined (Section 113) — aligns with and overlaps IT Act Section 66F
Technology-neutral drafting throughout — "electronic" and "digital" means explicitly included in cheating, forgery, stalking, and extortion provisions
Enhanced penalties for cyber-enabled fraud and impersonation
Petty organised crime (Section 112) — covers online fraud rings and mule account networks
Critical transition point: All offences committed BEFORE 1 July 2024 continue to be prosecuted under the IPC and CrPC. BNS and BNSS apply only to offences on or after 1 July 2024. Charge sheets, FIRs, and court pleadings must specify the correct statute based on date of alleged offence. Mixing IPC and BNS sections in the same charge sheet (for a post-July 2024 offence) is an error.

Organised Cybercrime: Section 111 BNS

A landmark provision for syndicate-level enforcement

Section 111 BNS is a landmark provision — for the first time in central Indian law, organised crime is defined and specifically criminalised. Previously only Maharashtra (MCOCA), Karnataka, and a few other states had such laws.

Definition of "Organised Crime Syndicate":

A group of two or more persons who, acting in concert, singly or collectively, as members of or on behalf of an organised crime syndicate, indulge in a continuing unlawful activity including violence, intimidation, coercion, or other unlawful means with the objective of gaining pecuniary benefits or undue economic or other advantage.

Cyber Application of Section 111 BNS:

Large-scale cyber fraud call centres running IVR/OTP scams
Cryptocurrency fraud syndicates with mule account networks
Ransomware-as-a-service operations with multiple actors (developers, affiliates, negotiators, launders)
Credential stuffing and account takeover networks operating systematically
Investment/stock market manipulation via social media (pig-butchering scams)
Darknet marketplace operators

Penalties:

Imprisonment not less than 5 years, extendable to life imprisonment + fine not less than ₹5 lakh. Commission of organised crime resulting in death of any person → life imprisonment or death.

Systemic prosecution: Section 111 allows prosecutors to take a systemic view of cybercrime operations — targeting the entire network (recruiters, money mules, technical operators, finance handlers, masterminds) under a single organised crime charge with severe minimum penalties. This transforms how large-scale cyber fraud can be prosecuted, moving beyond piecemeal individual charge sheets to enterprise-level accountability.

BNS vs IT Act: Lex Specialis & Dual Charging

When both statutes apply and how to prosecute

The fundamental principle: The IT Act, as a special law (lex specialis), generally prevails over the BNS (general law) for cyber-specific offences. However, this does not prevent concurrent charging under both statutes.

Concurrent charging best practice: When concurrent charges are filed, the accused is generally sentenced under the provision with the highest applicable penalty. Prosecutors should consider both statutes at the time of charge sheet filing — adding BNS charges later may require court permission.

Digital Evidence under Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023

Digital evidence admissibility and chain of custody

The BSA 2023 replaced the Indian Evidence Act from 1 July 2024 (same date as BNS/BNSS). Key changes relevant to cybercrime evidence:

Key BSA Provisions:

Section 61 BSA (equivalent of S.65B IEA): Electronic record admissibility certificate. The requirement for a certificate from a responsible official is maintained and clarified. Without this certificate, electronic records (screenshots, digital documents, system logs) are not admissible as evidence.
Section 57 BSA: "Document" now explicitly includes electronic records — broader and more modern than the IEA definition.
Section 63 BSA: Secondary evidence — printed copies of electronic records admissible as secondary evidence where the original electronic record cannot be produced.

Chain of Custody Requirements:

StepRequirement
SeizureSeizure memo with exact device details (IMEI, serial numbers); timestamp
Hash RecordingMD5 and SHA-256 hash values recorded immediately at seizure
Chain of Custody LogEvery person who handles evidence documented with date/time/purpose
Forensic AnalysisCFSL or state FSL analysis report with hash verification
S.61 BSA CertificateCertificate from responsible official before evidence produced in court
Most common failure point: The most common cause of cybercrime case failure in Indian courts is inadequate digital evidence — either no Section 61 BSA (S.65B IEA) certificate, broken chain of custody, or failure to record hash values at seizure. This problem is not new — it has plagued cybercrime prosecution for two decades. Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (2020 SC) confirmed the certificate is mandatory, not directory. Police and prosecution teams must treat digital evidence with the same forensic rigour as physical evidence.

Practical Guide: Investigators, Lawyers & Compliance Teams

Implementation guidance for different stakeholders

For Police / Investigators:

FIRs filed after 1 July 2024 must cite BNS sections, not IPC sections
Cite concurrent IT Act sections — both statutes apply and should appear in charge sheet
Section 80 IT Act (DSP rank for investigation) remains unchanged
Use BNSS for search, seizure, arrest, remand — not CrPC
Record hash values at evidence seizure; obtain Section 61 BSA certificate before trial

For Lawyers / Prosecutors:

Date of offence is the determinative factor — pre-July 2024 = IPC/CrPC; post-July 2024 = BNS/BNSS
Always consider Section 111 BNS for operations involving multiple actors, mule accounts, or syndicate structure
Victim compensation: Section 395 BNSS — compensation order available in criminal proceedings
Plea bargaining: available under BNSS Chapter XXIV for offences with max penalty ≤7 years

For Compliance Officers:

BNS does not change IT Act compliance obligations — IT Act regulatory framework (CERT-In, intermediary rules) is unchanged
Section 111 BNS organised crime: knowingly providing services to a cybercrime syndicate (e.g., KYC-free accounts, anonymous SIMs) may attract organised crime liability
Document vendor due diligence to demonstrate absence of knowing facilitation

For Victims:

File complaint at cybercrime.gov.in — system updated for BNS sections
FIR should reference both IT Act and BNS provisions
Financial fraud: call 1930 immediately — the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal's financial fraud unit can initiate freezing of mule accounts within hours of complaint
