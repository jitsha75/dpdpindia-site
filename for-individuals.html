For Individual Citizens

Know Your Data Rights

The DPDP Act 2023 gives every Indian citizen powerful rights over how companies collect, use, and store your personal data. You are a Data Principal — and these are your rights. In plain language, with no legalese.

DPDP Act 2023

Your Rights as a Data Principal

Under the DPDP Act 2023, every individual whose personal data is processed by a company in India is a "Data Principal" — and you have these legally enforceable rights.

📂
01
Right to Access
Section 11 · DPDP Act 2023
You have the right to know what personal data a company holds about you, how they are using it, and who they have shared it with. Any company processing your data must provide this information upon request.
How to Exercise This Right
Send a written request to the company's Grievance Officer (details must be on their website/app). They must respond within 30 days. Ask for: a summary of data held, purposes of processing, and list of processors/third parties.
✏️
02
Right to Correction
Section 12 · DPDP Act 2023
If a company holds inaccurate, incomplete, or outdated personal information about you, you have the right to have it corrected. This applies to all data they hold — your name, address, contact details, transaction records, and more.
How to Exercise This Right
Contact the Grievance Officer and specify exactly what data is incorrect and what the correct information is. Provide supporting documentation if available. The company must correct the data within 30 days.
🗑️
03
Right to Erasure
Section 12 · DPDP Act 2023
You can request a company to erase your personal data — either when the original purpose of collection is complete, when you withdraw your consent, or when the data is no longer necessary. Some data may be retained if required by law.
How to Exercise This Right
Send a deletion request to the Grievance Officer. Companies must erase data unless they have a legal obligation to retain it (e.g. for tax records). They must confirm deletion or give reasons for retention.
🚫
04
Right to Withdraw Consent
Section 6(4) · DPDP Act 2023
If a company is processing your data based on your consent, you have the right to withdraw that consent at any time — as easily as you gave it. Withdrawal does not make their past processing illegal, but they must stop after withdrawal.
How to Exercise This Right
Look for "Withdraw Consent" in the app/website settings, or contact the Grievance Officer. The withdrawal mechanism must be as simple as the consent mechanism. Companies must process withdrawals within 30 days.
📣
05
Right to Grieve
Section 13 · DPDP Act 2023
If a company fails to fulfil your rights requests, handles your data improperly, or violates the DPDP Act, you have the right to file a complaint — first with the company's Grievance Officer, then with the Data Protection Board of India (DPDPB).
How to Exercise This Right
Step 1: File grievance with company's Grievance Officer (must respond within 30 days). Step 2: If unsatisfied, escalate to the Data Protection Board of India at its official portal. DPDPB can award compensation and impose penalties on the company.
👤
06
Right to Nominate
Section 14 · DPDP Act 2023
You can nominate another person — a trusted family member or friend — to exercise your data rights on your behalf in the event of your death or incapacity. This nominee can file grievances, access your data, and request corrections or erasure.
How to Exercise This Right
The nomination process will be prescribed by the DPDPB rules. Once rules are in force, you will be able to designate a nominee through the company's platform or directly with the DPDPB portal.
Plain Language

Explained Simply

Real situations that you may have experienced — and what the DPDP Act says you can do about them.

An app I installed 2 years ago keeps sending me ads. I never agreed to this.

Under the DPDP Act, companies must have your explicit, specific consent before using your data for marketing. If you didn't consent to ads separately from the app's basic function, this processing is likely unlawful. You can: (1) withdraw consent through the app's privacy settings, (2) request they stop processing your data for marketing, (3) file a grievance if they don't comply within 30 days.

§ Section 6 DPDP Act: Consent must be free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous. Bundled consent is prohibited.
My old bank has wrong details about me — wrong address, old phone number. It's causing problems.

You have a statutory right to have this corrected. Contact the bank's Grievance Officer (their contact details must be on the website/app/statements). Send them the correct information with supporting documents. They must correct it within 30 days. If they don't, escalate to the Banking Ombudsman and the Data Protection Board.

§ Section 12 DPDP Act: Right to correction of inaccurate or outdated personal data — mandatory, time-bound obligation on Data Fiduciary.
I deleted my account on an app — but I'm worried they still have all my data somewhere.

Deleting your account triggers your right to erasure. The company must delete all personal data that they no longer need to provide the service. They may keep some data if legally required (e.g. for tax or fraud prevention) but must tell you what they're retaining and why. Write to the Grievance Officer to confirm deletion and request details of any retained data.

§ Section 12 + Section 8(7) DPDP Act: Erasure obligation on withdrawal of consent or completion of purpose. Retention only lawful with legal basis.
A company shared my data with another company and I found out by accident. Can I do something?

Yes — sharing your data with a third party requires either your consent (with disclosure of who the third party is) or a valid legal basis. You have the right to know who your data has been shared with (Right to Access). File a grievance with the Grievance Officer demanding a list of all third parties they've shared your data with. If the sharing was unauthorised, you can escalate to the DPDPB.

§ Section 11 + Section 5 DPDP Act: Right to access disclosure of recipients. Data sharing without consent is a violation of the Act.
Step by Step

How to Exercise Your Rights

Follow these steps to exercise any of your DPDP Act rights — access, correction, erasure, or withdrawal of consent.

01
Find the Grievance Officer
Every company that processes your data must publish their Grievance Officer's contact details on their website, app, and privacy policy. Look under "Privacy Policy → Contact" or "Settings → Privacy."
02
Send a Written Request
Write an email clearly stating: (a) which right you are exercising, (b) what data this concerns, and (c) what action you expect from them. Keep a copy of your email and note the date sent.
03
Wait for Response (30 Days)
Companies must respond within 30 days under the DPDP Act. If they don't respond or their response is unsatisfactory, you have grounds to escalate to the Data Protection Board.
04
Escalate to DPDPB
If the company doesn't comply, file a complaint at the Data Protection Board of India's portal (to be operationalised in 2026). The DPDPB can award compensation and fine companies up to ₹50 Crore.
Template — Data Rights Request Email
Subject: Data Subject Rights Request under DPDP Act 2023

Dear Grievance Officer,

I am writing to exercise my rights under the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023.

My details: [Your Name], [Your registered email/phone with the company]

I am requesting: [Select: Access to my data / Correction of my data / Erasure of my data / Withdrawal of consent]

Specific data concerned: [Describe the data — e.g. "All personal data held in my account registered under this email address"]

Expected action: Please [provide a summary of data held / correct the following information: ___ / erase all my data / confirm withdrawal of consent and cessation of processing] within 30 days as required by the DPDP Act 2023.

If you do not respond within 30 days, I will escalate this matter to the Data Protection Board of India.

Regards,
[Your Name]
How to File a Grievance

If a company is mishandling your data, not responding to your rights requests, or violating the DPDP Act — here's how to escalate.

Step 1 — Company Level
Grievance Officer
Every Data Fiduciary must appoint a Grievance Officer. File your complaint with them first. They must acknowledge within 24 hours and resolve within 30 days under DPDP Act rules.
⏱ 30 day resolution timeline
Step 2 — Regulatory Level
Data Protection Board (DPDPB)
If the Grievance Officer doesn't resolve your complaint or you're unsatisfied, file with the DPDPB at its official portal. The Board can investigate, order compensation, and impose penalties on the company.
⏱ DPDPB to be fully operational in 2026
Step 3 — Adjudication
Adjudicating Officer + High Court
DPDPB orders can be appealed to the Appellate Tribunal. For violations of other rights (e.g. under IT Act), you can also approach a consumer forum or High Court. Consider consulting an advocate for complex cases.
⏱ Judicial remedy — timeline varies
