Children's Data Under the DPDP Act — Section 9 Analysis

Section 9 of the DPDP Act imposes some of the Act's most stringent obligations — mandatory verifiable parental consent, absolute prohibition on behavioural tracking, and no targeted advertising directed at children. With India's uniform 18-year threshold, this is among the most protective children's data regimes globally.

01

Who is a "Child" Under the DPDP Act

Age threshold and scope of application

The DPDP Act defines a "child" under Section 2(f) as any individual below the age of 18 years. This is a uniform national threshold — unlike GDPR (EU), which allows Member States to lower consent age to 13. India's uniform 18-year threshold has major implications for tech platforms, social media, educational apps, and e-commerce services.

The Act does not distinguish between teenagers (13–17) and younger children. All individuals under 18 are treated identically, subject to potential exemptions under Section 9(4). This is a deliberate policy choice reflecting India's approach to child protection in the digital environment.

02

Parental Consent: Requirements & Verification

How Data Fiduciaries must obtain and manage parental consent

Section 9(1) requires that before processing any personal data of a child, the Data Fiduciary must obtain verifiable consent of the parent or lawful guardian. This is in addition to, not a substitute for, the standard consent requirements under Section 6.

Verifiable consent requirements: The Rules (pending notification) are expected to specify mechanisms. Likely methods include: Aadhaar-based parental authentication, government ID verification, supervised digital consent flow. No method is foolproof.

Process only consented data: Process only the data consented to, for the consented purpose
Right to review: Permit the parent/guardian to review the child's data on request
Withdrawal right: Allow the parent/guardian to withdraw consent at any time
On withdrawal: Stop processing and delete data (subject to lawful retention obligations)
Record-keeping: Maintain records of how consent was obtained and verified
Implementation challenge: The requirement for "verifiable" parental consent is one of the most technically challenging aspects of the Act. Aadhaar-based verification raises privacy concerns of its own; other mechanisms may be circumvented by determined children. Until Rules are notified, Data Fiduciaries should implement reasonable age-gating, document their due diligence, and build consent infrastructure that can be upgraded when Rules are finalised.
03

Absolute Prohibited Activities Under Section 9(3)

Three categories of processing that cannot be permitted for children, under any circumstances

Section 9(3) imposes absolute prohibitions on three categories of processing — these cannot be unlocked by parental consent or any other mechanism:

Prohibition 1
Behavioural Tracking
Data Fiduciaries must NOT undertake tracking or behavioural monitoring of children. This covers: cookie-based tracking, device fingerprinting, location tracking, activity monitoring, cross-site tracking, and all other forms of behavioural surveillance of users under 18.
Prohibition 2
Targeted Advertising
Data Fiduciaries must NOT target advertising at children. This includes: interest-based advertising, retargeting, lookalike audience advertising, demographic profiling for ad delivery, and any personalised advertising based on observed or inferred characteristics of a child.
Prohibition 3
Processing Detrimental to Well-being
Data Fiduciaries must NOT undertake any processing that may be detrimental to the well-being of the child. This is a principles-based catch-all — it covers processing that could cause psychological harm, addictive engagement patterns, undermining of self-esteem, or interference with healthy development.
Absolute prohibitions: These three prohibitions apply ABSOLUTELY — even if the parent consented, even if the child "wants" the service, and even if the processing is core to the platform's existing business model. Platform operators must redesign Indian-market products if their model depends on behavioural targeting or tracking of any users who may be under 18.

04

Age Verification: The Implementation Challenge

How Data Fiduciaries determine whether a user is under 18

The central practical challenge of Section 9 is: how does a Data Fiduciary know whether a user is under 18? The Act requires compliance but does not specify the verification mechanism — this is left to the DPDP Rules (draft released 2025, pending finalisation).

Three approaches under policy discussion:

1. Self-declaration with liability shift: User self-declares age at registration. Platforms add contractual language shifting liability to the user if they provide false information. Risk: easily circumvented; may not satisfy "verifiable" standard.

2. Aadhaar-based age verification: Leverages UIDAI's DigiYatra/age-band token architecture to confirm age range (e.g., "above 18") without revealing full identity. Technically sound but raises privacy-of-minors concerns and requires Aadhaar coverage.

3. Consent Manager platforms (S.2(g)): Third-party registered Consent Managers act as intermediaries — they verify parental identity and manage consent flows on behalf of Data Fiduciaries. This is the most architecturally clean solution once the Consent Manager framework is operationalised.

Until Rules are notified: Data Fiduciaries should: (a) implement reasonable age-declaration at signup; (b) apply Section 9 protections to all users where age cannot be verified; (c) build technical architecture that can integrate with Aadhaar-based verification or Consent Manager APIs once available; (d) document all due diligence measures taken.
05

Exemptions & Reduced Threshold (S.9(4))

Government discretion to exempt certain Data Fiduciaries

Section 9(4) empowers the Central Government to notify, by Rules, certain Data Fiduciaries or classes of Data Fiduciaries for whom Section 9(1) (parental consent) and Section 9(3) (prohibitions) will not apply, or will apply with modifications.

Potential categories being considered for exemption: Educational technology platforms, healthcare platforms for minors, government welfare schemes serving children, parental monitoring/safety apps.

Exemption controversy: Section 9(4) has been criticised by child rights advocates and digital rights organisations as creating a potential "whitelist" of platforms exempt from the core child protection obligations. The scope of exemptions will be a key test of the Government's commitment. Parliament will not see these exemptions before they are notified — they are subordinate legislation under the Rules.
