Cyber Forensics
& Digital Investigation

The six phases of cyber forensic investigation, chain of custody requirements, §79A Examiner of Electronic Evidence, and the intersection of forensic methodology with BSA 2023 admissibility standards.

§79A IT Act ISO 27037 NIST SP 800-86 Chain of Custody Digital Forensics BSA 2023
Framework

The Dual Master Problem in Cyber Forensics

Indian cyber forensics must simultaneously satisfy two distinct sets of requirements: the scientific and technical standards set by NIST SP 800-86 and ISO/IEC 27037:2012, and the legal admissibility standards imposed by §63 BSA 2023 (formerly §65B IEA). A forensically sound image is useless in court without a valid §63 certificate; a certificate is meaningless if the acquisition methodology was contaminated.

Failures at any phase travel forward — a broken chain of custody at acquisition will undermine the §63 certificate even if the analysis is impeccable. This is why Indian courts increasingly require the §79A certified Examiner of Electronic Evidence to testify as an expert witness, not merely file an affidavit.

Investigation Procedure

The Six Phases of Cyber Forensic Investigation

Phase 01

Identification

Identify all potential digital evidence sources before any device is touched.

  • Hard drives, SSDs, USB devices
  • Mobile phones and tablets
  • Cloud accounts, email servers
  • IoT devices, CCTV DVRs
  • Network logs, router configs
  • RAM (for live systems)

Document all items photographically and by seizure memo before any action.

Phase 02

Preservation

Prevent any alteration of evidence from the moment of seizure.

  • Live vs dead-box decision: RAM evidence (encryption keys, running processes) may require live acquisition before power-off
  • Seal mobiles in Faraday bag — prevents remote wipe
  • Issue preservation notices to intermediaries under §67C IT Act + Rule 3(1)(g) IT Rules 2011
  • Photograph device state, battery level, network connections
Phase 03

Acquisition

Create a forensic copy — never work on the original.

  • Bit-by-bit clone using hardware write-blocker
  • Tools: FTK Imager, EnCase, dd (Linux), CellebrITE (mobile)
  • Hash both source and clone — SHA-256 minimum; MD5 for legacy
  • Document: device model, serial number, examiner name, date/time, hash values
Phase 04

Analysis

Extract relevant evidence from the forensic image.

  • File system: FAT32/NTFS/APFS/ext4 — deleted file recovery, slack space carving
  • Metadata: EXIF, MAC times (Modified/Accessed/Created), document properties
  • Browser artifacts: cache, cookies, download history
  • Email headers: SPF/DKIM/DMARC validation, IP tracing
  • Mobile: WhatsApp .crypt12 backups, location data, app databases
  • Memory: Volatility framework — running processes, encryption keys
Phase 05

Reporting

Produce a court-ready forensic report.

  • Executive summary for non-technical readers
  • Methodology section (tools, version numbers, settings)
  • Findings with screenshot evidence, file paths, hash values
  • Chain of custody log as appendix
  • Prepare §63 BSA certificate simultaneously
Phase 06

Court Testimony

Present findings as expert witness under §45 BSA 2023.

  • §79A certified Examiner carries highest credibility
  • Must be prepared to explain SHA-256 hashing to lay judges
  • Cross-examination focus: write-blocker used? Hash values match? Chain of custody intact?
  • Anti-forensics detection must be documented if present
Statutory Authority

Section 79A IT Act — Examiner of Electronic Evidence

§ 79A IT Act 2000 (inserted by 2008 Amendment)

The Central Government may, for the purposes of providing expert opinion on electronic form evidence before any court or other authority, by notification in the Official Gazette, appoint any Department, body or agency of the Central Government or a State Government as an Examiner of Electronic Evidence.

Examiners notified under §79A include designated labs at CERT-In, CDAC, State Forensic Science Laboratories with digital forensics units, and CBI's Cyber Forensics Lab (CFSL). Their expert reports carry the weight of government-appointed expert opinion under §45 BSA 2023.

Significance for Practitioners

A report from a §79A notified examiner carries significantly more weight than a private forensic examiner's opinion in Indian courts. The key practical implication: where the investigating agency has access to a §79A lab, the forensic analysis should be routed there rather than to a private vendor, unless time constraints make it impossible.

Important Limitation

§79A only authorises the government to designate examiners — it does not make their reports automatically conclusive. Defence counsel can still challenge methodology, chain of custody, and specific findings. However, cross-examining a §79A examiner requires demonstrating a specific methodological error, not merely alleging incompetence.

Evidence Integrity

Chain of Custody — Requirements & Documentation

Chain of custody (CoC) is the unbroken, documented record of who had access to evidence, when, and what was done with it. A broken chain is one of the most common grounds for exclusion of digital evidence in Indian courts.

CoC ElementWhat Must Be DocumentedFailure Impact
Seizure memoDate, time, place, officer details, device description, witness signaturesDoubt over provenance of device

Applicable Standards

  • ISO/IEC 27037:2012 — Guidelines for identification, collection, acquisition and preservation of digital evidence. The international benchmark referenced by CERT-In and §79A labs.
  • NIST SP 800-86 — Guide to integrating forensic techniques into incident response. US standard but widely referenced by Indian practitioners.
  • ACPO Good Practice Guide (UK) — Cited in several Indian High Court judgments on digital evidence methodology.
  • MHA CyPAD Guidelines — Ministry of Home Affairs guidelines for state police cyber forensic units.
Adversarial Practice

Common Defence Challenges in Cyber Forensics Cases

  • No write-blocker used — most devastating challenge; if proved, the image may have been contaminated at acquisition
  • Hash mismatch — hash at analysis differs from hash at seizure; implies alteration
  • Time synchronisation errors — device clock was wrong; timestamps unreliable
  • Anti-forensic tools present — CCleaner, secure-erase programs on the device suggest evidence was wiped; but recovery of wiped files strengthens prosecution
  • Shared device — multiple users had access; cannot attribute specific file/activity to accused
  • Chain of custody gap — device transferred without documentation; access cannot be ruled out
  • §79A examiner not from notified institution — weight of report reduced to private expert opinion
  • §63 certificate defects — certificate does not cover the specific record; signed by wrong person; filed after charge framed without court permission
