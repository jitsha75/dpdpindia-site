01

Overview — Why Rules Matter as Much as the Act

The DPDP Act delegates most operational detail to the Central Government through rules

The DPDP Act 2023 is deliberately short — 44 sections covering 30 pages. The detailed operational requirements that compliance professionals and technology teams actually need to implement are contained in the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025, notified by the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY).

This delegation of power to the executive is both a strength (flexibility to adapt) and a vulnerability (rules can be changed without Parliamentary scrutiny). The three most significant areas addressed by the Rules are: the Consent Manager framework, the data breach notification protocol, and the compliance transition timeline.

Rules evolve: The DPDP Rules are secondary legislation and can be amended by the Central Government more readily than the parent Act. Compliance teams must monitor MeitY notifications regularly — this article reflects the framework as published in 2025.
02

Consent Managers — A New Digital Infrastructure

Third-party platforms authorised to manage consent tokens across services

What they are
Consent Managers Defined
A Consent Manager is a third-party platform registered with the DPB that acts as a single interface for data principals to give, manage, review, and withdraw consent across multiple Data Fiduciaries. Think of them as a dashboard for your digital consents.
How they work
Consent Token Architecture
When a data principal consents through a Consent Manager, a structured consent token is generated and shared with the Data Fiduciary. The Data Fiduciary must honour withdrawal of consent signalled through the same Consent Manager.
Registration
DPB Registration Required
Consent Managers must register with the Data Protection Board and meet prescribed technical and financial standards. The rules specify capital requirements, technical specifications for consent tokens, and interoperability standards.
Purpose
Solving Consent Fatigue
The Consent Manager model is designed to reduce consent fatigue — the phenomenon where users click 'Accept' on every consent request without reading them. By centralising consent management, data principals gain meaningful control.
Comparison with GDPR: GDPR does not have a direct equivalent to India's Consent Manager model. The closest analogue is Article 26 joint controllers and the concept of consent management platforms — but India's model is more structured, with DPB oversight and registration requirements.
03

Breach Notification Protocol

Standardised timelines and procedures for reporting data security incidents

The DPDP Rules 2025 prescribe a mandatory breach notification framework. A "Personal Data Breach" means any unauthorised processing, disclosure, acquisition, sharing, copying, modification, disposal, or loss of personal data that compromises the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of data.

Breach Notification Flow
1
Detection — Incident identified
The Data Fiduciary or Data Processor detects or becomes aware of a personal data breach affecting any number of data principals.
2
Notification to DPB — Within prescribed timeline
The Data Fiduciary must notify the Data Protection Board using the prescribed format within the timeline specified in the rules. Notification must describe the nature of the breach, data affected, likely consequences, and remedial measures taken.
3
Notification to affected data principals
If the breach is likely to cause harm to data principals, each affected individual must be informed — in plain language, in a manner accessible to them.
4
Remediation and follow-up report
The Data Fiduciary must submit a follow-up report to the DPB describing the full extent of the breach, its root cause, and measures implemented to prevent recurrence.
No threshold for notification: Unlike some international frameworks, the DPDP Act does not set a minimum threshold (number of individuals affected, sensitivity of data) below which breach notification is not required. Any breach of personal data must be assessed for notification obligations.
04

Phased Implementation & Legacy Data

18-month roadmap for aligning existing data infrastructure

The DPDP Rules 2025 recognise that organisations have significant legacy data holdings that were collected before the Act came into force. The rules provide for an 18-month phased compliance roadmap — though the specific timelines vary by obligation type and entity size.

Phase 1
Notice & Consent for New Data
From the compliance date, all new data collection must be accompanied by compliant notices and valid consent. Existing data processing relationships must be reviewed and, where necessary, fresh consent obtained.
Phase 2
Rights Infrastructure
Grievance Officer appointment, rights request handling systems, and consent withdrawal mechanisms must be operational. SDFs must also have DPO appointment and audit processes in place.
Phase 3
Legacy Data Alignment
Organisations must align their existing data stores with the Act's requirements — implementing encryption standards, data minimisation, and retention policies for data held before the compliance date.
Phase 4
Cross-Border & Processor Contracts
Data processing agreements with Data Processors and cross-border transfer arrangements must be reviewed and updated to include the mandatory clauses required by the rules.
