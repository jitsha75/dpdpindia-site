Data Protection Board of India
Powers, Composition & Enforcement

A definitive reference on India's data protection regulator — establishment under Chapter V of the DPDP Act 2023, appointment procedure under DPDP Rules 2025, inquiry powers, the complete penalty schedule (up to ₹250 crore per violation), the appeal mechanism to TDSAT, and the phased enforcement timeline through May 2027.

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act) established the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) as India's dedicated data protection adjudicatory body. Notified on 11 August 2023, the Act was operationalised through the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025 (DPDP Rules) vide Gazette Notification G.S.R. 846(E) dated 13 November 2025. The DPBI is not a conventional regulatory commission — it is constituted as a digital office with proceedings conducted electronically, capable of imposing fines up to ₹250 crore per breach. This article provides a comprehensive reference covering the Board's establishment, composition, powers, inquiry procedure, penalty schedule, and the phased implementation timeline that determines when entities actually become liable.

1. Establishment and Legal Character

The Data Protection Board of India is established by the Central Government under Section 18 of the DPDP Act 2023. Unlike conventional regulatory bodies, the DPBI has a distinctive architecture: it is constituted as a body corporate with perpetual succession and a common seal, capable of suing and being sued in its own name (s. 18(2)). Crucially, it is designed to function as a digital-first adjudicatory body — Section 20 mandates that all proceedings be conducted digitally, with video-conferencing as the default mode and physical hearings permitted only in exceptional circumstances specified by rules.

Legal Character — Key Distinctions

The DPBI is not an independent statutory regulator in the mould of SEBI or TRAI. It is constituted by, and members are appointed and removed by, the Central Government. Critics have raised concerns about structural independence from executive influence. The Act classifies it as a "quasi-judicial body" but its chairperson's salary (₹4,50,000/month) and tenure (2 years, renewable) are determined by government notification rather than by statute — a point noted in the SFLC India analysis and academic commentary on DPDP Act design.

1.1 Chapter V Overview — Sections 18–33

The DPBI is governed by Chapter V (ss. 18–27) and Chapter VI (ss. 28–33) of the DPDP Act. Chapter V covers establishment, composition, appointment, terms of service, resignation, removal, and meeting procedures. Chapter VI covers the powers and proceedings of the Board — complaint filing, inquiry initiation, inquiry procedure, interim orders, penalty powers, and voluntary undertakings.

2. Composition and Appointment

Section 19 provides that the Board shall consist of a Chairperson and such number of other Members as the Central Government may determine by notification. The Rules 2025 elaborate the qualification requirements and the appointment procedure through a Search-cum-Selection Committee (SCSC).

2.1 Qualifications

Under Rule 15 of the DPDP Rules 2025, the Chairperson and Members must be persons of ability, integrity, and standing who possess special knowledge or practical experience in one or more of the following fields: data governance; administration or implementation of laws related to social or consumer protection; dispute resolution; information and communications technology; digital economy; law; regulation or techno-regulation; or any other field that the Central Government considers useful to the Board. The Rules additionally require that at least one Member shall be an expert in the field of law.

2.2 Appointment — Chairperson (Rule 16)

Constitute the Chairperson SCSC
The Central Government constitutes a Search-cum-Selection Committee chaired by the Cabinet Secretary. Members include: Secretary, Department of Legal Affairs; Secretary, MeitY; and two external experts of repute nominated by the Central Government.
SCSC identifies and evaluates candidates
The Committee screens candidates for eligibility against the qualification criteria in Rule 15. The deliberation process and criteria for shortlisting are not publicly specified in the Rules — a transparency gap noted by civil society.
SCSC makes recommendations
The Committee recommends a panel of suitable individuals to the Central Government for consideration as Chairperson.
Central Government appoints
After considering the SCSC's recommendations, the Central Government appoints the Chairperson by order. The appointment is not subject to Parliamentary approval.

2.3 Appointment — Other Members (Rule 17)

A separate SCSC is constituted for Members, chaired by the Secretary, MeitY (rather than the Cabinet Secretary), with the Secretary, Department of Legal Affairs and two external experts as members. The procedure mirrors the Chairperson appointment process but with a lower-ranking committee chair — reflecting the relative seniority gradient between the two roles.

2.4 Terms of Service and Remuneration

Under Rule 18 read with Schedule V to the Rules, the Chairperson and Members serve for a term of 2 years, eligible for re-appointment. Monthly consolidated salaries are fixed at ₹4,50,000 for the Chairperson and ₹4,00,000 for Members. The Rules specify conditions for resignation (written notice to the Central Government) and removal (procedure to be prescribed by government, with safeguards for natural justice).

Independence Concern — Short Tenure

A 2-year term (with the same appointing authority controlling re-appointment) has drawn criticism from data governance scholars. Comparative frameworks: the EU's GDPR supervisory authorities require minimum 4-year terms to ensure independence (Art. 53, GDPR). India's own SEBI Chairman serves 5 years or until age 65, whichever is earlier. The DPBI's short renewable term raises structural concerns about adjudicatory independence from government interests — particularly where the Central Government is itself a significant data fiduciary.

3. Powers and Functions

The DPBI's powers are set out in Chapter VI (ss. 28–33) of the DPDP Act and are elaborated in Rules 19–21 of the DPDP Rules 2025. The Board functions primarily as an adjudicatory body — it is not a sector regulator with rule-making power. Regulatory functions (designating Significant Data Fiduciaries, specifying cross-border transfer destinations, issuing guidance) remain with the Central Government and MeitY.

3.1 Complaints and Suo Motu Cognisance

Section 28 provides two pathways for the Board to initiate proceedings. First, on receipt of a complaint from a Data Principal whose rights have been violated under the Act. Second, suo motu — the Board may take cognisance of a breach on its own motion if it has sufficient reason to believe that a breach has occurred, without waiting for a complaint. This suo motu power is significant: it enables the Board to act on media reports, civil society inputs, or regulatory intelligence without requiring an individual complainant to come forward.

Complaint Pre-Condition — Grievance Redressal First

Under Rule 19 of the DPDP Rules 2025, a Data Principal must first approach the Data Fiduciary's Grievance Redressal Officer and exhaust the internal grievance mechanism before filing a complaint with the DPBI. Only if the grievance remains unresolved within the timeframe specified by the Rules may the Data Principal escalate to the Board. This two-stage model is analogous to the RERA complaint mechanism and is designed to reduce Board docket pressure — but it also creates a potential barrier for data principals in asymmetric power relationships.

3.2 Inquiry Powers

Once a complaint is admitted or suo motu cognisance is taken, the Board may conduct a detailed inquiry under Section 28(2). The Board has wide powers during inquiry: it may summon any person whose evidence it considers necessary, examine witnesses on oath, compel production of documents, require a Data Fiduciary to produce records of data processing, and examine any other evidence material to the breach.

All proceedings are conducted digitally under Section 20 — parties, witnesses, and the Board interact through the DPBI's online portal. Physical attendance may be permitted only where the Board determines it is necessary in the interests of justice. This digital-first design is both efficient and, potentially, an access barrier for Data Principals without digital literacy or connectivity.

3.3 Interim Relief

The Act empowers the Board to issue interim directions during pending proceedings to prevent ongoing or imminent harm to Data Principals. This is the equivalent of an injunctive power in civil litigation — the Board may, for example, direct a Data Fiduciary to immediately suspend processing of particular categories of personal data pending the outcome of an inquiry.

3.4 Voluntary Undertaking (Section 29)

A novel feature of the DPDP Act is the voluntary undertaking mechanism. A Data Fiduciary may, at any stage of proceedings, offer the Board a voluntary undertaking to comply with specific obligations or remediate a breach. The Board may accept or reject such undertakings. If accepted, the Board issues a formal order incorporating the undertaking — and breach of that order is treated as breach of the original obligation and attracts the same maximum penalty as the underlying violation.

3.5 Directions to Government and Other Bodies

Under Section 28(3), the Board may direct Data Fiduciaries, including Government entities, to take specific corrective action to ensure compliance. The Board's directions are binding and enforceable. Notably, the DPDP Act does not create an express carve-out exempting government Data Fiduciaries from DPBI jurisdiction — though Section 17 exemptions (national security, public order, state interests) may effectively insulate significant government processing from the Board's reach.

4. Inquiry Procedure

Rule 20 of the DPDP Rules 2025 specifies the procedure for Board proceedings. The procedure is structured around principles of natural justice — the right to be heard (audi alteram partem) and the right to a reasoned order. The Board is not bound by the Code of Civil Procedure 1908 or the Indian Evidence Act 2023 but is guided by the principles of natural justice and such other rules as it may make.

Complaint Filing / Suo Motu Initiation
Data Principal files complaint through DPBI's online portal after exhausting Data Fiduciary's grievance mechanism. Alternatively, Board initiates proceedings on its own cognisance. Complaint must include the nature of the alleged breach, relief sought, and evidence in support.
Admission and Notice
The Board admits the complaint (or initiates suo motu proceedings) and serves a notice on the Data Fiduciary specifying the alleged breach and inviting a response within a prescribed timeframe. Notice is served digitally through the portal.
Response and Evidence
The Data Fiduciary files its response with supporting evidence. The Board may call for additional documents, evidence, or technical expertise. Summoning powers extend to any person whose evidence is considered necessary — including employees, data processors, or technology vendors.
Hearing (Digital)
Parties are heard via video-conferencing. The Board may hear technical experts, auditors, or independent assessors. All proceedings are digitally recorded and form part of the official record. Physical hearings permitted only in exceptional, specified circumstances.
Order / Penalty / Directions
Board passes a reasoned written order: dismissing the complaint; directing corrective action; accepting a voluntary undertaking; or imposing a financial penalty from the Schedule. A single order may include both corrective directions and financial penalties.
Appeal to TDSAT
Any person aggrieved by the Board's order may appeal to the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT) within 60 days, depositing 50% of the penalty amount or providing equivalent security. TDSAT may stay the remainder pending appeal.

5. The Penalty Schedule — Section 33 & Schedule

The DPDP Act adopts a fixed-ceiling schedule model (similar to the UK GDPR) rather than a percentage-of-turnover model (EU GDPR). Penalties are set as maximum amounts per violation category — the Board determines the actual penalty within the ceiling based on a proportionality assessment under Section 33(2).

Violation Section Maximum Penalty Enforcement Phase
Failure to implement reasonable security safeguards to prevent personal data breach s. 8(5) ₹250 crore May 2027
Failure to notify the Board and affected Data Principals of a personal data breach s. 8(6) ₹200 crore May 2027
Non-compliance with special provisions for children's data (verifiable parental consent, age-appropriate processing) s. 9 ₹200 crore May 2027
Failure of Significant Data Fiduciary to fulfil additional obligations (DPIA, audit, DPO appointment, data localisation) s. 10 ₹150 crore May 2027
Breach of any other obligation of a Data Fiduciary or Data Processor not covered by specific items above s. 12–16 ₹50 crore May 2027
Failure of Data Principal to observe duties under Section 15 (providing false information, impersonation, frivolous complaints) s. 15 ₹10,000 May 2027
Breach of voluntary undertaking accepted by the Board under Section 29 s. 29(3) Same as original violation May 2027
Penalty Determination Factors — Section 33(2)

When determining the actual penalty within the statutory ceiling, the Board must consider: (a) the nature, gravity, and duration of the breach; (b) the type and volume of personal data affected; (c) whether the breach was intentional or negligent; (d) the mitigating actions taken by the Data Fiduciary after discovering the breach; (e) the financial capacity of the entity; (f) whether the entity had previously received directions from the Board on the same matter; and (g) the repetitive nature of the breach. The Act does not impose mandatory minimums — the Board has wide discretion to impose token fines as well as ceiling-level penalties, which critics argue could weaken deterrence.

6. Significant Data Fiduciaries — Enhanced Obligations

Section 10 empowers the Central Government (not the DPBI) to designate entities as Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDF) based on the volume and sensitivity of data processed, risk to Data Principals, potential impact on sovereignty and public order, risk to electoral democracy, and national security implications. Rule 13 of the DPDP Rules 2025 specifies the additional obligations that SDFs must fulfil.

SDF Obligations Under Rule 13 (effective May 2027)

1. Annual DPIA: Data Protection Impact Assessment once every 12 months from designation notification. 2. Annual Audit: Independent audit of compliance with the Act and Rules, once every 12 months. 3. DPO Appointment: A Data Protection Officer based in India, accountable to the Board of Directors or equivalent governance body, serving as the primary contact point for the Board. 4. Independent Data Auditor: A separate, independent auditor (distinct from the internal DPO) to conduct the annual audit — the Rules specify qualification criteria for auditors. 5. Data Localisation: Personal data and traffic data pertaining to its flow must not be transferred outside India for categories specified by the Central Government. 6. Algorithmic Accountability: The SDF must ensure that algorithms and AI systems used to process personal data do not create risks identified in a DPIA — and must implement mitigation measures.

7. Phased Enforcement Timeline

The DPDP Rules 2025 introduced a three-phase enforcement timeline, critical for compliance planning. Entities that assume enforcement is imminent are partly correct — but the full weight of DPBI adjudicatory power over substantive obligations only engages in May 2027.

11 August 2023
DPDP Act Gazette Notification
Act published in the Official Gazette. Commencement date left to Central Government notification. No immediate enforcement obligations arise.
13 November 2025
DPDP Rules Notified — Phase 1 Commences
G.S.R. 846(E) notifies the DPDP Rules 2025. Phase 1 provisions effective from 14 November 2025: definitions, establishment of DPBI, administrative provisions, conflicts with other laws, and bar of civil court jurisdiction. The Board can be constituted; Chairperson and Members can be appointed. No substantive data fiduciary obligations yet active.
14 November 2026
Phase 2 — Consent Manager Obligations
Provisions relating to Consent Managers become effective. Entities wishing to operate as Consent Managers must register with the DPBI and comply with Rules on consent management platform architecture, data flows, and data principal interfaces. Consent Manager obligations are the first substantive enforcement milestone.
14 May 2027
Phase 3 — Full Substantive Enforcement
Rules 3, 5–16, 22, and 23 come into force — covering: consent notice requirements (r. 3); security safeguards (r. 6); data breach notification (r. 7); data erasure (r. 8); contact information obligations (r. 9); children's data processing rules (r. 10); Data Principal rights management (r. 11–12); SDF obligations (r. 13); cross-border transfer conditions (r. 14). All DPBI penalty powers over data fiduciary obligations become fully active.
Compliance Planning Implication

Entities have approximately 12 months from today (April 2026) before full enforcement in May 2027. However, building compliant data governance infrastructure — consent management platforms, breach notification protocols, DPIA frameworks, DPO appointments, vendor contract amendments, privacy notice redesign — typically requires 18–24 months of organised effort for large organisations. Entities that have not yet started should begin immediately. Smaller entities may benefit from the phased approach and lighter-touch obligations for non-SDFs, but the baseline obligations (security safeguards, breach notification, consent) apply to all Data Fiduciaries regardless of size.

8. Appeal to TDSAT

Section 30 of the DPDP Act provides that any person aggrieved by an order of the Data Protection Board may appeal to the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT). TDSAT was established under the TRAI Act 1997 and has been given jurisdiction over an expanding range of digital disputes — its remit now covers telecom, broadcasting, and data protection appeals.

8.1 Appeal Conditions

An appeal must be filed within 60 days of the DPBI order (TDSAT may condone delay on sufficient cause). As a mandatory pre-condition to filing, the appellant must either deposit 50% of the penalty amount with TDSAT or provide equivalent security approved by TDSAT. This deposit-first requirement is a significant practical barrier — a ₹250 crore penalty would require a ₹125 crore deposit to appeal, potentially making large penalties difficult to challenge in practice without interim relief.

8.2 Further Appeal

Orders of TDSAT may be further challenged before the High Court on questions of law, and ultimately before the Supreme Court of India under Article 136 (Special Leave Petition). Given the novelty of the DPDP Act, early enforcement actions are likely to generate significant judicial precedent at the High Court and Supreme Court level — practitioners should monitor this developing jurisprudence closely.

9. Interaction with Other Laws

Section 44 of the DPDP Act amends the Right to Information Act 2005 by adding a restriction: personal information in government records is now exempt from RTI disclosure unless the disclosure is overridden by a clear public interest that outweighs privacy. This significantly contracts the RTI scope and has attracted criticism from transparency advocates who argue that the DPDP Act's privacy protections should not be weaponised to shield government data from legitimate public scrutiny.

Section 43 provides that the DPDP Act's provisions shall be in addition to, and not in derogation of, any other law for the time being in force. This means existing sector-specific data protection obligations (RBI cybersecurity framework, SEBI data regulations, IRDAI data guidelines, CERT-In directions 2022) continue to apply alongside DPDP Act requirements — and may in some cases impose stricter obligations. Data Fiduciaries in regulated sectors must comply with both frameworks.

CERT-In Directions 2022 — Continuing Parallel Obligation

The CERT-In Directions of April 2022 (requiring 6-hour breach notification to CERT-In, 5-year VPN log retention, NTP synchronisation) remain fully operative alongside the DPDP Rules' breach notification obligations. The DPDP Rules require notification to the DPBI and the affected Data Principals; the CERT-In Directions require notification to CERT-In. In practice, a breach event will trigger dual notification obligations — to CERT-In within 6 hours and to DPBI and Data Principals within the timeframe specified by the DPDP Rules (which is expected to be substantially longer). Compliance teams must manage both notification workflows simultaneously.

10. Government Exemptions — Section 17

Section 17 of the DPDP Act carves out significant categories of government data processing from the Act's obligations. The Central Government may, by notification, exempt any instrumentality of the State from all or any provisions of the Act in the interests of: sovereignty and integrity of India; security of the State; friendly relations with foreign States; public order; prevention, detection, investigation, or prosecution of offences; or maintenance of public order.

This blanket exemption power — exercisable unilaterally by the Central Government without judicial or Parliamentary oversight — is the most-criticised provision of the Act. It effectively creates a zone where the government is both the rule-maker (specifying which of its own agencies are exempt) and the data processor, with no external accountability mechanism. The Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Aadhaar) (2018) 1 SCC 809 proportionality framework would arguably require that exemptions be narrowly drawn and subject to judicial review — but s. 17 as drafted gives courts limited purchase to supervise the scope of government exemptions.
