E-Governance in India:
IT Act as the Growth Catalyst

How Sections 6, 7, and the CCA digital signature framework gave legal force to paperless government — and powered the Digital India Stack that now processes 150 billion transactions annually.

The Legal Foundation: Sections 6 & 7

Before the IT Act, 2000, every interaction between a citizen and a government office required physical paper. Applications, licences, certificates, land records, court filings — all existed only in paper form, maintained in physical file rooms, and processed through bureaucratic chains that required physical presence at every step.

The IT Act's Chapter III fundamentally disrupted this centuries-old model by establishing the legal basis for electronic government — giving government departments the authority to accept, issue, and retain documents electronically with the same legal validity as paper.

Section 6 · IT Act 2000

Use of Electronic Records and Digital Signatures in Government and its Agencies

Where any law provides for the filing of any form, application, or any other document with any office, authority, body or agency owned or controlled by the appropriate Government in a particular manner — such filing may be done in electronic form and in such manner as may be prescribed by the appropriate Government.

This section also authorises: the issue of any licence, permit, sanction or approval in electronic form; the receipt or payment of money in electronic form; and the creation, retention or preservation of any record, register, deed, map, plan, schedule, entry or endorsement in electronic form.

Section 7 · IT Act 2000

Retention of Electronic Records

Where any law provides that documents, records or information shall be retained for any specific period, then, that requirement shall be deemed to have been satisfied if such documents, records or information are retained in the electronic form — provided that:

(a) the information contained therein remains accessible for subsequent reference; (b) the electronic record is retained in the format in which it was originally generated, sent or received or in a format which can be demonstrated to represent accurately the information originally generated, sent or received; (c) the details identifying the origin, destination, date and time of dispatch or receipt of such electronic record are available.

The Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA)

The IT Act, 2000 established the Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA) under Section 17, operating under the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY). The CCA's functions include:

  • Licensing and supervising Certifying Authorities (CAs) that issue digital certificates;
  • Maintaining a publicly accessible repository of all digital certificates and revocation lists;
  • Certifying public keys of Certifying Authorities;
  • Investigating alleged contraventions of the Act in relation to digital signatures.

Digital certificates issued by CCA-licensed CAs are legally valid for e-tendering, income tax filings, company filings with MCA, and a vast range of government-to-citizen (G2C) transactions. The CCA infrastructure forms the PKI (Public Key Infrastructure) backbone of India's formal digital economy.

From NeGP to Digital India: The Policy Arc

National e-Governance Plan (NeGP) — 2006

Building on the legal foundation created by the IT Act, the Government of India launched the National e-Governance Plan (NeGP) in 2006. The NeGP identified 31 Mission Mode Projects (MMPs) across Central, State, and Integrated categories — covering areas from income tax and passport services to land records, courts, and agriculture. The NeGP's vision was to "Make all Government services accessible to the common man in his locality, through common service delivery outlets."

Digital India Programme — 2015

The Digital India Programme, launched in July 2015, superseded and expanded upon NeGP, with a vision of transforming India into a digitally empowered society and knowledge economy. Organised around nine pillars, it encompassed BharatNet (rural broadband), DigiLocker (digital document storage), UMANG (universal government service delivery app), e-Courts, e-Hospital, and the broader Digital India Stack.

E-Courts: Section 65B and the Digital Evidence Revolution

One of the most consequential intersections of the IT Act and the Indian legal system is Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 — inserted by the IT Act, 2000 amendments. Section 65B specifies the conditions under which electronic records are admissible as evidence in court proceedings, requiring a certificate from the person responsible for the computer system attesting to its proper operation and the reliability of the electronic record.

The Supreme Court's ruling in Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (2020) 7 SCC 1 clarified that the Section 65B certificate is a mandatory pre-condition for admissibility of electronic records. This ruling has had profound implications for the admissibility of CCTV footage, electronic communications, and digital banking records in court proceedings — and has driven courts to upgrade their evidence management infrastructure.

The GIS Policy 2022 — A New Frontier

The National Geospatial Policy, 2022 (superseding the National Map Policy, 2005) significantly liberalised India's geospatial data regime, enabling private sector participation in mapping and GIS data. Read alongside the IT Act's electronic records framework and the DPDP Act 2023's data governance provisions, the 2022 Policy enables:

  • Indian entities to collect, generate, and use geospatial data freely (with national security caveats);
  • Integration of GIS layers with revenue land records (Bhoomi, BhuNaksha) for digital property registration;
  • Municipal GIS for urban planning and property tax under AMRUT and Smart Cities Mission.

The Bhoomi/GIS systems now interface directly with Revenue Departments, enabling citizens to obtain instant Pahani (Record of Rights) documents — a transformation from the earlier system that required physical visits to the Tahsildar's office.

Looking Ahead: The DPDP Act's Impact on E-Governance

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 introduces a new layer of obligations on government departments that operate digital services. While the DPDP Act's exemption provisions (Section 17) provide significant carve-outs for state entities in matters of national security and law enforcement, government bodies operating as "Data Fiduciaries" for citizen services (like DigiLocker, e-Courts, or tax portals) will increasingly need to comply with:

  • Purpose limitation principles when collecting citizen data;
  • Notice and consent requirements for data processing;
  • Data principal rights including access, correction, and erasure;
  • Security safeguard obligations for protecting sensitive citizen data.

The interplay between the IT Act's e-governance framework and the DPDP Act's data protection obligations will be one of the defining regulatory challenges for Indian digital government in 2026 and beyond.
