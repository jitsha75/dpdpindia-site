Legal Framework: Three-Statute Convergence

IT Act, POCSO, and the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023

Online crimes against children are governed by a convergence of three legal frameworks — the IT Act 2000, the Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act 2012 (POCSO), and the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023 (BNS). All three may apply simultaneously.

IT Act 2000
Technology-Specific Offences
Section 67B — CSAM. Section 66E — privacy violation (capturing/publishing private images). Sections 66C/D — identity theft and impersonation targeting children. Technology-specific provisions with up to 7 years imprisonment.
POCSO Act 2012
Child-Centric Sexual Offences
Gender-neutral child protection. Sections 14–15 — use of child for pornography and storage of CSAM. Sections 11–12 — sexual harassment including online. Child-friendly investigation and in-camera trial procedures. Mandatory reporting obligation on ALL persons.
BNS 2023
Post-IPC Framework (1 July 2024)
Section 78 — digital stalking. Section 77 — voyeurism. Section 96 — CSAM. Replaced IPC from 1 July 2024 — all offences post that date prosecuted under BNS.
Key principle — Lex Specialis: POCSO, as a special law protecting children from sexual offences, prevails over both the IT Act and BNS for sexual offences against minors. However, charges under all applicable statutes are filed simultaneously. A "child" under POCSO is any person below 18 years — gender-neutral — aligning with the DPDP Act definition.

CSAM: Section 67B IT Act & POCSO Sections 14–15

Dual criminalisation of child sexual abuse material

Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM) is simultaneously criminalised under both the IT Act and POCSO.

Section 67B IT Act — Five Sub-clauses:

Sub-sectionOffencePenalty
S.67B(a)Publishing/transmitting material depicting children in sexually explicit acts5 yrs + ₹10L (1st); 7 yrs + ₹10L (repeat)
S.67B(b)Creating, collecting, browsing, downloading, advertising, exchanging, or distributing CSAM5 yrs + ₹10L (1st); 7 yrs + ₹10L (repeat)
S.67B(c)Cultivating or enticing children into online relationship for sexual acts5 yrs + ₹10L (1st); 7 yrs + ₹10L (repeat)
S.67B(d)Facilitating online abuse of children5 yrs + ₹10L (1st); 7 yrs + ₹10L (repeat)
S.67B(e)Recording own abuse or abuse of others involving a child5 yrs + ₹10L (1st); 7 yrs + ₹10L (repeat)

POCSO Provisions:

Section 14 POCSO: Use of child for pornographic purpose — imprisonment not less than 5 years (first offence), not less than 7 years (repeat offence), plus fine.
Section 15 POCSO: Storage of CSAM — (a) for commercial purpose: 3 years + fine; (b) without commercial purpose: fine only; (c) failure to report or delete on becoming aware: fine.
Mere possession is an offence: Mere possession or storage of CSAM is an offence under Section 67B IT Act read with Section 15 POCSO. There is no "personal use" defence. Law enforcement agencies coordinate globally — NCMEC (National Center for Missing & Exploited Children) CyberTipline receives reports from platforms (WhatsApp, Google, Facebook) and shares them with Indian authorities through I4C.
Investigation standards: POCSO investigations must be conducted by a Special Juvenile Police Unit (SJPU) or designated child welfare police officer. Medical examination only as per POCSO prescribed procedure. Child-friendly investigation environment is mandatory.

Online Grooming & Enticement

Building trust for exploitation

Online grooming is the process by which an offender cultivates trust with a child — and often their family — with intent to facilitate sexual abuse, exploitation, or trafficking.

Applicable Legal Provisions:

Section 67B(c) IT Act: Cultivating, enticing, or inducing children into online relationships for sexual acts
Section 11 POCSO: Sexual harassment — includes making sexual gestures, showing pornography to a child, making a child expose their body parts, or cyber-stalking a child with sexual intent
Section 12 POCSO: Punishment for Section 11 offences — imprisonment up to 3 years + fine
Red flags for online grooming: Excessive attention and flattery online; requests for private photo/video sharing; moving conversation to encrypted platforms; questions about parental supervision and physical location; sexual comments or gradually normalising sexual topics; requests for in-person meetings; offers of gifts, money, or gaming credits; threats or emotional manipulation to maintain secrecy.
Digital evidence in grooming cases: Chat logs, DMs, email threads, call records, and device analysis (app history, contact lists). Section 65B/61 BSA certificate required for all digital evidence. Hash-verified copies of messages are admissible. Early preservation of evidence is critical — platforms may purge data on account deletion or after set periods.

Cyber Bullying & Online Harassment of Minors

Multi-statute framework for protecting children online

Cyber bullying includes: spreading rumours online, sharing embarrassing images without consent, creating fake profiles, excluding from online groups, persistent threats, and identity-based harassment.

Applicable Provisions Table:

School and regulatory standards: The National Commission for Protection of Child Rights (NCPCR) has issued guidelines on cyberbullying. CBSE and CISCE require schools to maintain anti-cyberbullying policies. If cyberbullying involves sexual content or sexual harassment, POCSO and IT Act Section 67A/67B may apply — escalating the seriousness significantly.

Sextortion of Minors

Coercion using intimate images or information

Sextortion is the use of intimate images or information as leverage to coerce the victim into compliance — typically demands for more images, money, or sexual acts. When the victim is a minor, all applicable statutes converge with enhanced penalties.

Applicable Statutes — Multi-Statute Coverage:

ProvisionRelevancePenalty
S.66E IT ActCapturing/publishing private images3 yrs / ₹2L
S.67A IT ActSexually explicit material5 yrs 1st offence
S.67B IT ActIf victim is under 185–7 yrs
S.308 BNS (Extortion)Threat to publish material unless demands met3–10 yrs
S.11–12 POCSOSexual harassment of child onlineUp to 3 yrs
S.78 BNSStalking with sexual intentUp to 3 yrs (1st), 5 yrs (repeat)
Simultaneous application: Sextortion of minors triggers BOTH IT Act and POCSO simultaneously. POCSO investigation procedures — child-friendly environment, no repeated examination, in-camera trial — must be followed even when the IT Act aspect is primary. Parents must NOT comply with demands — paying or sending more material almost always escalates abuse.
Steps for victims/guardians: (1) Do NOT comply with demands — payment or compliance escalates the situation. (2) Preserve all evidence BEFORE blocking (screenshots of all messages, profiles, demands). (3) Report immediately to cybercrime.gov.in or call 1930. (4) File police complaint under POCSO + IT Act simultaneously. (5) Contact CHILDLINE 1098 for support. (6) Seek psychological support — iCall (iitb.ac.in/icall) or school counsellor.

Statutory channels and procedural protections

Reporting Channels:

POCSO Trial Protections:

Section 33 POCSO: Special Courts designated for POCSO trials
Section 37 POCSO: In-camera trials — no public access, protects child's identity
Section 39 POCSO: Support person for child victim throughout trial
Section 26 POCSO: Child's statement recorded only once, before magistrate, in child-friendly environment; no repeated questioning by police
Section 24 POCSO: No police officer in uniform may investigate POCSO cases
Mandatory Reporting Obligation: Under Section 19 POCSO, ANY person who has knowledge or reasonable suspicion that a sexual offence has been committed against a child is legally required to report it to the Special Juvenile Police Unit or local police. Failure to report is itself an offence under Section 21 POCSO. This obligation extends to teachers, doctors, neighbours, platform employees — not just law enforcement or social workers.
