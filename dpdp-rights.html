01

Overview — Four Rights of Data Principals

Sections 11–14 of the DPDP Act

The DPDP Act grants four statutory rights to data principals — the individuals whose data is being processed. These rights are exercisable against the Data Fiduciary that holds or processes the individual's data, and must be honoured within timeframes specified in the rules. Exercising these rights is free of charge unless the request is excessive or repetitive.

Section 11
Right to Access Information
A data principal can request a summary of personal data held about them, details of entities with whom the data has been shared, and information about the purposes for which data is being processed.
Section 12
Right to Correction & Erasure
A data principal can request correction of inaccurate or misleading data, completion of incomplete data, updating of data, and erasure of data no longer necessary for the purpose for which it was collected.
Section 13
Right to Grievance Redressal
A data principal can file a grievance with the Data Fiduciary's designated Grievance Officer. If unsatisfied with the response, the data principal can escalate to the Data Protection Board.
Section 14
Right to Nominate
A data principal may nominate another individual to exercise their data rights in the event of death or incapacity. This is a distinctive provision not found in GDPR, designed for India's demographic context.
Timelines: The rules specify the timeframes within which Data Fiduciaries must respond to rights requests. Failure to respond within the prescribed period is itself a compliance violation and may be reported directly to the DPB.
02

Section 11 — Right to Access Information in Detail

What information the Data Fiduciary must provide on request

When a data principal makes a request under Section 11, the Data Fiduciary must provide:

A summary of personal data being processed and the processing activities.
The identities of all other Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors with whom the personal data has been shared, along with a description of the data shared.
Any other information relating to the personal data and its processing — as prescribed in the rules.
The grievance officer's name and contact details.
What Section 11 does NOT require: The Act does not require the Data Fiduciary to provide a full copy (data portability) of the personal data — only a summary and processing information. Data portability as a standalone right (similar to GDPR Article 20) is not included in the current Act, though the rules may introduce portability obligations for SDFs.
03

Section 12 — Right to Correction & Erasure

Inaccurate data must be corrected; unnecessary data must be erased

Section 12 grants the right to correction, completion, updating, and erasure of personal data. This right has two distinct limbs:

Correction & Updating
Data Accuracy Rights
The data principal can demand correction of inaccurate or misleading personal data, completion of incomplete data, and updating of out-of-date data. The Data Fiduciary must make reasonable efforts to ensure data accuracy.
Erasure
Right to Erasure — With Limits
The data principal can request erasure of personal data that is no longer necessary for the purpose it was collected. However, the Data Fiduciary may decline if retention is required by law (e.g. for tax, audit, or regulatory purposes).
Important constraint on erasure: The right to erasure under the DPDP Act is significantly narrower than GDPR's 'right to be forgotten'. It applies only where data is no longer necessary for the original processing purpose. There is no general right to demand erasure simply because a data principal withdraws consent — though withdrawal of consent will itself prevent further processing and trigger deletion obligations once the data is no longer needed for any residual purpose.
04

Sections 13 & 14 — Grievance Redressal & Nomination

Two-tier grievance mechanism and the right to nominate

Section 13 — Grievance Redressal: Every Data Fiduciary must establish a mechanism for data principals to register grievances. Designated Grievance Officers must be accessible, and responses must be provided within the timeframes specified in the rules. If the data principal is not satisfied with the Data Fiduciary's response, they may escalate to the Data Protection Board directly — without needing a lawyer or paying a fee.

Section 14 — Right to Nominate: A data principal may nominate any other individual to exercise their rights under Sections 11–13 in the event of the data principal's death or incapacity. This provision is distinctive to the DPDP Act and reflects India's need to address digital estates in a country where many individuals may not have formal digital wills or executors.

Low-barrier access to DPB: The Act deliberately makes the Data Protection Board accessible without legal representation or fees for individual data principals. This is a significant consumer-protection feature — data principals can file complaints directly against large Data Fiduciaries without needing to engage lawyers.
