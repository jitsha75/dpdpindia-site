Data Centre & CERT-IN Audit
Checklist for Sub-KUAs

About this checklist: The technical verification performed by CERT-IN empanelled auditors is based on the Compliance Checklist v2.0 issued in May 2025 and updated through March 2026 circulars. For a Sub-KUA, the audit is not just about the code — it covers the entire ecosystem, from the physical server rack to the way keys are stored in an HSM. Use the interactive checklist below to track your preparation. Click any item to mark it complete.
01

Physical & Environmental Security

Data centre inspection — auditors physically visit the hosting facility

Auditors will physically inspect the hosting facility — whether on-premise or a MeitY-empanelled cloud provider's data centre. The facility must demonstrate controlled access, surveillance, and environmental resilience specifically for the Aadhaar infrastructure.

Isolated Housing — dedicated lockable cabinet
Servers must be placed in a dedicated, lockable cabinet. Cabinets must be labelled as "Restricted Area" and must not share space with non-Aadhaar workloads unless logically separated. The auditor will verify the label and physical lock mechanism.
Access Control — biometric or card with entry/exit logs
Biometric or card-based access to the DC floor is mandatory. The auditor will check Entry/Exit Logs for the last 6 months — including date, time, and stated purpose of each visit. Tailgating prevention measures must be demonstrable.
CCTV Coverage — 24/7 with 90-day retention, no blind spots
Round-the-clock CCTV surveillance with at least 90 days of video retention. There must be no blind spots over the Aadhaar server racks. Auditor may request a live demonstration and playback of a specific past timestamp.
Environmental Controls — fire suppression, redundant cooling, UPS/DG
Functional Fire Suppression system (FM200 or equivalent clean-agent system). Redundant cooling units (N+1 minimum). UPS and DG backup with maintenance records and test logs. Auditor will inspect service records, not just the equipment.
02

Logical & Infrastructure Security

System hardening for all servers that communicate with the AUA/KUA

This section focuses on the "hardening" of systems that interact with the AUA/KUA stack. The auditor will review configuration baselines, patch levels, and access control settings — not just policies, but evidence that those policies are implemented.

OS Hardening — removal of unnecessary services, ports, default accounts
All non-essential services, ports, and default accounts must be disabled or removed. Auditors look for a Hardening Certificate signed by the CISO — a documented sign-off that the hardening baseline has been applied and validated. Reference against CIS Benchmarks is advisable.
Endpoint Security — restricted USB, latest OS patches
USB access must be restricted or disabled on all servers and admin terminals. OS must be fully patched — Windows Server 2022+ or Linux Kernel 5.x+ is the baseline. Patch management logs and scheduled patching evidence will be reviewed.
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) — mandatory for all admin access
MFA is mandatory for any administrative access to the Aadhaar application or database — including remote desktop, SSH, and database consoles. Single-factor authentication, even with strong passwords, will fail this check.
Account Lockout — automatic lock after 3 successive failed logins
System must automatically lock accounts after 3 successive failed login attempts. The lockout policy must be demonstrable, with evidence that it applies to all authentication interfaces — not just the primary login screen.
Network Segregation — Aadhaar environment in dedicated VLAN/DMZ
The Aadhaar environment must reside in a separate VLAN or DMZ with a dedicated firewall. Traffic between the Aadhaar zone and other corporate networks must be explicitly controlled via firewall rules — implicit permit-all rules will fail the audit.
03

Aadhaar Data Vault (ADV) Compliance

The most critical section of the 2026 audit — no Aadhaar numbers in clear text anywhere

2026 audit priority: UIDAI auditors treat ADV compliance as the primary gate. A failure on any ADV requirement will result in an audit failure regardless of scores elsewhere. Prepare evidence for each item well in advance of the audit date.
Tokenisation — every Aadhaar number replaced by a Reference Key
Verify that every Aadhaar number in the business database has been replaced by a Reference Key (token). Auditors may query the business database directly and check that no 12-digit UIDs appear. Spot-checking is common — any Aadhaar number in the main DB is an automatic finding.
Storage Separation — ADV logically or physically separate from main DB
The Aadhaar Data Vault must be a logically or physically separate database from the main application database. The ADV must have its own access controls — the application DB credentials must not grant access to the ADV, and vice versa.
Encryption Standard — AES-256 for all data at rest in the vault
All data in the vault must be encrypted using AES-256. Encryption keys must be managed by the HSM (not stored in config files or environment variables). Auditor will verify the encryption configuration and key storage location.
Vault Access — only authorised APIs, not human users, can query ADV
Only authorised APIs (not direct human database access) should be able to query the ADV to resolve tokens. There must be no mechanism for a DBA or admin to run a SELECT * on the ADV and retrieve Aadhaar numbers in bulk. API call logs must be maintained.
04

HSM (Hardware Security Module) Operations

FIPS 140-2 Level 3 certification and key management discipline

The HSM is the most critical piece of hardware in the Aadhaar infrastructure. It stores all private keys for signing and decryption, and must be configured so that keys can never be extracted. Sub-KUAs may use a logical partition on the KUA's HSM rather than a dedicated device.

FIPS 140-2 Level 3 — certification verified by serial number
The HSM must meet FIPS 140-2 Level 3 certification. The auditor will verify the HSM's serial number against the NIST certificate database. A Level 2 device, or an HSM with a lapsed certification, will fail this requirement. Have the FIPS certificate documentation printed and ready.
Logical Partitioning — dedicated partition for Sub-KUA if sharing KUA's HSM
If the Sub-KUA is using the KUA's HSM, verify that a dedicated logical partition exists for the Sub-KUA with its own set of keys. The Sub-KUA's keys must not be accessible from other partitions, including the KUA's own partition. Partition boundary configuration evidence is required.
Key Management — private keys never leave the HSM, Exportable = No
Private keys for digital signing and decryption must never leave the HSM. Auditors specifically check whether the "Exportable" or "Extractable" flag is set to No for all key objects. Any key with an exportable flag set to Yes — even if never actually exported — is an automatic finding.
05

Application & API Security

VAPT, SAST/DAST, API controls, and 2026 face authentication requirements

VAPT Report — fresh, zero High/Critical open vulnerabilities
A Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing (VAPT) report not older than 6 months is required, with zero High or Critical open vulnerabilities. Closed findings must have evidence of remediation — a finding marked "closed" without proof of the fix will be re-opened during audit.
SAST / DAST — static and dynamic testing on latest build
Proof of Static Application Security Testing (SAST) and Dynamic Application Security Testing (DAST) performed on the latest production build. Tool names, scan dates, and summary reports are required. SAST/DAST must cover the Aadhaar-specific code paths, not just the general application.
API Whitelisting — requests only from registered static IPs
The application must only allow requests from whitelisted IP addresses. Dynamic or range-based whitelists are not acceptable. The whitelist must include the KUA's static IPs and any registered Registered Device service IPs. Auditor will attempt a connection from an unlisted IP.
SSL Pinning — implemented in mobile apps to prevent MITM
For mobile applications, SSL/certificate pinning must be implemented to prevent Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) attacks. Pinning must be against the UIDAI's current certificate chain. Auditor may perform a proxy interception test to verify pinning is active.
Face Auth Liveness Detection — new requirement for 2026 (Circular 2/2026)
If using Face Authentication, the auditor will check for Liveness Detection compliance as per Circular 2 of 2026. Passive liveness detection is the minimum; active challenge-response liveness is preferred. Spoofing resistance test results from a CERT-IN approved tool are required.
06

Governance & Documentation

People, process, and paper — the organisational controls auditors examine

Technical controls alone are insufficient. Auditors spend significant time reviewing organisational governance — who is responsible, what agreements exist, how consent is captured, and whether the people handling Aadhaar data have been background-verified.

CISO Appointment — designated and documented Chief Information Security Officer
Documented proof of a designated Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) — name, appointment letter, and scope of responsibility. The CISO must have Aadhaar infrastructure in their explicit remit. The CISO's signature is required on several other compliance documents checked during the audit.
Joint Undertaking — signed legal agreement between KUA and Sub-KUA
Signed copy of the Joint Undertaking executed between the KUA and Sub-KUA — holding both parties jointly and severally liable for data breaches. The agreement must be current and countersigned by authorised signatories of both organisations. The auditor verifies the original signed copy, not a photocopy.
Consent Logs — random sample of 10–20 transactions verified
The auditor will randomly sample 10–20 transactions and request the corresponding Electronic Consent Log for each — including timestamp, purpose stated, language of consent, and the resident's acknowledgement. Consent logs must be immutable and stored separately from the main application logs.
Background Verification (BGV) — for all IT staff handling Aadhaar infrastructure
BGV reports for all IT staff who have any form of access to the Aadhaar infrastructure — including developers, database administrators, network engineers, and security personnel. BGV must include criminal record check, previous employment verification, and identity verification. Reports must not be older than 2 years.
07

Auditor's Final Output

What the CERT-IN auditor produces — and what you must submit to UIDAI

Upon completing the site visit and systems review, the CERT-IN empanelled auditor prepares two documents. Both must be submitted by the KUA to the UIDAI portal to activate or renew the Sub-KUA licence.

Two documents are produced by the auditor

These documents are produced by the CERT-IN empanelled auditor — not self-certified by the Sub-KUA. They must be uploaded by the KUA to the UIDAI portal to activate or renew the Sub-KUA licence. The audit cycle repeats periodically as specified by UIDAI.

📋
Certificate of Compliance
A signed, dated certificate from the auditor confirming the Sub-KUA meets all requirements of Compliance Checklist v2.0. This is the "pass" document.
📄
Detailed Audit Report
A full report covering every checklist item — findings, evidence reviewed, exceptions noted, and remediation dates for any conditional pass items. Uploaded to UIDAI portal by the KUA.
Practical timeline: Allow 4–8 weeks between initial auditor engagement and receiving the final Certificate of Compliance. This includes: pre-audit questionnaire (1–2 weeks), site visit scheduling (1–2 weeks), site visit and systems review (1–3 days), findings discussion (1 week), remediation of minor findings (1–2 weeks), and certificate issuance. Plan your go-live date around this timeline, not the other way round.
