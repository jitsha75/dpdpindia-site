Cybercrime Remedies in India 2026
A Complete Legal Guide for Victims

From the 1930 helpline to the High Court — every legal remedy available to cybercrime victims under the IT Act 2000, BNS 2023, BNSS 2023, and RBI/SEBI guidelines. Covers the I4C and CFMC institutional framework, the NCRP complaint process, IT Act adjudication for compensation, and a step-by-step action plan for financial fraud, identity theft, account takeover, and data breach victims.

India recorded over 1.77 million cybercrime complaints on the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal in 2024-25, with financial fraud accounting for the majority. The legal landscape for victims has been substantially strengthened through the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023 (BNS) and Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita 2023 (BNSS), which replaced the IPC and CrPC from 1 July 2024 and introduced several cybercrime-specific provisions. Simultaneously, the Ministry of Home Affairs' Indian Cybercrime Coordination Centre (I4C) and its Cyber Fraud Mitigation Centre (CFMC) have created an institutional ecosystem for rapid response. This article maps every available remedy — immediate, civil, criminal, regulatory, and banking — and provides a practical action plan for victims.

1. Immediate Action — The Golden Window

Cybercrime — particularly financial fraud — has a narrow golden window for fund recovery. Digital payment systems allow funds to move rapidly through multiple accounts. The first 24 hours after a financial cybercrime are the most critical. The following steps should be taken immediately upon discovering a cybercrime:

Call 1930 — National Cyber Crime Helpline Within hours
Dial 1930 (toll-free, 24×7). The helpline is operated by I4C and connected in real-time to the CFMC, which has representatives of all major banks, payment aggregators, and telecom operators. A prompt report allows CFMC to flag the destination accounts for a lien — freezing the funds before they can be withdrawn or moved further. This is the single most important action for financial fraud victims. Save your complaint reference number.
File on cybercrime.gov.in Within 24 hours
Log in to www.cybercrime.gov.in (National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal, NCRP) and file a formal complaint. The portal has specific categories: financial cybercrime, cybercrime against women/children, online harassment, identity theft, data breach, etc. Upload all available evidence (screenshots, bank statements, call recordings, transaction IDs). Your 1930 complaint reference can be linked to your NCRP complaint.
Contact your bank immediately Within 3 days for zero-liability
For unauthorised banking transactions, contact your bank's fraud reporting channel (Call, Net Banking, or Branch) and lodge a written complaint. Under RBI Master Direction on Fraud 2024 and the RBI Circular on customer liability (2017, updated), customers who report unauthorised transactions within 3 working days are entitled to zero liability if the breach was not due to the customer's own negligence. Delay reduces or extinguishes this protection. Get a complaint reference number in writing.
Secure your accounts Immediately
Change passwords on compromised accounts and all accounts that share the same password. Enable two-factor authentication. Revoke suspicious app permissions. If an Aadhaar-linked transaction occurred, lock your Aadhaar biometric via the UIDAI app or uidai.gov.in (free, instant). Report SIM swaps to your telecom provider and request a SIM block if necessary.
Preserve all evidence Before anything is deleted
Take screenshots of every communication, transaction, and interface involved. Note the time, date, IP addresses visible, and any URLs. Export chat histories (WhatsApp, SMS). Download and save bank statements. Preserve the original device used — do not factory reset. Evidence preservation is critical for IT Act adjudication and criminal prosecution.
File a formal FIR with Police Within 72 hours
Visit the nearest police station and file an FIR under the relevant provisions of BNS 2023 and IT Act 2000 (see Section 3). Under BNSS 2023, you may also file a Zero FIR at any police station regardless of jurisdiction. The e-Zero FIR initiative (I4C, launched May 2025) automatically registers a Zero FIR for NCRP complaints involving financial losses above ₹10 lakh with the e-Crime Police Station, Delhi. For losses below ₹10 lakh, follow up with your local cybercrime cell.

2. Institutional Framework — I4C, CFMC, and NCRP

India's cybercrime response ecosystem has been substantially strengthened since 2024 through the formalisation of three interconnected institutions under the Ministry of Home Affairs.

🏛️
Indian Cybercrime Coordination Centre (I4C)
Established as an Attached Office of MHA from 1 July 2024, I4C is the apex coordination body for cybercrime response in India. It operates the NCRP, the 1930 helpline, the Samanvay platform (data repository and analytics for LEAs), and the Citizens Financial Cyber Frauds Reporting and Management System (CFCFRMS). I4C coordinates between state/UT police, banks, telecom providers, and digital platforms.
mha.gov.in → Cyber & Information Security Division
⚡
Cyber Fraud Mitigation Centre (CFMC)
Launched 10 September 2024 by Home Minister Amit Shah, the CFMC is co-located at I4C and has real-time representatives from major banks (SBI, HDFC, ICICI, PNB, etc.), payment aggregators (Razorpay, PayU, PhonePe, etc.), telecom operators (Jio, Airtel, BSNL), and state/UT LEAs. When a victim calls 1930 within the golden window, CFMC can freeze destination accounts in near-real-time. It is designed as a cooperative federalism model for immediate financial cybercrime response.
Available through 1930 helpline — no direct public portal
🌐
National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (NCRP)
Operational since 2019, upgraded with e-Zero FIR in May 2025. The portal accepts complaints across all cybercrime categories. Complaints are automatically routed to the relevant state/UT cybercrime cell based on the complainant's location. Complaints above ₹10 lakh trigger automatic e-Zero FIR with Delhi's e-Crime Police Station. The portal also has a special section for women and children (cybercrimes like online harassment, sextortion, and CSAM).
cybercrime.gov.in
🔬
State Cybercrime Cells
Every state/UT operates a dedicated Cybercrime Cell (or Cybercrime Police Station) equipped with cyber forensic tools and trained investigators. State cells receive forwarded NCRP complaints and are the primary investigating authorities for most cybercrime FIRs. Major states also have Cyber Forensic Laboratories — accredited by MHA/I4C — capable of device forensics, network analysis, and digital evidence preservation. For large-scale crimes, CERT-In and CBI's cyber division may be engaged.
Accessible via local police or NCRP complaint routing

3. Criminal Remedies — BNS 2023 and IT Act 2000

Criminal prosecution is the primary enforcement mechanism for most cybercrimes. From 1 July 2024, the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023 (BNS) replaced the Indian Penal Code 1860 as India's substantive criminal law, and the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita 2023 (BNSS) replaced the Code of Criminal Procedure. The IT Act 2000 continues to operate as a lex specialis for technology offences. Charges may be laid under both frameworks simultaneously.

BNSS 2023 — New Tools for Cyber Investigation

The BNSS 2023 introduced procedural changes specifically beneficial for cybercrime investigation: (a) Zero FIR provisions (s. 173) allow any police station to accept an FIR regardless of territorial jurisdiction — critical for cybercrimes where the offender is in a different state; (b) Electronic evidence provisions (ss. 94-95) permit police to require digital intermediaries to produce electronic records; (c) Trial-in-absentia provisions for absconders, enabling prosecution of overseas cybercriminals; (d) Time-bound investigation requirements — chargesheet must be filed within 60 days (extendable to 90 days for serious offences); (e) Mandatory audio-visual recording of statements for cybercrime victims, reducing secondary victimisation.

4. Civil Remedies — IT Act Adjudication and Compensation

Alongside criminal prosecution, victims may simultaneously pursue civil compensation through the IT Act's adjudication mechanism. This is a specialist quasi-judicial route distinct from civil courts, designed for technology-related disputes.

4.1 Adjudicating Officers — Section 46, IT Act 2000

Each state/UT government appoints an Adjudicating Officer (AO) under Section 46 of the IT Act 2000 (typically a senior IAS officer of at least Joint Secretary rank). The AO has jurisdiction to adjudicate on complaints related to Sections 43 and 43A — covering unauthorised access, data theft, and body corporate liability for data protection failures. The AO can award compensation up to ₹5 crore for Section 43 violations. For Section 43A, there is no statutory ceiling on compensation — the amount is determined by the damage proved.

The adjudication process is faster than civil court litigation: there is no requirement to follow CPC procedure, and the AO may summon witnesses, inspect documents, and take technical expert assistance. Proceedings are designed to be concluded expeditiously. However, in practice, AO proceedings have been slow in many states, and the Cyber Appellate Tribunal (appeal body) has faced staffing challenges.

4.2 Key Compensation Scenarios

IT Act s. 43 — Civil
Unauthorised Access & Data Theft
Hacker accessed your computer, downloaded your data, introduced malware, or disrupted your system. File complaint with AO. Compensation up to ₹5 crore covering actual loss and damage.
IT Act s. 43A — Civil
Corporate Data Breach
Your bank, e-commerce platform, or telecom provider suffered a data breach due to negligent security. File against the body corporate for failure to implement "reasonable security practices." No ceiling on compensation.
RBI Guidelines — Banking
Unauthorised Banking Transactions
Zero liability for reporting within 3 days (if bank's fault). Limited liability of ₹5,000–₹25,000 for 4–7 day reporting window. Beyond 7 days, bank determines liability. File complaint with bank's internal ombudsman first.
RBI Ombudsman — Regulatory
Banking Fraud Escalation
If bank refuses compensation or delays: escalate to RBI Integrated Ombudsman (cms.rbi.org.in) — free, online, jurisdiction over all regulated banks and NBFCs. RBI can order banks to compensate and fix systemic issues.

5. Banking and Financial Fraud Remedies

5.1 RBI Zero Liability Framework

Under the RBI Circular DBR.No.Leg.BC.78/09.07.005/2017-18 (Customer Protection — Limiting Liability), the liability of customers for unauthorised electronic transactions depends on timing of reporting and the source of the breach:

RBI Zero Liability — Summary Table

Zero Liability (customer not at fault): Report within 3 working days → Full refund within 10 working days of reporting. Limited Liability: Report within 4–7 working days → Maximum customer liability: ₹5,000 (basic/BSBD savings), ₹10,000 (other savings/prepaid), ₹25,000 (credit cards above ₹5L limit). No refund: Customer contributed to fraud through sharing OTP/credentials → Bank not liable. Key rule: The burden of proving the customer was negligent lies with the bank, not the customer. If the breach was due to the bank's system failure, the bank bears 100% liability regardless of reporting timing.

5.2 RBI Integrated Ombudsman Scheme

If your bank refuses to refund or delays resolution beyond 30 days, you may file a complaint with the RBI Integrated Ombudsman at cms.rbi.org.in or by calling 14448. The scheme covers all RBI-regulated entities (banks, NBFCs, payment system operators). Filing is free. The Ombudsman can award compensation up to ₹20 lakh for deficiency of service, plus compensation for consequential losses up to ₹1 lakh.

5.3 SEBI Investor Complaints — Stock Market Fraud

For investment fraud, stock market manipulation, and unauthorised trading, file complaints with SEBI's SCORES platform (scores.sebi.gov.in). SEBI's Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience Framework applies to brokers and exchanges — a data breach by a SEBI-regulated entity is both a SEBI compliance failure and an IT Act s. 43A violation. SEBI can direct entities to compensate investors and take regulatory action.

6. Specific Crime Types — Targeted Remedies

Financial Fraud
UPI/OTP/Phishing Fraud
Immediate: Call 1930; contact bank within 3 days. Legal: FIR under IT Act s. 66C, 66D + BNS s. 318. Civil: AO compensation if intermediary liable. Recovery probability highest within 24 hours of reporting.
Identity Theft
Account Takeover / Fake KYC
Immediate: Lock Aadhaar biometric (uidai.gov.in); change passwords; report to bank. Legal: FIR under IT Act s. 66C + BNS s. 319. Civil: AO compensation; DPBI complaint if DPDP-regulated entity involved (from May 2027).
Online Harassment
Stalking / Threats / Doxxing
Immediate: Screenshot and preserve evidence; block attacker; report to platform. Legal: FIR under BNS s. 78 (stalking), IT Act s. 67/66E. Civil: Injunction from Civil Court. Women can use NCW portal or 1091 helpline.
Sextortion / Non-Consensual Images
Intimate Image Abuse
Immediate: Do not pay ransom (escalates demands); report to NCRP (special women/children section). Legal: IT Act s. 66E + BNS s. 308 (extortion). Platform: Use StopNCII.org hash-matching tool to prevent spread. NCW, iCall helplines available.
Data Breach Victim
Personal Data Leaked / Sold
Immediate: Change passwords; enable 2FA; monitor credit reports (CIBIL, Experian). Legal: IT Act s. 43A AO complaint against company. From May 2027: DPBI complaint under DPDP Act. Criminal FIR if data sold intentionally.
Investment / Crypto Fraud
Fake Schemes / Pig Butchering
Immediate: Call 1930; report to NCRP. Legal: FIR under BNS s. 318 + IT Act. Regulatory: SEBI SCORES if involves securities; RBI if forex involved; ED if money laundering suspected. Involve FIU-IND for cross-border crypto fraud.

7. Escalation Ladder — From NCRP to Supreme Court

Victims who do not get resolution at the first tier should systematically escalate. The Indian cybercrime adjudication framework operates on a multi-tier escalation model:

Tier 1: 1930 Helpline + NCRP Portal
First point of contact. Complaints routed to state cybercrime cells. Financial complaints activate CFMC fund-freeze mechanism. Track complaint status on NCRP portal using your complaint ID. If no action within 15 days, escalate.
Tier 2: District Cybercrime Cell / Police Station FIR
If NCRP complaint is not acted upon, visit the district cybercrime cell or local police station to register an FIR personally. Under Section 173 BNSS, you can also file a Zero FIR at any police station. If police refuse to register FIR, you may apply to the Magistrate under Section 175 BNSS for direction to register FIR.
Tier 3: SP/SSP / State Cybercrime Headquarters
Escalate to the Superintendent of Police (SP/SSP) if local police are unresponsive. State Cybercrime Headquarters (e.g., CID Tech Cell, Maharashtra Cyber, Tamil Nadu Cyber Crime Cell) handle complex multi-jurisdictional cases and can override inaction at the district level.
Tier 4: IT Act Adjudicating Officer — Civil Compensation
File a separate civil complaint with the state's Adjudicating Officer (AO) under IT Act s. 46 for compensation under ss. 43/43A. The AO process runs parallel to criminal prosecution — both routes are simultaneously available. No court fee; present evidence and testimony before AO.
Tier 5: Cyber Appellate Tribunal (Appeal from AO)
If dissatisfied with the AO's order, appeal lies to the Cyber Appellate Tribunal (CAT) under IT Act s. 57. CAT was constituted under the IT Act with a Chairperson of High Court Judge rank. Further appeal lies to the High Court.
Tier 6: High Court / Supreme Court
High Courts exercise supervisory jurisdiction (Art. 226, 227) over cybercrime police action and adjudication. Writ petitions may be filed for mandamus (directing police/AO to act), certiorari (quashing unlawful orders), or habeas corpus (if a person is unlawfully detained in connection with cybercrime). Second appeal from CAT lies to the High Court on questions of law. Supreme Court jurisdiction under Art. 136 (SLP) is available against High Court orders.

8. Cross-Border Cybercrime — International Mechanisms

A significant portion of financial cybercrime targeting Indian victims originates from overseas — particularly from Southeast Asian cybercrime syndicates (Myanmar, Cambodia, Laos) and organised international groups. Recovery in cross-border cases is harder but not impossible.

Cross-Border Cybercrime Response Mechanisms

CBI Cybercrime Wing: For international cybercrime with major financial impact, CBI's Special Crime Branch (cyber) can be engaged. CBI has Interpol liaison and can issue Red Notices for fugitive cybercriminals. ED / PMLA: If proceeds of cybercrime are being laundered, the Enforcement Directorate can take action under PMLA 2002 — including attachment of assets and extradition requests. FIU-IND: Financial Intelligence Unit India can flag and freeze cross-border cryptocurrency transactions linked to fraud. CERT-In: For state-sponsored cyber attacks or attacks on critical infrastructure, CERT-In can engage foreign CERTs through bilateral cybersecurity MOUs. India has MoUs with the USA, UK, Australia, Israel, and several other nations. Budapest Convention Watch: India has not signed the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime — limiting some formal mutual legal assistance channels, though bilateral MLATs (Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties) partially compensate.

9. Upcoming DPDP Act Remedies — From May 2027

When the substantive provisions of the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 enter full force in May 2027, cybercrime victims who are also victims of data breaches will gain an additional remedy pathway through the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI).

Under Section 28 of the DPDP Act, a Data Principal (individual) may file a complaint with the DPBI against a Data Fiduciary (company, government entity, or platform) that failed to implement adequate security safeguards or failed to notify the individual of a data breach affecting their personal data. The DPBI can impose penalties up to ₹250 crore on the entity — but critically, these are regulatory penalties payable to the government, not compensation payable to the victim.

Important: DPDP Act Does Not Provide Victim Compensation

A common misconception: the DPDP Act's penalty framework does not direct payment to victims — penalties go to the Consolidated Fund of India. For personal compensation, victims must continue to use the IT Act s. 43/43A adjudication route before the Adjudicating Officer. The DPDP Act's DPBI complaint mechanism is primarily a regulatory enforcement tool — useful for systemic accountability but not for individual financial recovery. The two pathways (DPBI complaint + AO compensation) are complementary and should be used simultaneously where appropriate.
