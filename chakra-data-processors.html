Data Processors — Processing, Security & Accountability

Data Processors — cloud providers, IT vendors, outsourcing partners, SaaS platforms — sit at the heart of India's digital economy. The DPDP Act 2023 establishes a clear contractual and accountability framework governing their relationship with Data Fiduciaries and their obligations toward the data they process.

01

Definition & Distinction from Data Fiduciary

Section 2(k) — the critical dividing line

Under s. 2(k) of the DPDP Act 2023, a Data Processor means "any person who processes personal data on behalf of a Data Fiduciary." The definition is concise — but its implications are significant. The key criterion is processing on behalf of another: the Processor has no independent discretion over the purpose or means of processing.

The hybrid problem: Many entities act as both Fiduciary and Processor simultaneously — for example, a cloud provider that processes customer data on behalf of a client (Processor role) but also processes its employees' data independently (Fiduciary role). Careful legal analysis of each processing activity is required to determine the applicable role and obligations.
02

Contractual Framework

Section 8(2) — the Data Processing Agreement

Section 8(2) of the DPDP Act requires that a Data Fiduciary engaging a Data Processor must do so only pursuant to a valid contract. The contract must require the Processor to:

  • Process personal data only as instructed by the Fiduciary — no independent use for the Processor's own purposes.
  • Implement appropriate technical and organisational security measures as required under the Act.
  • Assist the Fiduciary in fulfilling Data Principal rights requests — particularly access, correction, and erasure.
  • Notify the Fiduciary of any personal data breach promptly — to enable the Fiduciary to meet its s. 8(6) notification obligations.
  • Delete or return all personal data at the end of the processing relationship, as instructed by the Fiduciary.
  • Make available to the Fiduciary all information necessary to demonstrate compliance with the Act.
Data Processing Agreements (DPAs): While the DPDP Act does not prescribe a standard form DPA (unlike GDPR Article 28 which provides a detailed template), the DPDP Rules 2025 are expected to specify minimum contractual requirements. In the interim, organisations should model DPAs on the Act's s. 8(2) requirements and international best practice (GDPR SCCs adapted for India).

Critically, a contractual arrangement between Fiduciary and Processor does not transfer the Fiduciary's regulatory obligations to the Processor. The Fiduciary remains accountable to the DPBI and to Data Principals — even where a breach is caused by the Processor's negligence.

03

Security Obligations

Technical and organisational measures for data processors

While the DPDP Act's security requirements (s. 8(5)) are expressly imposed on Data Fiduciaries, Processors inherit these obligations contractually and must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to protect personal data they process.

The DPDP Rules 2025 and industry guidance suggest the following security measures for Processors:

  • Encryption: Personal data must be encrypted at rest and in transit using industry-standard protocols (AES-256, TLS 1.2+).
  • Access controls: Role-based access control (RBAC), principle of least privilege, multi-factor authentication for systems handling personal data.
  • Logging and monitoring: Comprehensive audit logs of all access to personal data; anomaly detection systems; regular log review.
  • Incident response: A documented and tested incident response plan, including the breach notification process to the Fiduciary.
  • Penetration testing: Regular vulnerability assessments and penetration tests; patching protocols.
  • Data isolation: Personal data of different Fiduciaries must be isolated — a breach of one client's environment must not compromise another's.
  • ISO 27001 / SOC 2: Certification against recognised information security standards is the practical minimum for cloud and IT service providers.
Security standards under DPDP Rules 2025: The Rules prescribe minimum security standards for Data Fiduciaries and their Processors — including requirements for periodic security audits, mandatory security certifications for SDFs and their critical Processors, and incident response timeframes. MeitY is expected to issue detailed technical guidance circulars in 2026.
04

Sub-Processors

Chain-of-processing accountability

A Data Processor frequently engages further entities — sub-processors — to perform parts of the processing activity. For example, a payroll SaaS company (Processor) may use a cloud infrastructure provider (sub-processor) to host its platform, and an SMS gateway (another sub-processor) to send payslips.

The DPDP Act does not expressly address sub-processors, but the contractual logic of s. 8(2) requires Processors to ensure that sub-processors are subject to equivalent obligations — particularly security standards and breach notification requirements. This must be reflected in the Data Processing Agreement between Fiduciary and Processor.

Best practice for sub-processor management:

  • Maintain a current register of all sub-processors with access to personal data.
  • Obtain written approval from the Fiduciary before engaging new sub-processors (or notify and allow objection).
  • Impose equivalent data protection obligations on sub-processors by contract.
  • Conduct due diligence on sub-processors' security posture before engagement.
  • Include sub-processor breach notification obligations with timelines that allow the Processor to meet its obligations to the Fiduciary.
The cloud provider chain: Many Indian enterprises use hyperscale cloud providers (AWS India, Azure, GCP) which themselves use sub-processors for specific services (CDN providers, support tools, etc.). Enterprises must map their entire data processing chain to identify all sub-processors and assess whether appropriate contractual protections are in place.
05

Liability & Accountability

Who bears the risk when a Processor causes a breach?

The DPDP Act's liability framework places primary accountability on the Data Fiduciary — not the Processor. From the DPBI's perspective, the Fiduciary is responsible for ensuring compliance of its Processors and is the entity against which penalties are imposed.

However, liability between Fiduciary and Processor is a matter of contract. A well-drafted DPA should address:

  • Indemnification: The Processor indemnifies the Fiduciary for penalties, damages, and costs arising from the Processor's breach of the DPA or the Act.
  • Liability cap: Most Processors will seek to cap liability at fees paid in a given period — Fiduciaries should negotiate higher caps for sensitive data or critical processing.
  • Cyber insurance: Both parties should maintain cyber liability insurance — Fiduciaries should require evidence of adequate coverage from Processors.
  • Audit rights: The Fiduciary's right to audit the Processor's compliance — through questionnaires, certifications, or on-site audits.
DPBI enforcement risk: The DPBI will pursue the Data Fiduciary — not the Processor — for regulatory penalties. The Fiduciary's only recourse for Processor-caused breaches is through the contractual indemnification. This makes DPA negotiation a critical risk management exercise, not a compliance formality.
06

Practical Compliance Checklist for Processors

What Indian IT/ITES companies and cloud providers need to do now

  • ✓Audit all client contracts to identify where you act as a Data Processor — update DPA terms to reflect DPDP Act s. 8(2) requirements.
  • ✓Map all personal data flows within your systems — identify what personal data you process, on whose behalf, and for what purpose.
  • ✓Create and maintain a sub-processor register; ensure sub-processors are under equivalent contractual obligations.
  • ✓Implement or upgrade breach detection and notification procedures — ensure you can notify Fiduciaries within the contractual notification window (typically 24–48 hours of detection).
  • ✓Obtain or renew ISO 27001 / SOC 2 Type II certification — this is the practical baseline for demonstrating security to Fiduciaries and the DPBI.
  • ✓Train staff handling personal data on DPDP Act obligations, data minimisation, and incident response procedures.
  • ✓Implement data isolation controls to prevent cross-contamination of different Fiduciaries' personal data in shared environments.
  • ✓Establish a documented process for responding to Data Principal rights requests forwarded by Fiduciaries — with timelines that allow Fiduciaries to meet their response obligations.
  • ✓Review retention and deletion procedures — implement automated deletion workflows that activate when Fiduciaries invoke the storage limitation obligation.
  • ✓Cross-border transfer: where you process data in locations outside India, ensure appropriate contractual safeguards with the Fiduciary (note: the Fiduciary bears the transfer compliance obligation, but you must enable it contractually).
Competitive advantage: Indian IT/ITES companies that can demonstrate DPDP Act compliance — through robust DPAs, security certifications, and documented procedures — will have a significant competitive advantage when tendering for contracts with DPDP-compliant Fiduciaries and for global clients who require their vendors to meet data protection standards.
