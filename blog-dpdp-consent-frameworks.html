Consent Frameworks Under the DPDP Act:
What Compliance Really Looks Like

The DPDP Act redefines what "consent" means in Indian data protection law. For product and legal teams, this means rebuilding consent architecture from notice through withdrawal. Here's what actually has to happen.

Published 27 March 2026
Category DPDP Act · Compliance
Read time 8 min
DPDP Act 2023 Consent Data Principal Compliance Legal Notice Product Design

Under the DPDP Act 2023, consent is not a checkbox. It is a legal architecture — a framework that spans notice design, specification of purpose, mechanism of withdrawal, and record-keeping. When it fails, penalties go up to ₹250 crore. For product teams, this is the difference between compliance and liability.

The Five Core Requirements of DPDP Consent

Section 6 of the DPDP Act establishes the foundation. Consent under the Act must be:

  • 1. Itemised — the data fiduciary must specify, clearly and distinctly, what personal data will be processed and for what purpose.
  • 2. Specific — consent given for one purpose cannot be reused for another; purpose creep is not permitted.
  • 3. Clear — the language must be unambiguous, free of technical jargon that obscures meaning, and expressed so that the data principal understands what they are consenting to.
  • 4. Informed — the data principal must have all material information about processing, risks, and their rights before consent is sought.
  • 5. Freely Given — consent cannot be a condition of service where withdrawal would deny the principal a core benefit to which they are entitled.

Read that list again. Most consent interfaces today fail at least three of these requirements.

📌 Notice Language

Section 6(1) specifies that consent notice must be in English or any language specified in the Eighth Schedule of the Indian Constitution. This is not optional. Notice in a vendor's preferred language, or notice translated by automated tools without expert review, does not satisfy statutory requirement.

What "Itemised" and "Specific" Actually Mean in Practice

Itemisation is where compliance breaks down in real applications. A consent interface that says "we will process your data to provide services" fails this test entirely.

The DPDP Act requires that you specify:

  • ? Which categories of personal data? Not "user data" — but names, location data, transaction history, behavioural signals, each itemised separately.
  • ? What exact purposes? Not "service improvement" — but account authentication, fraud prevention, marketing, analytics, each with its own consent toggle.
  • ? How long will it be retained? "As long as necessary" is too vague. Specify a timeframe or clear deletion triggers.
  • ? Who will have access? If data flows to third parties, processors, or government agencies, that must be disclosed upfront.
  • ? What are the risks? Where profiling or automated decision-making occurs, the principal must understand the consequences.

This is not theoretical. Under Section 6, each purpose requires separate consent. You cannot bundle consent for marketing, analytics, and service improvement into a single toggle. The legal position is unambiguous: one purpose per consent interaction.

⚠ Design Failure

A common pattern: "I agree to our Terms" + a single toggle. This violates DPDP consent on four counts — it is not itemised, not specific, not clear, and attempts to bundle unrelated purposes. The fiduciary exposure is direct.

The Withdrawal Right: As Easy to Withdraw as to Give

Section 6(4) of the DPDP Act is deceptively simple: "consent must be as easy to withdraw as to give."

What this means:

  • ✓ If consent is given via a one-click button on the signup form, withdrawal cannot require navigating six menu layers or contacting support.
  • ✓ If the data principal requests withdrawal via email, the data fiduciary must process it within the prescribed timelines (typically 5-10 working days per DPDP Rules draft).
  • ✓ Withdrawal of consent for one purpose does not require the principal to withdraw consent for all purposes — they must remain granular.
  • ✓ Upon withdrawal, data collection must stop immediately. Existing data must be deleted or anonymised per retention policies (Section 5).

This has immediate product implications. Account settings must expose a withdrawal interface at parity with the consent interface. If your consent flow is three steps, withdrawal cannot be buried in a support ticket system.

"Consent that is hard to withdraw is not legally given consent — it is coerced permission, and coercion is not lawful under the DPDP Act."

When Consent Is Not Required: The Seven Lawful Bases

Section 7 of the DPDP Act establishes seven categories where processing is lawful even without explicit consent. Understanding these is critical because they change the compliance calculus entirely.

The Act deems consent unnecessary when:

  • 1. Employment — Processing employee personal data for employment purposes, statutory compliance, payroll, and workplace administration.
  • 2. Medical emergency — Immediate processing to prevent harm or loss of life, without time to obtain consent.
  • 3. State functions — Government processing personal data in exercise of statutory or constitutional powers (subject to exemptions under Section 35-37).
  • 4. Legal proceedings — Processing required to establish, defend, or enforce legal claims.
  • 5. Legitimate uses by government — Other lawful state functions as may be specified in rules.
  • 6. Consent previously obtained — Processing within the scope of prior consent already recorded (but not beyond).
  • 7. Other lawful bases — As may be notified by the Data Protection Board via rule-making.

The critical trap: Just because consent is not required does not mean processing is unmonitored. Under Section 8, even without consent-based processing, the data fiduciary must still comply with purpose limitation, storage limitation, and all other fiduciary obligations.

📌 Legal Classification

If your processing falls under Section 7, you must explicitly classify it as such and document the legal basis. Simply saying "we don't need consent" without identifying which lawful basis applies is not sufficient. The fiduciary bears the burden of proof that the lawful basis actually applies.

Purpose Limitation and Storage Limitation: The Boundaries

Section 5 of the DPDP Act establishes two non-negotiable principles that define the entire consent framework:

Purpose Limitation

Personal data collected for a specified purpose cannot be processed for any unrelated purpose, even if that data is available in your system. If you collected location data for delivery routing, you cannot use that same data to build a profile for targeted marketing without fresh, specific consent.

Storage Limitation

Data must not be retained longer than necessary to serve the purpose for which it was collected. This is not discretionary. The data fiduciary must establish and enforce clear deletion schedules. "We delete after a reasonable time" does not meet statutory requirement — you must define what "reasonable" means for each category of data.

These two principles directly constrain what consent can achieve. Consent is not a license to collect data once and use it forever. It is permission to process for a specific purpose, for a defined period, under defined conditions.

⚠ Common Violation

Consent for "improving services" followed by data use for building customer profiles, training recommendation engines, or selling aggregated insights to third parties. This violates purpose limitation even if the principal gave "general" consent. Purpose must be specific, and deviation requires new consent.

Contrast with GDPR Article 7: India's Stricter Standard

The European GDPR (Article 7) places the burden of proving valid consent on the controller. The DPDP Act goes further: it prescribes detailed statutory requirements that consent must satisfy independent of what the fiduciary documents.

Under GDPR, consent must be "freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous." Under DPDP, it must additionally be "itemised" and "as easy to withdraw as to give." India's standard is stricter on withdrawal mechanism and on itemisation specificity.

The implication: If your consent interface barely satisfies GDPR, it almost certainly fails DPDP. The Act does not import GDPR-style flexibility on consent mechanism. Indian law prescribes a more prescriptive standard.

Building Compliant Consent Architecture: What Teams Must Build

For product and engineering teams, DPDP consent compliance translates into specific technical and UX requirements:

  • ✓ Granular consent toggles. One toggle per purpose. Marketing, analytics, profiling, third-party sharing, each requires separate consent interaction.
  • ✓ Plain language notices. Not T&Cs. Clear, structured disclosure of data categories, purposes, retention periods, third parties, and risks. Jargon-free.
  • ✓ Consent records. Backend logging of when consent was given, for what purposes, under what version of the notice. This log is your defense if challenged.
  • ✓ Withdrawal flows. Account settings must allow withdrawal with one or two clicks, matching the friction of giving consent.
  • ✓ Deletion pipelines. When consent is withdrawn, systems must delete or anonymise the relevant data within the prescribed timelines. This requires data mapping and deletion orchestration.
  • ✓ Version control. When notice terms change, the system must track which version the principal consented to and allow re-consent if necessary.
📌 Engineer's Responsibility

Engineering teams must implement consent as a core data governance function, not as a compliance sidebar. This means consent state flows through your data pipeline — when processing occurs, the system checks whether valid consent exists for that specific purpose. Without this, compliance is assertion without mechanism.

Penalties for Breach: The Scale of Exposure

Section 33 and the DPDP Act Schedule set out penalty tiers:

  • ₹ Up to ₹250 crore or 6% of global turnover (whichever is higher) for failure to obtain valid consent, or misuse of consent once given.
  • ₹ Up to ₹100 crore for breach of purpose limitation or storage limitation obligations.
  • ₹ Proportionate reductions for first-time breaches and corrective steps, but the ceiling remains the same.

For a mid-sized tech company with ₹500 crore annual revenue, a 6% cap is ₹30 crore. For companies larger than ₹4,000 crore, the ceiling is ₹250 crore outright. These are not theoretical risks.

Final Word

Consent under the DPDP Act is not a legal box to check during signup. It is an ongoing compliance obligation that spans notice design, withdrawal mechanism, consent records, and data deletion. When it is done correctly, it protects both the data principal and the fiduciary. When it fails, the exposure is substantial.

"Build consent as a core feature. The alternative is betting against ₹250 crore in penalties."
